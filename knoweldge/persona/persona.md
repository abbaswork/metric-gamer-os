# Metric Gamer — User Personas

## Research Methodology

These personas are built from a combination of primary sources (community sentiment from the Reddit threads referenced below) and secondary research. Sources were selected based on recency, sample size, and relevance to the gamer-as-consumer behaviour Metric Gamer needs to understand.

### Sources Used

| Source | Why Trusted | Key Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Big Games Machine — Game Discovery Survey 2024** (n=2,000+ UK gamers) | Large sample, recent, consumer-focused | How gamers discover games; platform trust rankings |
| **Quantic Foundry — Gen AI in Gaming Survey 2025** (global sample) | Specialist gaming research lab, rigorous methodology | Gamer distrust of AI/inauthentic content; authenticity as a value |
| **Niche Gamer — Professional vs. Community Reviews** | Direct relevance to Metric Gamer's positioning | Peer review trust vs. critic trust data |
| **MixBright / Activision Blizzard — Gamer Personas Research** | Cross-platform behavioural segmentation | Persona archetypes, motivation frameworks, engagement patterns |
| **r/EpicSeven — Positive tool reception (epic7rta.statscom)** | Real community reaction to a community-built game tool | What gamers respond to positively in player-built tools |
| **r/EpicSeven — Negative tool reception (Epic Helper AI/ML)** | Real community reaction to an AI-assisted game tool | What triggers distrust; the AI-vs-community divide |
| **r/JRPG — Ranking 25+ JRPGs by metrics post** | Real community engagement with metric-based game ranking | How gamers engage with ranked lists; what they debate |
| **r/technology — Gamers vs. AI in-game content thread** | Broad gamer sentiment on authenticity vs. AI | Emotional drivers behind authenticity demand |

## Persona 1 — "The Targeted Seeker"

> *"I just want to know the best games for exactly what I'm looking for — I don't have time to trawl through 40 reviews."*

### Who They Are

- **Age:** 22–38
- **Gender:** Skews male (~65%), but non-binary and female gamers are a meaningful segment (and notably more sensitive to authenticity signals)
- **Platform:** PC / console cross-player; heavy Steam user
- **Discovery behaviour:** Google-first. Searches specific questions like *"best co-op RPGs on PS2"*, *"split screen games worth playing 2024"*, *"is [game] good for someone who liked [other game]"*
- **Review habits:** Checks Steam user reviews (60% of this segment do — MixBright data). Skims Reddit threads. Distrusts Metacritic professional scores (only 3% use Metacritic as a primary discovery source — BGM 2024)
- **Typical session:** Lands on a Metric Gamer ranked list page from Google, scans the ranking, reads 1–2 game entries, possibly clicks into a game page

### Motivations

- **Time efficiency** — wants a confident answer fast, not a 3,000-word essay
- **Context specificity** — a generic "best RPGs" list is useless; they want the list that matches their exact context (platform, co-op, era, etc.)
- **Peer validation** — they trust the answer more if they know it comes from players like them, not a press outlet that got a review copy

### Pain Points (from community research)

- Searches return SEO-bloated listicles with no real methodology ("we tested 50 games!" — no you didn't)
- Review scores that don't explain *why* or relate to the specific use case they care about
- Can't tell if a review was written by someone who played 8 hours or 800 hours
- Fear of wasting money on a game that turns out to be wrong for them specifically

**Community signal (r/JRPG ranking thread):** When a poster ranked 25+ JRPGs by specific metrics and explained their criteria, the community engaged intensely — debating methodology, asking follow-up questions, and adding their own data. The format itself (metric-based, personal playtime-backed, transparent criteria) generated trust and conversation that a simple opinion post wouldn't.

**AI distrust signal (Quantic Foundry 2025):** 85% of gamers are negative about generative AI in gaming, with 63% choosing the most negative option available. The concern is explicitly about losing *"handcrafted authenticity"*. For Metric Gamer: if this persona suspects scores are AI-generated rather than researched from real player reviews, trust collapses immediately.

### How They Use Metric Gamer

1. **Entry:** Google → Ranked List Page (cold landing)
2. **Behaviour:** Reads list intro to understand the criteria → scans rankings → checks per-metric breakdown on the top 2–3 games → may toggle off a metric that doesn't apply to them (e.g. removes "Online Multiplayer" because they only care about couch co-op)
3. **Exit paths:** Clicks into a Game Page to deep-dive on their top pick → checks "Where to Play" → potentially follows to social for future lists

### What Earns Their Trust

- Visible methodology ("scores are based on X hours of Steam/Reddit/forum research")
- Per-metric breakdown — they can see *why* a game scored what it scored
- Pros/cons that reflect real player frustrations, not PR speak
- The metric toggle — shows the tool respects that their priorities differ from someone else's

### Implications

- **Content:** Ranked List Page must communicate methodology clearly above the fold on cold landing — this user hasn't seen the homepage
- **Growth:** SEO on long-tail, context-specific queries is the primary acquisition channel for this persona
- **Design:** Metric toggle needs to be prominent — it's the differentiator that converts this user from "another ranking site" to "this is actually built for me"

## Persona 2 — "The Evaluator"

> *"I'm thinking about buying this game — I just want to know if it's actually worth it for someone like me."*

### Who They Are

- **Age:** 18–35
- **Gender:** Mixed; broadly representative of the gaming population
- **Platform:** Steam-heavy; console players who cross-reference before purchasing
- **Discovery behaviour:** Arrives at a game page directly — either from a Google search for the game name, or from a backlink on a gaming blog / guest post they were already reading
- **Review habits:** Reads multiple sources before committing. Checks Steam reviews, Reddit threads, YouTube video reviews (YouTube is the most trusted discovery source at 52% — BGM 2024). Is specifically looking for *"is this worth it for me"*, not a universal score
- **Typical session:** Lands on a Metric Gamer game page, reads end-to-end, focuses on pros/cons and verdict, checks FAQ for their specific concern

### Motivations

- **Purchase confidence** — they're close to a buying decision and want one more data point that validates or challenges it
- **Relatability** — they want to read a review written by someone who cared about the same things they care about
- **Specificity of concerns** — they often have *one specific worry* (e.g. "is the co-op good enough to justify this?") and they scan for it

### Pain Points (from community research)

- Professional reviews that score a game 9/10 but bury the co-op issues in paragraph 6
- User reviews that are either "10/10 best game ever" or "0/10 trash" with nothing in between
- AI-generated review content that sounds thorough but doesn't reflect what actual players experienced

**Community signal (r/EpicSeven positive tool thread):** When a community member built a stats tool that genuinely solved a player pain point, the reception was warm and the comments reflected *relief* — "finally something that actually helps with X". The tool was trusted immediately because it came from a community member, not a company. The methodology was visible and the creator was present to answer questions.

**Authenticity signal (Niche Gamer review trust data):** 46% of gamers trust other players' reviews more than professional ones. Only 12% prefer expert opinions. The key word in the data is *"relatable"* — not necessarily more accurate, but more trustworthy because the reviewer had the same stake in the game as the reader.

**AI reviews on Steam (Oreate AI research):** The rise of AI-generated reviews on Steam has triggered community concern specifically because they *sound* authentic but aren't. This persona is primed to smell inauthenticity — they've been burned by it. Metric Gamer's explicit sourcing from Reddit, Steam, and forums is a direct counter to this distrust.

### How They Use Metric Gamer

1. **Entry:** Google (game name) → Game Page, or guest post backlink → Game Page
2. **Behaviour:** Reads introduction to get context → jumps to pros/cons (this is their anchor — it's where real player pain points live) → reads verdict → checks FAQ for their specific concern → checks "Where to Play" if convinced
3. **Exit paths:** Clicks "Similar Games" → Ranked List Page (discovers the broader context of the game) → may explore 1–2 more games on the list

### What Earns Their Trust

- Pros/cons that include genuine negatives — sanitised, positive-only reviews are an immediate red flag for this persona
- FAQ section that addresses real questions (sourced from community questions, not invented)
- Attribution or signals that scores come from real player research ("based on Steam reviews, Reddit discussions, and forum posts")
- Absence of affiliate-heavy language or obvious promotional framing

### Implications

- **Content:** Pros/cons and FAQ must be the most honest, player-grounded sections of the game page — this is where this persona decides if they trust the site
- **Growth:** Guest post backlinks are a strong acquisition channel; the game page must stand alone with no context from the homepage
- **Design:** The game page should surface the "why behind the score" quickly — metric breakdown visible without heavy scrolling

## Persona 3 — "The Browser"

> *"I'm not looking for anything specific — I just want something new to play and I'll know it when I see it."*

### Who They Are

- **Age:** 16–28 (skews younger than other personas)
- **Gender:** Mixed; Gen Z representation higher here
- **Platform:** Console and mobile crossover; PC casual
- **Discovery behaviour:** Social-first. Finds content through Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube. Doesn't start with a Google search — starts with a piece of content that catches their eye. 80% of Gen Z gamers play to stay connected with friends (MixBright data), so social and co-op framing resonates strongly
- **Review habits:** Lighter research than other personas. More likely to be influenced by a single well-presented piece of content. TikTok and Instagram rank as 36% and 35% top discovery sources for this age group (BGM 2024)
- **Typical session:** Sees a Metric Gamer Instagram post or TikTok about a ranked list → taps the link → lands on the homepage or a ranked list → browses

### Motivations

- **Inspiration** — they want to discover something they didn't know they wanted
- **Social proof** — a ranking or list format signals that others have validated these games
- **Shareable context** — content they can send to a friend ("check this out, we should play this")
- **Low friction** — they won't read 1,500 words; they want scannable, visual, punchy content

### Pain Points (from community research)

- Discovery overload — Steam has 20,000+ games released per year; choice paralysis is real
- Recommendations that don't know anything about them — generic "popular games" lists feel irrelevant
- Content that feels corporate or inauthentic — this demographic is highly attuned to brand voice that doesn't feel genuine

**Community signal (r/JRPG ranking thread):** The engagement pattern on ranking content is consistent: gamers don't just read, they *participate*. They comment, they debate, they add their own rankings. Metric Gamer's ranked list format — especially with metric toggles — is built for this kind of participation impulse.

**Social media discovery (BGM 2024):** YouTube is the #1 discovery platform (64%), TikTok and Instagram are both top 3 for the 18–24 segment. Metric Gamer's presence on Instagram, YouTube, and TikTok directly maps to where this persona discovers content. The challenge: social content needs to earn a click through to the site — the value proposition must be clear in the post itself.

**AI/inauthenticity signal:** This persona is the most likely to disengage silently — they don't write angry comments, they just leave. Brand authenticity and a genuine voice matter significantly here. The "gamer-to-gamer" positioning of Metric Gamer is a core trust signal for this audience.

### How They Use Metric Gamer

1. **Entry:** Social media post → Homepage (or directly to a Ranked List Page if the post links there)
2. **Behaviour:** Scans the ranked list visually → reads the list intro to understand the context → may toggle metrics to see how the ranking changes → clicks into a game or two out of curiosity → may explore other lists via the rankings index
3. **Exit paths:** Follows Metric Gamer on social → returns later for new list content → may share a list with friends

### What Earns Their Trust

- Strong, specific list titles ("Best Games to Play Drunk with Friends" lands better than "Top Party Games")
- Visual hierarchy that lets them scan without reading everything
- A voice that feels like it was written by a gamer, not a content team
- The metric toggle — interactive elements keep this persona engaged longer than static content

### Implications

- **Content:** Social content must contain enough value in the post itself to earn the click; the "hook" is the ranked list concept and specific niche framing
- **Growth:** Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube are primary acquisition channels for this persona; content strategy should prioritise shareable, niche-specific list formats
- **Design:** Ranked List Page must be scannable first, deep-readable second — this persona decides in seconds whether to stay

## Shared Truths Across All Personas

These apply regardless of which persona you're building for:

1. **Peer trust beats critic trust.** 46% of gamers trust other players over professionals. Metric Gamer's sourcing methodology is a direct product feature, not just a positioning line — communicate it.

2. **AI content is an existential threat to trust.** 85% of gamers are negative about generative AI. Any hint that scores or descriptions are AI-generated rather than research-backed will collapse trust. Metric Gamer must visibly ground its output in real player sources.

3. **Context specificity is the product.** Generic rankings are not valued. The niche framing of each campaign ("Best Split-Screen RPG Games on PS2") is the core differentiator — it's what makes a Metric Gamer list worth reading over any other list.

4. **Average gamer uses 4–5 discovery channels.** Metric Gamer doesn't need to win every channel — it needs to be present and consistent across the channels where its personas are active.

5. **The metric toggle is a trust mechanism, not just a UX feature.** It signals to the user that Metric Gamer understands their priorities are individual — which is the same promise as "gamer-to-gamer".

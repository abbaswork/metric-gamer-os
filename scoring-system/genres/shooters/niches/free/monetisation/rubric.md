# Metric: Monetisation Model
**Sub-genre:** Shooters — Free
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = monetisation problems absent, 1 = the business model defines the experience

## Why This Matters

Free-to-play shooters have a specific monetisation challenge that other free genres don't share: the battle pass. Because shooters are the genre where battle passes became the dominant model, they are also the genre with the most documented community debate about when a battle pass is fair and when it becomes a pay-to-progress system.

The community distinction the FPS community makes is between cosmetic and gameplay content in the same battle pass. A battle pass that locks weapon blueprint skins, sprays, and operator skins behind its paid tier is accepted — these are cosmetic. A battle pass where the paid tier unlocks XP bonuses or weapon blueprints with different iron sights than the free base variant crosses into gameplay impact. This is the shooter-specific pay-to-win concern in its sharpest form: in a game where gunfights are the primary mechanic, you can see the weapon stats, look up the tier lists, and find out precisely what the performance gap is between what you can earn and what others paid for. The community documents two concrete versions of it. The first is the weapon blueprint problem: paid blueprints include unique iron sights or reticles that provide an aiming advantage over the default version of the same weapon — technically the same gun, functionally different in a heads-up engagement. The second is the locked-weapon problem: newer, stronger weapons are introduced through battle passes or store bundles and aren't made earnable through gameplay, creating a growing power gap over time. Warzone is the documented case for both, plus a related operator skin controversy — though the specific mechanism there is narrower than "different hitboxes." Community testing has generally found hitbox geometry is identical across operators; the real advantage is visibility, since skins like the all-black Roze or the Terminator/Endo bundle blend into dark corners and shadows, making the wearer harder to spot at range.

Fortnite's original battle pass is the positive reference: cosmetic-only, battle pass progression independent of match performance. CS:GO and VALORANT extend the same principle to weapons specifically — all competitively relevant weapons available to every player, paid content limited to skins that are purely cosmetic — which is the benchmark the competitive shooter community uses when criticising other games' weapon access. Apex Legends' Legend unlock system is a separate, adjacent debate: Legends are playable characters with unique abilities, and whether they should be earnable is a consistent community discussion distinct from weapon or cosmetic monetisation.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | All gameplay-affecting content — weapons, abilities, characters, operators — is accessible through play, and new weapons are earnable on the same timeline as paid players rather than gated behind a battle pass tier. The battle pass, if one exists, is cosmetic only. No paid tier provides a visibility, movement, or mechanical advantage. XP progression is not locked behind payment. | The free experience is the full competitive game. What you look like costs money. What you shoot with and who you play as doesn't. |
| 4 | Almost all gameplay content, including new weapons, is accessible for free. Some content takes longer to earn without spending, but nothing behind the paid tier — including weapon blueprints — provides a meaningful competitive advantage. Battle pass rewards are primarily cosmetic with minor non-gameplay additions. | You'll access the competitive meta through play. The battle pass might have things you want, but you're not missing gameplay because you didn't buy it. |
| 3 | Some gameplay-affecting content — an operator ability, a weapon blueprint with different iron sights, or an XP bonus that affects unlock speed — sits behind the paid tier. New weapons may be time-gated to the battle pass before becoming earnable. The core game is competitive for free players but the paid tier provides acknowledged advantages. | You can compete for free. You'll notice what the paid tier does to the competitive environment, and some of it will affect matches. |
| 2 | The paid battle pass includes content that meaningfully affects competitive outcomes. Operator skins with a documented visibility/camouflage edge, weapon blueprints with real iron-sight or attachment advantages, or character ability access create a two-tier competitive environment. The free weapon pool falls behind as new weapons are introduced exclusively through purchase. Free players are at a structural disadvantage in ranked or competitive modes. | Paying buys competitive advantage, not just cosmetics. The game is not free-to-win, but it isn't free-to-compete-equally either. |
| 1 | Paid content provides direct, measurable competitive advantages. Operator skins are demonstrably harder to spot than the default roster, paid weapon blueprints are quantifiably stronger or have a clear aiming edge, and free players cannot replicate the top-tier loadout regardless of time invested. The free experience is a lobby for paying players to farm. | The game has pay-to-win mechanics. The competitive environment is defined by who spent money, not who got better. |

# Metric: Squad Play & Class Design
**Sub-genre:** Shooters — Multiplayer (Squad / Team-Based)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = roles are distinct and teams that coordinate win more, 1 = class choice doesn't affect team outcomes

## Why This Matters

Squad play is the quality dimension that separates team-based shooters from games that happen to have teams. When class design is working, a coordinated squad beats a better-aimed but disorganised one — and players know which role they filled in that outcome. A medic reviving a teammate who holds a key position, a scout marking targets for a sniper, an engineer repairing a tank at the right moment — these create the moments that define the genre for its fans. When class design isn't working, the five-player team is five individual players sharing a respawn timer.

Battlefield is the community's reference frame for class design done right. BF3 and BF4's Scout, Assault, Support, and Engineer roles are designed so that playing your role well and playing it for your team are the same action — the Medic's revive is cited most consistently as the example of an inherently cooperative action that produces better outcomes for the team than individual play. Battlefield 5 pushed this further with fortification: squads that build and defend positions together get a tangible, mechanical advantage that solo play can't replicate, layered on top of role-specific tools like the Engineer's vehicle repair and the Support player's ammo resupply. Apex Legends is the smaller-squad equivalent — Legend abilities are designed to have team utility, and high-level Apex play is documented as being defined as much by ability synergy (a scout's intel before an entry, a support's heal mid-fight) as by aim. The failure reference is Warzone, where operators are cosmetic-only with no underlying class system — a direct community comparison point against Battlefield, whose classes are built to complement each other in a way Warzone's loadouts aren't. PlanetSide 2 sits at the largest end of this spectrum: outfit-level coordination is credited as the thing that makes 1,200-player combined-arms battles more than a mindless shooter, not just the individual mechanics.

This is distinct from general teamplay in arena shooters. The defining trait here is mechanical role specialization tied to squad-level cooperation — not just "5 v 5 with comms," but classes built so that a squad with all roles filled plays meaningfully differently from four players running the same loadout.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Distinct roles create genuinely different team functions. Coordinated squads consistently outperform aim-only squads in the same match. Mechanics like reviving, repairing, resupplying, or building/defending fortified positions are tied to specific roles, so a full squad with every role filled has a clear edge over four players in the same loadout. Players can identify their contribution to team outcomes through their role. | Playing your role well and playing for your team are the same thing. When a coordinated squad beats a better-aimed one, you understand why. |
| 4 | Classes have distinct functions and team play is rewarded in most contexts. Some roles are more impactful than others but no class is redundant. Coordination improves team outcomes measurably. Some abilities are primarily individual but the class structure as a whole supports team play. | Playing with a coordinated squad feels meaningfully better than going in without a plan. Your role matters, even if not all roles are equal in every context. |
| 3 | Classes exist with differentiated abilities but the team coordination benefit is moderate. Individual skill and aim are the primary performance drivers. Class abilities provide situational advantages but not enough to change team outcomes in most matches. A team that doesn't coordinate can still beat a team that does. | Classes add texture to the game but team composition is not a significant factor in most matches. Coordinating is better than not coordinating, but not by enough to build a playstyle around. |
| 2 | Class system exists but roles are not functionally distinct enough to affect team strategy. Abilities are primarily individual. Team coordination has minimal impact on match outcomes. Character or class choice is more aesthetic than strategic. | The class system is a selection screen. What you pick doesn't change much about what you or your team does in a match. |
| 1 | Class design provides no meaningful team function. All classes play identically in practice or ability differences are too minor to change outcomes. Team coordination has no measurable benefit over individual play. The squad is five individuals by any measure that matters. | There is no team game here, only individual play that happens to share a map with four other people. |

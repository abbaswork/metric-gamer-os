# Metric: Map Design
**Sub-genre:** Shooters — Multiplayer
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = maps support diverse tactics and memorable moments, 1 = maps produce the same outcome every match

## Why This Matters

Map design in multiplayer shooters is the single biggest determinant of whether matches feel tactical or random. Unlike single-player level design — which is about pacing, exploration, and set-pieces — multiplayer maps are evaluated on whether they produce skill-differentiating situations. A map that consistently funnels all players through one lane produces the same firefight every time. A map that offers multiple viable approaches to the same objective creates a game where positioning and teamwork change outcomes.

The community references are consistent: Dust 2 in Counter-Strike is the most cited example of map design that has lasted decades because its geometry makes every tactical element of the game legible — angles, economy, rotations. Battlefield's large-scale conquest maps are praised for vehicle and infantry integration that creates multiple simultaneous theatres of play. The failure reference is equally consistent: the domination of one-lane or three-lane maps in modern Call of Duty was directly tied to the decline of flanking and map control as skills, reducing matches to reaction-time contests in predictable corridors.

## Sources

- **r/GlobalOffensive** (training data) — Dust 2 map geometry and its tactical properties are one of the most analysed topics in CS community; "map knowledge" as a separate skill to aim is well documented
- **r/Battlefield** (training data) — Conquest map design and vehicle integration are consistently praised; Rush mode map balance is one of the most discussed design elements in the franchise
- **r/CODWarzone and r/ModernWarfare** (training data) — the shift toward three-lane linear maps and the loss of flanking routes is documented as a community frustration; "map design has gotten worse" is a recurring thread topic
- **designbettergames.com / gamedeveloper.com** — multiplayer map design principles and the difference between reaction-time and tactical skill in corridor maps are documented in game design writing (training data)

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Maps offer multiple viable approaches to objectives. Flanking routes exist and are regularly used. Map knowledge is a learnable skill that rewards experienced players. No single choke point defines every match. Vehicle and infantry roles are meaningfully integrated where relevant to the game. | The map is part of your toolkit. Where you move matters as much as how you shoot. Matches feel different because different strategies played out, not because the same firefight happened in the same spot again. |
| 4 | Maps generally support tactical play with occasional bottlenecks that dominate matches at certain times. Multiple routes exist but some are underused due to balance. Map knowledge provides a real advantage. The best maps in the pool are consistently praised; the weakest are acknowledged issues. | Most matches feel tactical. You'll learn the maps and feel the benefit. A few maps will frustrate you with their design, but the rotation as a whole supports good play. |
| 3 | Maps support basic tactical play but tend toward predictable flow. One or two choke points dominate most matches. Flanking routes exist but are rarely viable in competitive play. Map knowledge helps but the geometry is simple enough that it diminishes quickly as a differentiator. | You'll learn the maps quickly. Matches follow recognisable patterns. You'll have moments of tactical satisfaction but the maps won't regularly produce surprise. |
| 2 | Maps are predominantly linear or three-lane with limited flanking options. Match flow is highly predictable. Choke points define most engagements. Map knowledge provides minimal advantage over reaction time. The community has identified specific maps as design failures. | The map is a backdrop for aim duels rather than a tactical environment. Positioning matters less than reflexes because the geometry doesn't reward it. |
| 1 | Maps actively undermine tactical play. Single-lane or spawn-trap designs produce the same outcome regardless of team strategy. Flanking is mechanically impossible or punished by design. Map knowledge offers no meaningful advantage. | The map is the match. Winning is determined by which team spawned at the better end, not what they did once the match started. |

# Metric: Community
**Sub-genre:** Shooters — Multiplayer (Player Behavior & Population Health)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = the actual playerbase is friendly, low-toxicity, and free of rampant cheating, with a healthy population that gets you into a match, 1 = lobbies are toxic and/or hacker-dominated, and the population is too thin to reliably find a match

## Why This Matters

This metric is scored on two parts. Part one is the actual, observed behavior of the playerbase — not the game's design intentions. A game can ship every social-friendly system imaginable and still have a toxic or cheater-ridden community in practice, and a game with minimal moderation tooling can still have a genuinely friendly one; what matters here is what the community actually is, not what a developer built it to be. That behavior has two failure modes worth scoring separately: toxicity (harassment, hostility, an unwelcoming lobby culture) and cheating (aimbots, wallhacks, and other illicit software that make matches feel unfair regardless of social tone). A lobby can be perfectly polite and still be ruined by a cheater, and a cheat-free lobby can still be toxic — both are real, independent ways a community can fail.

Toxicity is one of the most commonly cited reasons players stop playing a multiplayer game altogether, independent of how the shooting itself feels. Industry research puts a number on it: a majority of multiplayer players say they'd likely stop playing a game specifically because of another player's toxic behavior, and voice chat is repeatedly named as the highest-risk surface for it. Hell Let Loose is the community's own reference point for what a genuinely welcoming playerbase looks like in practice, despite being a voice-comms-dependent, high-stakes team shooter — exactly the profile that tends to produce toxic lobbies.

Cheating is a distinct, and currently more acute, community-health failure. Warzone's own subreddit is described as "buzzing with complaints" about hackers despite years of dedicated anti-cheat investment, and Counter-Strike 2 players report roughly a coin-flip chance of running into a cheater in high-level lobbies as of 2026, with wallhacks and speed hacks bypassing detection frequently enough to be a constant topic of community discussion. A community can be perfectly friendly and still be unplayable if every third match has someone cheating in it.

Part two is population and matchmaking health — whether the playerbase is large enough, right now, to actually get you into a match. This is the OP's own experience from the r/fps intro post backing this research: excited to try Battlefield 1's dogfight mode, but never able to find an active match for it, while niche modes elsewhere sat empty even with the base game still installed. This is a community-scale property, not a mode-design one — a game can have a brilliantly designed mode list and still fail this metric if there simply aren't enough people left playing to fill it. Battlefield 1's own player forums are full of the same complaint independently: threads asking where the active servers are, describing multiplayer with "significant server problems," years after launch.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The actual playerbase is friendly or neutral by default — toxic behavior is the rare exception, not something you brace for every match. Cheaters are rare enough that you don't expect to run into one. The population is healthy enough that every mode you'd want to play is reliably reachable. | You lose track of time playing this because the people in it make it fun, the matches feel legitimate, and you're never stuck waiting for a game. |
| 4 | Most of the playerbase is friendly or neutral, with toxicity or the occasional cheater showing up rarely enough that it doesn't define a session. Reporting or anti-cheat tools catch most problems. Population supports a match without much of a wait. | You'll hit the odd bad apple or suspicious death, but it's rare enough not to change how you feel about the game, and you're rarely waiting long for a match. |
| 3 | Whether a match is pleasant and cheater-free is close to a coin flip. Moderation and anti-cheat tools exist but are inconsistently effective. Finding a populated match takes real effort at some hours or in some modes. | Some sessions are great, some aren't, for reasons of both people and population, and you've made peace with that. |
| 2 | Toxic behavior or cheaters show up often enough that you expect to run into one or the other in a given session, and reporting or anti-cheat tools rarely produce a visible consequence. Matches are frequently hard to fill outside the most popular mode. | You'll assume the worst about a chunk of your lobbies and treat a clean, populated match as the surprise rather than the expectation. |
| 1 | Toxicity and cheating are both the norm rather than the exception, with no effective moderation or anti-cheat response. The population is thin enough that finding a match at all, in almost any mode, is a struggle. | The community and the empty servers are the reason to stop playing, even if you still like the shooting underneath it. |

# Metric: Maps
**Sub-genre:** Shooters — Multiplayer (Large-Scale)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = maps support diverse tactics, reward map knowledge, and are scaled so a full lobby never feels empty, 1 = maps produce the same outcome every match and regularly feel empty regardless of population

## Why This Matters

Map design in multiplayer shooters is the single biggest determinant of whether matches feel tactical or random. Unlike single-player level design — which is about pacing, exploration, and set-pieces — multiplayer maps are evaluated on whether they produce skill-differentiating situations and whether objectives stay relevant across the whole map rather than collapsing into one contested area. A map that consistently funnels all players through one lane produces the same firefight every time, whether that funnel is a choke point or a single dominant route between objectives. A map that offers multiple viable approaches and spreads objectives out enough that the whole map sees play creates a game where positioning and teamwork change outcomes.

The community references are consistent: Dust 2 in Counter-Strike is the most cited example of map design that has lasted decades because its geometry makes every tactical element of the game legible — angles, economy, rotations. Battlefield's large-scale conquest maps are praised for spreading objectives across varied terrain so no single capture point decides the match. The failure reference is equally consistent: the domination of one-lane or three-lane maps in modern Call of Duty was directly tied to the decline of flanking and map control as skills, reducing matches to reaction-time contests in predictable corridors.

A map's scale relative to its lobby size is a second, equally important axis, distinct from route/objective design — a map can have excellent flanking and objective spread and still fail if it's simply too large for the number of players in it. This is the OP's own wording from the r/fps intro post backing this research: "maps that don't feel empty with a full lobby is pretty big one." Battlefield 2042 is the community's own reference case for getting this wrong even at max population — despite 128-player lobbies, its maps were widely described as empty and tedious to cross, with the term "Walking Simulator" used to describe the amount of time spent getting to a fight; DICE itself acknowledged that bigger maps don't automatically mean more fun and scaled future maps down in response.

This metric covers route/objective layout and population density specifically. How well a map's terrain integrates vehicles is scored under Movement, not here — a map can have excellent flanking, objective spread, and population density regardless of whether it has vehicles at all.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Maps offer multiple viable approaches to objectives. Flanking routes exist and are regularly used. Map knowledge is a learnable skill that rewards experienced players. No single choke point defines every match, and objectives stay contested across the whole map rather than collapsing into one area. Maps are scaled to their player count — a full lobby produces constant, spread-out engagement rather than long empty walks between fights. | The map is part of your toolkit. Where you move matters as much as how you shoot. Matches feel different because different strategies played out, not because the same firefight happened in the same spot again, and you're never far from the action. |
| 4 | Maps generally support tactical play with occasional bottlenecks that dominate matches at certain times. Multiple routes exist but some are underused due to balance. Map knowledge provides a real advantage. The best maps in the pool are consistently praised; the weakest are acknowledged issues. A map or two in the rotation feels slightly oversized for a full lobby, with the occasional longer walk to a fight. | Most matches feel tactical. You'll learn the maps and feel the benefit. A few maps will frustrate you with their design or feel a bit emptier than the rest, but the rotation as a whole supports good play. |
| 3 | Maps support basic tactical play but tend toward predictable flow. One or two choke points dominate most matches. Flanking routes exist but are rarely viable in competitive play. Map knowledge helps but the geometry is simple enough that it diminishes quickly as a differentiator. A noticeable portion of the rotation feels sparse even with a full lobby. | You'll learn the maps quickly. Matches follow recognisable patterns. You'll have moments of tactical satisfaction but the maps won't regularly produce surprise, and a few will feel oddly quiet even with everyone online. |
| 2 | Maps are predominantly linear or three-lane with limited flanking options. Match flow is highly predictable. Choke points define most engagements. Map knowledge provides minimal advantage over reaction time. The community has identified specific maps as design failures, including maps that feel sparsely populated even at full lobby size. | The map is a backdrop for aim duels rather than a tactical environment. Positioning matters less than reflexes because the geometry doesn't reward it, and several maps feel emptier than the player count should allow. |
| 1 | Maps actively undermine tactical play. Single-lane or spawn-trap designs produce the same outcome regardless of team strategy. Flanking is mechanically impossible or punished by design. Map knowledge offers no meaningful advantage. Maps regularly feel empty and lifeless regardless of how full the lobby actually is. | The map is the match. Winning is determined by which team spawned at the better end, not what they did once the match started, and long stretches of a match are spent walking toward a fight rather than being in one. |

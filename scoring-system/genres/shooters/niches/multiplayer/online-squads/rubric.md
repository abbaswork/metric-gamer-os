# Metric: Squad Play & Class Design
**Sub-genre:** Shooters — Multiplayer (Squad / Team-Based)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = roles are distinct and teams that coordinate win more, especially in hardcore/competitive play, without needing to be strictly enforced, 1 = class choice doesn't affect team outcomes

## Why This Matters

Squad play is the quality dimension that separates team-based shooters from games that happen to have teams. When class design is working, a coordinated squad beats a better-aimed but disorganised one — and players know which role they filled in that outcome. A medic reviving a teammate who holds a key position, a scout marking targets for a sniper, an engineer repairing a tank at the right moment — these create the moments that define the genre for its fans. When class design isn't working, the five-player team is five individual players sharing a respawn timer.

This doesn't require classes to be a hard requirement — the OP's own 5/5 definition from the r/fps intro post backing this research is explicit that "doesn't have to be strictly enforced but we can run squad play with different roles that contribute to the win." A squad that self-organises into complementary roles without the game forcing a rigid class lock still counts, as long as that coordination produces a real result. The same definition also scopes where this matters most: "especially in tourney and hardcore matches" — casual play not showing a strong coordination benefit isn't a failure on its own, but a game's hardcore or competitive modes should be where role-based coordination clearly separates a good squad from a disorganised one.

Battlefield is the community's reference frame for class design done right. BF3 and BF4's Scout, Assault, Support, and Engineer roles are designed so that playing your role well and playing it for your team are the same action — the Medic's revive is cited most consistently as the example of an inherently cooperative action that produces better outcomes for the team than individual play. Battlefield 5 pushed this further with fortification: squads that build and defend positions together get a tangible, mechanical advantage that solo play can't replicate, layered on top of role-specific tools like the Engineer's vehicle repair and the Support player's ammo resupply. Apex Legends is the smaller-squad equivalent — Legend abilities are designed to have team utility, and high-level Apex play is documented as being defined as much by ability synergy (a scout's intel before an entry, a support's heal mid-fight) as by aim. The failure reference is Warzone, where operators are cosmetic-only with no underlying class system — a direct community comparison point against Battlefield, whose classes are built to complement each other in a way Warzone's loadouts aren't. PlanetSide 2 sits at the largest end of this spectrum: outfit-level coordination is credited as the thing that makes 1,200-player combined-arms battles more than a mindless shooter, not just the individual mechanics.

This is distinct from general teamplay in arena shooters. The defining trait here is mechanical role specialization tied to squad-level cooperation — not just "5 v 5 with comms," but classes built so that a squad with all roles filled plays meaningfully differently from four players running the same loadout.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Distinct roles create genuinely different team functions, whether the game enforces them with a hard class lock or players adopt them by choice. Coordinated squads consistently outperform aim-only squads, and the gap is clearest in the game's hardcore or competitive modes specifically. Mechanics like reviving, repairing, resupplying, or building/defending fortified positions are tied to specific roles, so a full squad with every role filled has a clear edge over four players in the same loadout. Players can identify their contribution to team outcomes through their role. | Playing your role well and playing for your team are the same thing. When a coordinated squad beats a better-aimed one, you understand why — and in hardcore or ranked play, that gap is impossible to miss. |
| 4 | Classes have distinct functions and team play is rewarded in most contexts, without requiring strict role enforcement to pay off. Some roles are more impactful than others but no class is redundant. Coordination improves team outcomes measurably, especially in hardcore or competitive matches. Some abilities are primarily individual but the class structure as a whole supports team play. | Playing with a coordinated squad feels meaningfully better than going in without a plan. Your role matters, even if not all roles are equal in every context, and it matters most once things get competitive. |
| 3 | Classes exist with differentiated abilities but the team coordination benefit is moderate, even in the game's hardcore or competitive modes. Individual skill and aim are the primary performance drivers. Class abilities provide situational advantages but not enough to change team outcomes in most matches. A team that doesn't coordinate can still beat a team that does. | Classes add texture to the game but team composition is not a significant factor in most matches, even the ones meant to reward it. Coordinating is better than not coordinating, but not by enough to build a playstyle around. |
| 2 | Class system exists but roles are not functionally distinct enough to affect team strategy, in casual or hardcore modes alike. Abilities are primarily individual. Team coordination has minimal impact on match outcomes. Character or class choice is more aesthetic than strategic. | The class system is a selection screen. What you pick doesn't change much about what you or your team does in a match, whatever mode you're in. |
| 1 | Class design provides no meaningful team function. All classes play identically in practice or ability differences are too minor to change outcomes. Team coordination has no measurable benefit over individual play, even in the game's most competitive setting. The squad is five individuals by any measure that matters. | There is no team game here, only individual play that happens to share a map with four other people. |

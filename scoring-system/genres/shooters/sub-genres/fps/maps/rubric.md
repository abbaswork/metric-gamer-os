# Metric: Maps
**Genre:** Shooters
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = maps support varied infantry and vehicle tactics across multiple objectives, 1 = maps are linear bottlenecks that funnel every fight into the same chokepoint

## Why This Matters

In large-scale multiplayer shooters, a map has to do more work than in a tactical arena shooter — it needs to support infantry on foot, vehicles moving at speed, and multiple simultaneous objectives without any single approach becoming mandatory. "Maps" was one of the most consistently raised factors in player discussion of Battlefield-style shooters, and the recurring praise pattern is specific: multiple viable routes between objectives, terrain that rewards both vehicle and infantry play, and capture points spread out enough that the whole map stays relevant rather than collapsing into one contested chokepoint.

Vehicle traversal is part of map quality here, not a separate concern. A map that places vehicle lanes through terrain infantry can't meaningfully contest, or that leaves no cover for infantry crossing open ground a vehicle just drove through, is failing at map design even if the individual vehicles themselves handle well. Good large-scale maps balance the two so that vehicles and infantry are both threats and both vulnerable, depending on terrain and positioning.

## Sources

- **research/shooters/readme.md** — user-curated research from Reddit discussions of Battlefield 5/1/2042 and Call of Duty Black Ops 2/Modern Warfare 2; "Maps" listed as a standalone recurring factor, with vehicle variety and customization listed separately as a related but distinct concern
- **r/Battlefield** (training data) — recurring complaints about maps with one dominant route or vehicle-dominated sightlines that leave infantry with no safe approach; praise for maps with multiple flanking routes and capture points spread across varied terrain
- **r/CallOfDuty** (training data) — large-team modes (e.g. Ground War) discussed specifically in terms of whether maps are scaled appropriately for vehicle play without leaving infantry-only objectives exposed

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Every map supports multiple viable routes between objectives for both infantry and vehicles. Vehicle lanes and infantry cover are balanced — neither dominates the other by default. Objectives are spread out enough that the whole map stays contested. | You'll approach every objective differently depending on what you're playing. No single route or vehicle run wins the map by itself. |
| 4 | Most maps balance vehicle and infantry play well, with a couple of maps where one vehicle lane or sightline is a bit too dominant. Objectives are spread out enough that most of the map sees play. | You'll have a few maps where vehicles call the shots more than you'd like, but it doesn't define your sessions. |
| 3 | Maps function without major structural problems, but encounters cluster around the same chokepoints or vehicle lanes match after match. Some objectives see far less traffic than others. | You'll learn where the real fight happens fast, and after that, a chunk of the map stops mattering. |
| 2 | Several maps have a dominant vehicle lane or sightline that infantry can't meaningfully contest, or objectives clustered closely enough that capturing one decides most of the match. | A handful of maps will feel like they're fought entirely on the vehicles' terms, or over before they've really started. |
| 1 | Maps funnel every fight into the same chokepoint regardless of vehicles or objectives. Vehicle lanes leave infantry no safe crossing, or the map is so small that positioning stops mattering. | Where you spawn and what you're driving decides the match more than how you play it. |

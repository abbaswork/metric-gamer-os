# Metric: Enemy Variety
**Genre:** Shooters (Single Player Campaign)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = diverse enemies that demand different tactics, 1 = the same enemy type throughout with cosmetic variations

## Why This Matters

Enemy variety is the difference between a campaign that teaches you to play and one that just makes you repeat what you already know. The community reference is Halo 2: "incredible variety requiring tactical thinking about weapon choices and enemy types." Different enemy types required different weapons, different positioning, different timing. The flip side — bullet sponge enemies that only scale health and damage — is the community's complaint about cheap difficulty. "Once an enemy takes a couple of bullets they either should be dead, or their ability to fight back should be greatly hindered." More health on the same enemy is not more variety.

Doom Eternal is the other reference point. Each demon type has a preferred weapon and an optimal engagement range. Not knowing the counter is a disadvantage. Knowing it is a skill. That's what good enemy design produces.

## Sources

- **NeoGAF: "What Constitutes Good Gunplay in a Shooter?"** — "once an enemy takes a couple of bullets they either should be dead, or their ability to fight back should be greatly hindered"; bullet sponges cited as a specific failure distinct from difficulty (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **gamerant.com: "10 FPS Masterpieces Defined By Gunplay And Level Design"** — Halo 2 cited for enemy variety that "required tactical thinking about weapon choices and enemy types"; Doom Eternal praised for encounter design built around distinct enemy-weapon interactions (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **whatculture.com: "10 Worst FPS Campaigns"** — "dumb enemy intelligence" and same-enemy repetition cited as failure patterns in poorly rated campaigns (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **r/Doom and r/Halo** (training data) — Doom Eternal's codex-driven enemy counter system is regularly cited as the benchmark for meaningful enemy variety

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Different enemy types require different approaches. Which weapon to use, where to position, and when to engage all change based on what's in the room. Encounters are designed around combinations that create specific problems. New enemy types are introduced across the full campaign and each one changes how you play. | Learning each enemy type feels like learning a new rule. What works on one type fails on another. |
| 4 | Good enemy variety with meaningful behavioral differences across most types. A few enemies are too similar to others to demand a real change in approach. The selection requires tactical adjustment through most of the campaign. | You'll develop different habits for different enemies. The exceptions are rare enough not to matter. |
| 3 | A functional selection of enemy types that vary in health and damage output. The underlying approach doesn't change much between them. Difficulty scales through numbers rather than behavior. | You'll learn the enemies quickly and carry the same habits through the whole game. |
| 2 | Most enemies behave in the same basic way. Variants look different but play identically. The campaign stops introducing new enemy types within the first half. | One approach handles everything. You won't need to adjust after the first few hours. |
| 1 | One dominant enemy type with cosmetic variations throughout. No tactical adjustment is required. | The enemy design doesn't ask anything of you after the tutorial. |

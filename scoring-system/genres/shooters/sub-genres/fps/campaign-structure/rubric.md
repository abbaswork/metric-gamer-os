# Metric: Campaign Structure
**Genre:** Shooters (Single Player Campaign)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = well-paced campaign that keeps introducing new ideas, 1 = repetitive corridor shooting with no structural variation

## Why This Matters

The community reference for what a campaign structure should do is Titanfall 2. Players return to it specifically because "it constantly introduces fresh content and inventive encounters." Each mission brings a new mechanic, environment constraint, or gameplay idea that isn't repeated. The contrast case is the pattern the community calls corridor shooting: a linear sequence of rooms with enemies, nothing to distinguish one section from another, no escalation in the type of challenges presented. FEAR is cited as the canonical example of good gunplay undermined by repetitive structure — the shooting was excellent, but by the middle of the game every encounter took place in an office.

Campaign structure is distinct from campaign length. A well-structured campaign produces specific memories of specific missions. A poorly structured one produces a general blur of the same encounter repeated.

## Sources

- **gamerant.com: "10 FPS Masterpieces Defined By Gunplay And Level Design"** — Titanfall 2 praised specifically for introducing fresh encounters across every mission; Half-Life 2 praised for variety across urban environments, highway sections, and horror areas (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **whatculture.com: "10 Worst FPS Campaigns Of All Time"** — identifies "repetitive level design," "dumb enemy intelligence," and "uninspired level objectives" as the failure patterns in poor campaigns (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **ResetEra: "Best FPS single player campaign recommendations"** — community discussion confirms that variety and pacing are what players remember most positively; missions players cite by name are the ones that did something different (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **r/FPS and r/patientgamers** (training data) — campaign replay discussions cite level variety and memorable set-pieces as reasons to return; "it plays the same throughout" is the recurring criticism of poorly rated campaigns

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Each mission introduces something new. A new mechanic, environment type, gameplay constraint, or encounter configuration that doesn't repeat. Pacing escalates without padding. The campaign leaves specific memories of specific moments. | You'll remember individual missions. The game earns the time it takes from you. |
| 4 | Well-paced with real variety across most of the campaign. A few sections lean too hard on one environment or encounter type. The overall structure keeps the experience moving forward. | The campaign holds your attention through most of its runtime. The slow stretches are short. |
| 3 | A functional campaign that covers the basics without memorable differentiation. Missions are competent. Objectives vary and environments change, but the underlying encounter structure stays the same. | You'll finish it without frustration. You probably won't replay it. |
| 2 | Campaign structure that leans heavily on one environment type or encounter pattern. The game stops introducing new ideas within the first third and repeats the same formula through to the end. Difficulty scales through enemy count. | A few hours in, you'll know exactly what the rest of the game holds. |
| 1 | A corridor with enemies. No structural variation, no pacing consideration, no moment distinct from another. | Every room is the same room. The game gives you nothing to remember. |

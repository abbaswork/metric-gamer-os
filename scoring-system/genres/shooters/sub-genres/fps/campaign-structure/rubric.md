# Metric: Campaign Structure
**Genre:** Shooters (Single Player Campaign)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = well-paced campaign that keeps introducing new ideas and leaves you wanting to replay it regardless of length, 1 = repetitive corridor shooting with no structural variation and no reason to return

## Why This Matters

The community reference for what a campaign structure should do is Titanfall 2. Players return to it specifically because "it constantly introduces fresh content and inventive encounters." Each mission brings a new mechanic, environment constraint, or gameplay idea that isn't repeated. The contrast case is the pattern the community calls corridor shooting: a linear sequence of rooms with enemies, nothing to distinguish one section from another, no escalation in the type of challenges presented. FEAR is cited as the canonical example of good gunplay undermined by repetitive structure — the shooting was excellent, but by the middle of the game every encounter took place in an office.

Campaign structure is distinct from campaign length, and length should never be scored as a downside on its own. The OP's own 5/5 definition from the r/fps intro post backing this research makes this explicit: "I don't mind short campaigns if they make me want to replay them and leave an impression." Their named gold standard is the original Modern Warfare 2's stealth missions — "All Ghillied Up" specifically is one of the most consistently cited standout levels in the entire genre, praised on release as one of the best stealth missions in gaming and still referenced today as a mission players actively return to, not just remember. A well-structured campaign produces specific memories of specific missions and a pull to play them again. A poorly structured one produces a general blur of the same encounter repeated, regardless of how many hours it runs.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Each mission introduces something new. A new mechanic, environment type, gameplay constraint, or encounter configuration that doesn't repeat. Pacing escalates without padding. The campaign leaves specific memories of specific moments strong enough that you want to play them again — a short campaign built this way loses nothing for its length. | You'll remember individual missions and want to revisit them. The game earns the time it takes from you regardless of how much time that actually is. |
| 4 | Well-paced with real variety across most of the campaign, and enough standout missions that you'd happily replay a few of them. A few sections lean too hard on one environment or encounter type. The overall structure keeps the experience moving forward. | The campaign holds your attention through most of its runtime, and a handful of missions are worth coming back to. The slow stretches are short. |
| 3 | A functional campaign that covers the basics without memorable differentiation. Missions are competent. Objectives vary and environments change, but the underlying encounter structure stays the same, and nothing in it pulls you back for another run. | You'll finish it without frustration. You probably won't replay it. |
| 2 | Campaign structure that leans heavily on one environment type or encounter pattern. The game stops introducing new ideas within the first third and repeats the same formula through to the end. Difficulty scales through enemy count. Nothing about it invites a second playthrough. | A few hours in, you'll know exactly what the rest of the game holds, and you won't be back once it's over. |
| 1 | A corridor with enemies. No structural variation, no pacing consideration, no moment distinct from another, and nothing that would ever justify replaying it. | Every room is the same room. The game gives you nothing to remember and no reason to return. |

# Metric: Movement
**Genre:** Shooters
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = movement options create real tactical choices and skill expression, 1 = movement is limited to walk/sprint/crouch with no mechanical depth

## Why This Matters

Movement is the layer underneath every other shooter mechanic — it determines how players close distance, break line of sight, and approach a fight before gunplay even comes into it. The community's interest in "gameplay around movement" shows up as a recurring factor even in shooters not built around movement tech specifically, which suggests it's evaluated as a baseline expectation rather than a bonus feature. Sliding, mantling, prone positioning, and vehicle-to-foot transitions are the concrete mechanics players point to when a shooter's movement feels like it adds options rather than just covering distance.

Where a shooter has vehicles, they're an extension of this same layer rather than a separate system. A vehicle is only a movement upgrade if it gives players a traversal option that wasn't there on foot — covering ground faster, reaching terrain infantry can't, or enabling a fast rotation between fights — and if getting in and out of it is clean enough that the transition itself doesn't cost you the advantage. A shooter with no vehicles at all isn't penalised for it; infantry-only movement can earn a 5 entirely on its own terms. But a shooter that has vehicles and uses them as a genuine extension of the movement skill layer should score higher than one where vehicles exist but movement effectively ends at the vehicle door, or where transitions are clumsy enough to fight the player instead of extending what they can do.

The failure case is movement that exists only to get from cover to cover with no decisions involved — no momentum, no verticality, no way to use terrain creatively. When movement is purely functional, every approach to a fight looks the same, and positioning stops being something a player can be good at.

## Sources

- **research.md — Reddit:** user-curated research from Reddit discussions of Battlefield 5/1/2042 and Call of Duty Black Ops 2/Modern Warfare 2; "Gameplay around movement" listed as a recurring factor
- **r/Battlefield** (training data) — prone positioning, terrain use, and the transition between vehicle and infantry movement are recurring discussion points in comparing entries in the series; vehicle lanes that infantry can't meaningfully use or contest are a recurring complaint distinct from vehicle balance itself
- **r/CallOfDuty** (training data) — sliding, mantling, and traversal speed are frequently compared across entries, with slower or more restrictive movement cited as a complaint in large-team modes specifically

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Movement includes multiple deliberate options — sliding, mantling, prone, vaulting — that change how you approach terrain and combat. Skilled movement creates a real advantage over walking straight at an objective. Where the game includes vehicles, transitions between vehicle and foot are seamless and genuinely extend what you can do with terrain rather than just covering ground faster; their absence doesn't cap the score on its own. | Getting somewhere well feels like its own skill. Terrain becomes something you use, not just something you cross — on foot or, if the game has them, behind the wheel. |
| 4 | A solid movement kit covers most situations well, with one or two mechanics that feel underused or clunky in specific terrain. Movement still rewards skill in most encounters. Vehicle-to-foot transitions, where present, work well in most situations even if a specific terrain type fights them. | You'll find a rhythm with the movement that works most of the time, with the occasional terrain — or vehicle handoff — that fights you. |
| 3 | Functional movement that covers the basics — sprinting, crouching, basic vaulting — without much beyond that. Terrain doesn't meaningfully reward creative positioning. Vehicles, where present, cover ground faster but don't add a meaningful skill layer beyond that. | You can get where you need to go. Getting there well doesn't change much, whether you're on foot or in a vehicle. |
| 2 | Movement is limited and occasionally fights the player — slow transitions, awkward vaulting, no meaningful prone or slide options. Terrain mostly just slows you down rather than offering options. Vehicle-to-foot transitions, where present, are clunky enough to interrupt rather than extend your movement options. | Crossing ground feels like a delay between fights more than part of one — and hopping in or out of a vehicle doesn't help. |
| 1 | Movement is restricted to walk, sprint, and crouch with no additional options. Terrain has no tactical value beyond cover. | There's nothing to get good at here besides aiming. |

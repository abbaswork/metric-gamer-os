# Metric: Movement
**Genre:** Shooters
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = movement matches the Titanfall 2 standard, with multiple maneuvers that chain together and preserve momentum, turning traversal itself into constant skill expression, 1 = movement is limited to walk/sprint/crouch with no mechanical depth

## Why This Matters

Movement is the layer underneath every other shooter mechanic — it determines how players close distance, break line of sight, and approach a fight before gunplay even comes into it. The community's interest in "gameplay around movement" shows up as a recurring factor even in shooters not built around movement tech specifically, which suggests it's evaluated as a baseline expectation rather than a bonus feature. Sliding, mantling, prone positioning, and vehicle-to-foot transitions are the concrete mechanics players point to when a shooter's movement feels like it adds options rather than just covering distance.

Feel and synergy with the rest of the game are necessary for a good score, but the site owner's own standard sets a specific, deliberately hard ceiling on top of that: a full 5 is reserved for movement that matches Titanfall 2's depth specifically — wall-running, double-jumping, sliding, and grappling as maneuvers that chain into each other with momentum carrying from one into the next, rather than as a list of separate abilities. This isn't a casual comparison; critics and players alike describe Titanfall 2's movement as still unbeaten years later for its speed, versatility, and fluidity, to the point that Call of Duty's own movement looked like "wading through invisible mud" by comparison when Titanfall first introduced it. Until another shooter matches that specific combination of chainable, momentum-preserving maneuvers, this metric holds a 5 to that bar. Movement that feels satisfying and reinforces the rest of the game's identity without reaching that depth — Black Ops's wall-running or Modern Warfare's grounded, weighty movement among them — is a genuinely strong 4, not a 5; the absence of Titanfall-level depth does cap the score, even for movement that is otherwise excellent on its own terms.

Where a shooter has vehicles, they're an extension of this same layer rather than a separate system. A vehicle is only a movement upgrade if it gives players a traversal option that wasn't there on foot — covering ground faster, reaching terrain infantry can't, or enabling a fast rotation between fights — and if getting in and out of it is clean enough that the transition itself doesn't cost you the advantage. A shooter with no vehicles at all isn't penalised for it; infantry-only movement can earn a 5 entirely on its own terms. But a shooter that has vehicles and uses them as a genuine extension of the movement skill layer should score higher than one where vehicles exist but movement effectively ends at the vehicle door, or where transitions are clumsy enough to fight the player instead of extending what they can do.

The failure case is movement that exists only to get from cover to cover with no decisions involved — no momentum, no verticality, no way to use terrain creatively. When movement is purely functional, every approach to a fight looks the same, and positioning stops being something a player can be good at.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Movement matches the Titanfall 2 standard: multiple maneuvers — wall-running, double-jumping, sliding, grappling, or equivalents — chain together with momentum carrying from one into the next, rather than existing as a list of separate abilities. This is an intentionally hard ceiling; movement that feels satisfying and reinforces the rest of the game's identity without reaching this specific depth tops out at a 4. Where the game includes vehicles, transitions between vehicle and foot are seamless and genuinely extend what you can do with terrain; their absence doesn't cap the score on its own. | Getting somewhere well is its own constant, high-skill layer of play — chaining maneuvers together feels as expressive as the shooting itself. Terrain becomes something you use, not just something you cross. |
| 4 | Every movement option feels satisfying to execute on its own and reinforces the rest of the game's identity rather than sitting apart from it, without chaining together with Titanfall-level depth. Skilled movement still creates a real advantage over walking straight at an objective. Vehicle-to-foot transitions, where present, work well in most situations even if a specific terrain type fights them. | Getting somewhere well feels good and feels like part of the same game you're playing, even if it's not the constant skill-expression layer the very best movement shooters offer. |
| 3 | Functional movement that covers the basics — sprinting, crouching, basic vaulting — without much beyond that. It works, but it reads as its own system rather than something that reinforces anything else about the game. Terrain doesn't meaningfully reward creative positioning. Vehicles, where present, cover ground faster but don't add a meaningful skill layer beyond that. | You can get where you need to go, and it doesn't feel bad, but it doesn't feel like it's doing anything for the rest of the game either. |
| 2 | Movement is limited and occasionally fights the player — slow transitions, awkward vaulting, no meaningful prone or slide options — and feels disconnected from the rest of the game's identity. Terrain mostly just slows you down rather than offering options. Vehicle-to-foot transitions, where present, are clunky enough to interrupt rather than extend your movement options. | Crossing ground feels like a delay between fights more than part of one — and hopping in or out of a vehicle doesn't help. |
| 1 | Movement is restricted to walk, sprint, and crouch with no additional options and nothing satisfying about any of it. Terrain has no tactical value beyond cover. | There's nothing to get good at here besides aiming, and nothing about moving through the world feels good on its own. |

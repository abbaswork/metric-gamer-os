# Metric: Level Design
**Genre:** Shooters (Single Player Campaign)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = combat spaces that reward positioning and create memorable encounters, 1 = interchangeable corridors with no tactical dimension

## Why This Matters

Level design in a shooter determines whether the combat system gets to show what it can do. Good encounter spaces give the player positional options. Poor level design puts the player in corridors where none of those decisions exist. FEAR is the most-cited community example of this failure: widely praised for its AI and gunplay, criticised for confining almost all of that gunplay to offices and corridors that asked nothing tactical from the player. Doom Eternal is the positive reference. Each arena is specifically built to enable the game's movement and combat loop, with ledges for vertical play, glory kill opportunities built into the space, and enough room for the combat to breathe.

Level design is distinct from campaign structure. Campaign structure is about how missions are sequenced and paced at a macro level. Level design is about the quality of the individual spaces within those missions.

## Sources

- **gamerant.com: "10 FPS Masterpieces Defined By Gunplay And Level Design"** — Half-Life 2 cited for level variety across urban environments, coastal highways, and Ravenholm; DUSK praised for "incredible level design and excellent mixing with level gimmicks" (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **whatculture.com: "10 Worst FPS Campaigns Of All Time"** — "repetitive level design" identified as a primary failure mode independent of other qualities; FEAR cited as a specific case of good mechanics undermined by poor encounter spaces (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **r/Doom and r/FPS** (training data) — Doom Eternal's arena design is cited as the benchmark for SP shooter level design; the specific praise is about how spaces enable the mechanics rather than just containing them
- **r/patientgamers** (training data) — level design discussions in SP shooter threads use "every room looks the same" as the shorthand for poor design

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Each combat space has its own shape and offers genuine positional choices. Cover creates angles worth contesting, elevation changes are built into arenas, and the geometry rewards knowing where to be. Environments vary across the campaign. Individual rooms have distinct layouts that change how fights play out. | You'll approach different spaces differently. The level is doing work alongside you. |
| 4 | Solid encounter spaces with meaningful cover and positioning across most of the campaign. A few areas are more linear than they should be. The majority of combat happens in spaces that reward thinking about where you stand. | Most fights give you room to work. The occasional corridor section is the exception. |
| 3 | Functional spaces that don't ask much of you tactically. Cover is present without creating interesting decisions. Encounters happen in rooms with generic layouts that don't change what's possible. | You'll play through without fighting the level. It's just there. |
| 2 | Most combat takes place in environments without positional options. Corridors and narrow rooms are the default. Cover exists without enabling flanking or vertical play. | You'll play every fight the same way because the level doesn't offer anything else. |
| 1 | Every combat space is interchangeable. The level design provides no information about how to approach a fight and no variation that changes the answer. | The environment is wallpaper. You fight the same fight in a different-coloured room for the entire campaign. |

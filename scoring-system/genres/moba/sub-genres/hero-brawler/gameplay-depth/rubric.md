# Metric: Gameplay Depth
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Hero Brawler
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = meaningful strategic depth that rewards learning without requiring the mastery investment of a classic MOBA, 1 = so shallow that skill stops mattering within the first few sessions

## Why This Matters

Hero brawlers deliberately trade classic MOBA depth for accessibility — Naavik characterises Brawl Stars as offering "less depth than progression-based or strategy games" as a design choice, not a failure. Heroes of the Storm removes last-hitting and itemisation to keep "the action on the battlefield." This is the sub-genre's deal: lower entry friction in exchange for a more bounded skill ceiling. The question isn't whether hero brawlers match Dota 2's depth — they don't and aren't trying to. The question is whether the depth that exists is interesting enough to sustain engagement past the first few sessions.

The depth in hero brawlers comes from different sources than classic MOBAs: mode-specific strategy (which map positions matter in Gem Grab vs Showdown), hero/brawler counters and synergies, map and wall usage (Brawl Stars' terrain cover), talent or gadget builds (HotS talent trees, Pokemon Unite held item configuration), and team objective coordination. Heroes of the Storm specifically emphasises "map objectives — about taking over the battleground and engaging with those objectives, and it's very much about playing with your team and team leveling." A hero brawler that doesn't develop this kind of accessible depth will feel exhausted within a week of play.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Meaningful strategic depth that takes weeks to engage and months to master within the sub-genre's accessible framework. Team objective coordination, brawler/hero counter-picking, mode-specific strategy, and terrain usage all reward learning. Skilled play is clearly visible — a coordinated experienced group performs markedly better than a group of beginners on the same characters. The skill ceiling is appropriate to the sub-genre: lower than classic MOBAs but high enough that there's always something to improve. | You can see what better players are doing differently. The game keeps rewarding learning across weeks of play. |
| 4 | Clear depth in team strategy and hero/brawler matchups. Mode-specific positioning and objective priority are learnable and impactful. Skilled play is visibly better. The skill ceiling is reached in weeks rather than months for dedicated players, but the journey there is consistently rewarding. | The game rewards learning. You improve noticeably across sessions and can feel the gap between good and great play. |
| 3 | Basic strategic depth but it plateaus relatively quickly. Mode-specific strategy exists but becomes predictable after a few hours per mode. Hero/brawler counters are simple rather than nuanced. Coordinated play is better than uncoordinated play but the margin isn't large enough to sustain long-term improvement motivation. | Fun for the first few weeks. Strategic improvement exists but the ceiling isn't far away. |
| 2 | Minimal strategic depth beyond learning each hero's basic ability rotation. Mode strategy is thin or so simple it's learned in the first session. Skill primarily expresses as reaction time and aim rather than game-knowledge decisions. Coordinated play barely outperforms random team assembly. | You figure out the game in the first few hours. After that, you're just executing the same patterns faster. |
| 1 | No meaningful strategic layer. Outcomes are determined by character pick and luck rather than decision-making. Learning the game provides no durable advantage beyond the first few matches. | Depth is absent. Winning and losing are interchangeable experiences after the tutorial. |

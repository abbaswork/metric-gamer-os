# Metric: Monetisation Model
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Hero Brawler
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = cosmetic-only spending with accessible character unlocks and no gameplay advantage behind a paywall, 1 = pay-to-win progression or character access so restricted it requires payment for competitive viability

## Why This Matters

Hero brawlers often have more complex monetisation ecosystems than classic MOBAs, and Brawl Stars specifically has generated significant community discussion about its F2P fairness — "brawl stars free" at +30% and "code brawl stars" at +30% in the research shows players actively seeking free access routes, which signals sensitivity to monetisation barriers. Brawl Stars has historically operated across multiple currencies (Gold, Gems, Credits), brawler progression tracks, and seasonal battle passes simultaneously — a level of monetisation layering that requires careful evaluation against the fairness line.

The fairness line in hero brawlers is the same as in classic MOBAs: cosmetic-only spending is acceptable, anything affecting in-match outcomes or restricting the competitive character pool in ways that create structural disadvantage crosses it. Hero brawlers typically lock characters behind unlock systems — HotS heroes via Gold/Gems, Brawl Stars brawlers via progression boxes or direct purchase, Pokemon Unite Pokémon via Aeos Coins or Gems. The speed and cost of unlocking a competitive roster determines whether the free experience is genuinely playable or functionally gatekept. Progression boosters that accelerate unlock speed at a paid price raise the same concern as direct purchases — they create a two-tier unlock experience even without selling raw power.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Nothing affecting gameplay is behind a paywall. All brawlers/heroes are earnable through play at a pace where a new player has a competitively viable roster within weeks of regular sessions. The progression system is clear and consistent — you always know what you're working toward and how long it will take. Cosmetics are purchasable at prices the community broadly considers fair, with a meaningful free cosmetic track. | You never feel like spending is the path to winning. The free experience is complete. |
| 4 | Cosmetic-only spending with a slightly slower free unlock pace. A new player needs a few extra weeks before they have a competitive roster, but the path is clear and achievable without payment. Most cosmetics are reasonably priced. Battle pass content is cosmetic and doesn't create significant FOMO. | Fairly free. The grind exists but it's not exploitative. Spending is for preference, not competitive access. |
| 3 | Cosmetic-only but unlock pace is slow enough to create real friction for free players. A full competitive roster requires either significant time or moderate spending. Battle pass progression creates noticeable FOMO pressure. Multiple overlapping currencies make it hard to understand the real cost of unlocks. | The free path works but it's slow and occasionally confusing. Spending feels like it accelerates something that should be accessible by default. |
| 2 | Unlock pace for free players is slow enough to create meaningful competitive disadvantage. New players may feel effectively compelled to spend to have a viable character pool. Progression boosters, energy systems, or limited-time unlocks create consistent spend pressure even on cosmetically-only content. | The free experience feels deliberately limited. Spending becomes the obvious path to keeping up. |
| 1 | Pay-to-win elements present — direct stat advantages, exclusive brawler abilities, or progression that directly affects in-match outcomes available for purchase. Or the free character pool is so small that competitive play in the game's primary modes is inaccessible without payment. | The game punishes non-payers in-match. Spending isn't optional for competitive participation. |

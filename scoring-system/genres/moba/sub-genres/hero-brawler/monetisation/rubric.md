# Metric: Monetisation Model
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Hero Brawler
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = cosmetic-only spending with accessible character unlocks and no gameplay advantage behind a paywall, 1 = pay-to-win progression or character access so restricted it requires payment for competitive viability

## Why This Matters

Hero brawlers often have more complex monetisation ecosystems than classic MOBAs, and Brawl Stars is the starkest example. The research data tells the story directly: "brawl stars free" at +30% and "code brawl stars" at +30% show players actively seeking free access routes, which signals sensitivity to monetisation barriers. "brawl stars shop" at +60% shows the in-game shop is front-of-mind for the player base. Brawl Stars has historically operated across multiple currencies, brawler progression tracks, and seasonal battle passes simultaneously — a level of layering that requires careful evaluation against the fairness line.

The fairness line is the same as everywhere else in the genre: cosmetic-only spending is acceptable, anything affecting in-match outcomes or restricting the competitive character pool to create structural disadvantage crosses it. Naavik notes Brawl Stars achieves "impressive long-term retention" by scaling to a wide audience with revenue from "a much wider bucket of players compared to hardcore MOBAs" — broad monetisation rather than high-spender dependence, which is the sustainable F2P model. The games that get this wrong create a two-tier experience even without selling raw power: slow unlock pace, progression boosters, and energy systems all create spend pressure on players who simply want a fair competitive pool.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Nothing affecting gameplay is behind a paywall. All brawlers/heroes are earnable through play at a pace where a new player has a competitively viable roster within weeks of regular sessions. The progression system is clear and consistent — you always know what you're working toward and how long it will take. Cosmetics are purchasable at prices the community broadly considers fair, with a meaningful free cosmetic track. | You never feel like spending is the path to winning. The free experience is complete. |
| 4 | Cosmetic-only spending with a slightly slower free unlock pace. A new player needs a few extra weeks before they have a competitive roster, but the path is clear and achievable without payment. Most cosmetics are reasonably priced. Battle pass content is cosmetic and does not create significant FOMO. | Fairly free. The grind exists but it's not exploitative. Spending is for preference, not competitive access. |
| 3 | Cosmetic-only but unlock pace is slow enough to create real friction for free players. A full competitive roster requires either significant time or moderate spending. Battle pass progression creates noticeable FOMO pressure. Multiple overlapping currencies make it hard to understand the real cost of unlocks. | The free path works but it's slow and occasionally confusing. Spending feels like it accelerates something that should be accessible by default. |
| 2 | Unlock pace for free players is slow enough to create meaningful competitive disadvantage. New players may feel effectively compelled to spend to have a viable character pool. Progression boosters, energy systems, or limited-time unlocks create consistent spend pressure even on cosmetically-only content. | The free experience feels deliberately limited. Spending becomes the obvious path to keeping up. |
| 1 | Pay-to-win elements present — direct stat advantages, exclusive brawler abilities, or progression that directly affects in-match outcomes available for purchase. Or the free character pool is so small that competitive play in the game's primary modes is inaccessible without payment. | The game punishes non-payers in-match. Spending is not optional for competitive participation. |

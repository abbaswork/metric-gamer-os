# Metric: Roster Depth
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Hero Brawler
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = roster large and varied enough that each mode produces different optimal picks and player identity is tied to specific brawlers/heroes, 1 = thin roster where the same characters dominate every mode and playstyle options collapse

## Why This Matters

In hero brawlers, the roster serves a different function than in classic MOBAs. There is no draft phase and no fixed role structure. Each brawler or hero needs to feel distinctly valuable across the game's range of modes and maps. A close-range brawler that dominates in tight corridor maps might be weak on open-arena maps. A support character that excels in objective-based modes becomes less relevant in deathmatch formats. Roster depth in hero brawlers is not measured by role coverage but by how many distinct strategic niches each character fills across the mode ecosystem.

Naavik's analysis of Brawl Stars makes this connection directly: "the radically different Events expedite the use of various Brawlers because different character types excel in distinct modes." Roster depth and mode variety are co-dependent — one without the other produces a shallow experience. Naavik also identifies "Brawler diversification" as a core retention mechanism: character-specific trophy tracks incentivize players to explore the full roster rather than one-tricking, which extends the game's long-term hold.

Heroes of the Storm leverages 30+ years of Blizzard IP for character recognition. Brawl Stars has 80+ brawlers with distinct personalities. In both cases, the breadth and distinctiveness of the roster is what makes mode rotation feel like a different game each time rather than a skin change.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Large roster of brawlers/heroes with distinct archetypes — close-range, long-range, support, tank, assassin — each well-suited to different modes and maps. The optimal pick changes meaningfully by mode: what dominates in an objective mode is not what dominates in a deathmatch. New additions expand underrepresented playstyles rather than duplicating existing ones. Character personality and visual identity are strong enough to build player attachment. Roster exploration is incentivised and rewarded. | Every mode feels like it has characters built for it. Roster mastery is a genuine long-term pursuit. |
| 4 | Strong roster variety with good mode-based differentiation. Most playstyle archetypes are well-represented. Some modes show a dominant tier of characters but alternatives exist. New additions are generally distinct. Character identity is strong for the core roster even if newer additions are thinner in personality. | Good variety. You'll find favourites but there's genuine reason to explore the rest. |
| 3 | Adequate roster but limited mode-based differentiation. The same 8-10 characters are strong across most modes. Playstyle variety exists but a few archetypes have significantly deeper options than others. New additions do not reliably expand the strategic space. | The roster works but you'll find yourself returning to the same picks. Mode-specific character selection becomes less meaningful quickly. |
| 2 | Small roster with limited playstyle variety. A narrow set of characters performs consistently well across all modes, and the rest feel like underpowered alternatives rather than different strategic tools. New character additions are infrequent or do not address gaps. | The roster does not deliver variety. The best 5-6 characters cover most situations and the rest see minimal play. |
| 1 | Minimal roster that collapses to 2-3 dominant characters across every mode. No meaningful playstyle differentiation. Character identity is shallow. Roster breadth does not function as a strategic or collection system. | Character choice is cosmetic. The same picks win regardless of mode or map. |

# Metric: Roster Depth
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Hero Brawler
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = roster large and varied enough that each mode produces different optimal picks and player identity is tied to specific brawlers/heroes, 1 = thin roster where the same characters dominate every mode and playstyle options collapse

## Why This Matters

In hero brawlers, the roster serves a different function than in classic MOBAs. There is no draft phase and no fixed role structure — instead, each brawler or hero needs to feel distinctly valuable across the game's range of modes and maps. A close-range brawler that dominates in tight corridor maps might be weak on open-arena maps. A support character that excels in objective-based modes becomes less relevant in deathmatch formats. Roster depth in hero brawlers isn't measured by role coverage but by how many distinct strategic niches each character fills across the mode ecosystem.

The research identifies character appeal as a universal driver: "I get to be cool, cute characters, that have very compelling stories." In hero brawlers this combines with a collection-and-mastery loop: Naavik's analysis of Brawl Stars found that "Brawler diversification" — where character-specific trophy tracks incentivize players to explore the full roster rather than one-tricking — is a core retention mechanism. Heroes of the Storm leverages 30+ years of Blizzard IP (Warcraft, StarCraft, Overwatch, Diablo) for immediate character recognition across a wide audience. A shallow roster in this sub-genre is especially costly: without deep itemisation to differentiate games between the same two champions, the roster IS the variety engine.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Large roster of brawlers/heroes with distinct archetypes — close-range, long-range, support, tank, assassin — each well-suited to different modes and maps. The optimal pick changes meaningfully by mode: what dominates in an objective mode is not what dominates in a deathmatch. New additions expand underrepresented playstyles rather than duplicating existing ones. Character personality and visual identity are strong enough to build player attachment. Roster exploration is incentivised and rewarded. | Every mode feels like it has characters built for it. Roster mastery is a genuine long-term pursuit. |
| 4 | Strong roster variety with good mode-based differentiation. Most playstyle archetypes are well-represented. Some modes show a dominant tier of characters but alternatives exist. New additions are generally distinct. Character identity is strong for the core roster even if newer additions are thinner in personality. | Good variety. You'll find favourites but there's genuine reason to explore the rest. |
| 3 | Adequate roster but limited mode-based differentiation — the same 8–10 characters are strong across most modes. Playstyle variety exists but a few archetypes have significantly deeper options than others. New additions don't reliably expand the strategic space. | The roster works but you'll find yourself returning to the same picks. Mode-specific character selection becomes less meaningful quickly. |
| 2 | Small roster with limited playstyle variety. A narrow set of characters performs consistently well across all modes, and the rest feel like underpowered alternatives rather than different strategic tools. New character additions are infrequent or don't address gaps. | The roster doesn't deliver variety. The best 5–6 characters cover most situations and the rest see minimal play. |
| 1 | Minimal roster that collapses to 2–3 dominant characters across every mode. No meaningful playstyle differentiation. Character identity is shallow. Roster breadth doesn't function as a strategic or collection system. | Character choice is cosmetic. The same picks win regardless of mode or map. |

# Metric: Teamplay & Social
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Hero Brawler
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = social infrastructure that makes friend groups the natural play unit with frictionless group formation and communication, 1 = systems that make coordinated group play harder than solo

## Why This Matters

Hero brawlers have a structural social advantage over classic MOBAs: shorter matches (15–20 minutes) make the ask to friends lower. "Come play one game" is a 15-minute commitment rather than a 45-minute one — which means the friend-recruitment mechanic the research identifies ("group game: friends will try to get other friends to play together") is more accessible in this sub-genre. The lower session length also means the group social loop iterates faster — more games per hour, more chances for a memorable moment, more natural opportunities for a friend group to extend a session.

The research identifies this virality as the genre's primary growth mechanism: "friends will try to get other friends to play together with them, so a bit of virality going on there." Hero brawlers also often target a broader age range than classic MOBAs — Brawl Stars draws a younger audience alongside its core competitive players — which means the social systems need to work clearly and with low friction, not assume MOBA-veteran familiarity with party formation. Club/guild systems (Brawl Stars Clubs, HotS groups), friend challenges, and club leagues are the social infrastructure layer that turns individual players into retained communities.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Forming and playing with a group of friends is seamless — invitations, party queues, and club/guild membership work without friction. The shorter match format makes spontaneous group sessions easy to start and extend. Communication tools (ping system, quick-chat, club chat) cover tactical and social needs without requiring external apps. Club or team systems create a persistent community layer beyond individual match sessions. Post-match the group stays together by default. | Playing with friends is the natural mode. The game keeps your group together and creates reasons to come back together. |
| 4 | Party play works well with minor friction. Group queues are fast. Communication tools cover basic tactical needs. Club or community systems exist and are functional even if not deeply developed. Friend-adding is easy. | Playing with friends works. The systems support it even if they weren't explicitly built to optimise it. |
| 3 | Party systems are functional but create friction — group queues are noticeably slower, party sizes are limited, or communication tools are minimal enough that the group needs external apps to coordinate. Club or community systems exist but are thin or rarely used organically. | You can play with friends but the game treats solo and group play as equivalent rather than optimising for groups. |
| 2 | Significant party friction — slow group queues, hard party size caps, or poor communication tools make the coordinated group experience measurably worse than solo. Community systems are absent or non-functional. The game doesn't support the friend-group session loop that drives the sub-genre's appeal. | Playing with friends requires too many workarounds. The game doesn't support groups. |
| 1 | No meaningful social systems. Every session begins as strangers. No party functionality, no club or community layer, no communication tools beyond basic text chat. The friend-group session loop that defines the sub-genre's appeal is entirely absent. | Solo is the only real mode. Group play is entirely external to the game. |

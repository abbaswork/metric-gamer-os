# Metric: Teamplay & Social
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Hero Brawler
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = social infrastructure that makes friend groups the natural play unit with frictionless group formation and communication, 1 = systems that make coordinated group play harder than solo

## Why This Matters

Hero brawlers have a structural social advantage over classic MOBAs: shorter matches make the ask to friends lower. "Come play one game" is a 15-minute commitment rather than a 45-minute one. The friend-recruitment mechanism the research identifies ("group game: friends will try to get other friends to play together with them, so a bit of virality going on there") is more accessible in this sub-genre because the session length removes the biggest friction point in the recruitment ask.

QUT research on MOBA player behaviour confirms how important this social layer is: the study found that "the ability to play with friends is salient when beginning play and during periods of engagement" and that social factors were responsible for 22.99% of players returning to MOBA play. The mechanism works through friend groups, and shorter session lengths make those groups easier to keep active.

Hero brawlers also often target a broader age range than classic MOBAs — Brawl Stars draws a younger audience alongside its competitive players — which means social systems need to work clearly and with low friction. Club and guild systems (Brawl Stars Clubs, HotS group finder) are the layer that turns individual players into retained communities. When this infrastructure is missing or underdeveloped, the community that forms around the game is smaller and less sticky than it should be.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Forming and playing with a group of friends is seamless — invitations, party queues, and club/guild membership work without friction. The shorter match format makes spontaneous group sessions easy to start and extend. Communication tools (ping system, quick-chat, club chat) cover tactical and social needs without requiring external apps. Club or team systems create a persistent community layer beyond individual match sessions. Post-match the group stays together by default. | Playing with friends is the natural mode. The game keeps your group together and creates reasons to come back together. |
| 4 | Party play works well with minor friction. Group queues are fast. Communication tools cover basic tactical needs. Club or community systems exist and are functional even if not deeply developed. Friend-adding is easy. | Playing with friends works. The systems support it even if they were not explicitly built to optimise it. |
| 3 | Party systems are functional but create friction. Group queues are noticeably slower, party sizes are limited, or communication tools are minimal enough that the group needs external apps to coordinate. Club or community systems exist but are thin or rarely used organically. | You can play with friends but the game treats solo and group play as equivalent rather than optimising for groups. |
| 2 | Significant party friction. Slow group queues, hard party size caps, or poor communication tools make the coordinated group experience measurably worse than solo. Community systems are absent or non-functional. The game does not support the friend-group session loop that drives the sub-genre's appeal. | Playing with friends requires too many workarounds. The game does not support groups. |
| 1 | No meaningful social systems. Every session begins as strangers. No party functionality, no club or community layer, no communication tools beyond basic text chat. The friend-group session loop that defines the sub-genre's appeal is entirely absent. | Solo is the only real mode. Group play is entirely external to the game. |

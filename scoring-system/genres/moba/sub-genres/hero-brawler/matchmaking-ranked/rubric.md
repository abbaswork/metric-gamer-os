# Metric: Matchmaking & Ranked Quality
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Hero Brawler
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = fast, accurate matchmaking that creates competitive games appropriate to the sub-genre's pace, 1 = wildly inconsistent skill gaps or rank systems that don't reflect skill

## Why This Matters

Hero brawlers have different matchmaking dynamics than classic MOBAs. Shorter matches (15–20 minutes vs 40–50 for classic) mean the quality of each individual game is more important — a single badly matched game is a proportionally larger waste of time when sessions are short. Smaller team sizes in many modes (3v3 in Brawl Stars, 5v5 in HotS) change the structural impact of mismatched players: in a 3v3, one severely outmatched player is a third of the team rather than a fifth.

Hero brawlers often use per-character or per-mode trophy systems rather than a single ladder — Brawl Stars has individual trophy counts per brawler, which means matchmaking must either blend or separate these progressions. This creates situations where a player who is highly skilled on one character gets mismatched when trying a new one, or where ranked and casual modes attract very different populations. The research confirms players care about competitive standing: "brawl stars championship" at +20%, "pokemon unite rank" at +40%, "how to see leaderboard in mobile legends" at +600%.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Queues are near-instant at most skill levels. Games feel competitive — opponents are close enough in skill that outcomes depend on play rather than base advantage. The rank or trophy system accurately reflects improvement over a sample of sessions. Per-mode or per-character rank separation is handled in a way that doesn't create obvious mismatches when trying new characters. New players are calibrated appropriately, not thrown into experienced lobbies immediately. | Ranked feels like a practice tool. You get matches fast, and the matches teach you something. |
| 4 | Fast queues with generally accurate skill-based matching. Occasional outlier games — a clear mismatch or a rank bracket that doesn't feel accurate — but these are exceptions. The trophy or rank system is understandable. Playing a new character creates a brief calibration period that resolves within a few games. | Mostly fair and fast. The rare bad game doesn't define the experience. |
| 3 | Queues are acceptable but matchmaking accuracy is inconsistent. Skill gaps appear in a noticeable minority of games. The trophy or rank system sometimes feels disconnected from actual performance. Trying a new character in ranked modes creates a longer mismatch period than feels reasonable. | Functional but frustrating at times. You can still climb but the system occasionally works against you. |
| 2 | Slow queues or frequent skill mismatches. The ranking system shows signs of inflation, smurfing, or poor calibration — rank doesn't reliably reflect who will win. Playing new characters in ranked creates long periods of obvious mismatches. The competitive experience is undermined more by matchmaking than by skill differences. | Ranked feels unreliable. Outcomes are too often determined by who got lucky in the lobby, not by who played better. |
| 1 | Broken or absent matchmaking. Queues are long or skill gaps in lobbies are so large that competitive play is impossible. The rank or trophy system doesn't track skill. The game cannot deliver a consistent competitive experience. | You can't improve from ranked. Games are too uneven to teach anything. |

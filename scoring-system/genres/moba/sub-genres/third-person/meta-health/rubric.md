# Metric: Meta Health
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = broad god/hero viability across the roster with a meta that evolves through regular patches, 1 = solved meta where a handful of picks dominate every game

## Why This Matters

Third-person MOBAs face a meta health challenge amplified by their smaller player bases compared to classic titles. When the player count is lower, imbalanced gods or characters appear in a higher proportion of games — there are simply fewer players spreading play across the full roster. A character that is 5% overtuned in LoL's 150+ million player pool might produce visible frustration; in Smite's or Deadlock's smaller pools that same character can feel inescapable. The research confirms tier list tracking is active across all MOBAs: "smite builds" and "smite patch notes" appear in the Google Trends data, confirming Smite players track meta state as actively as LoL players.

Patch cadence is the primary meta health mechanism in third-person MOBAs — the community is smaller but often more vocal and analytically engaged, which means imbalanced states become community talking points faster. "dota 2 patch (+50%)" and "smite patch notes (+20%)" in the research confirm patch-following as core player behaviour. A game that patches too slowly allows dominant picks to define seasons; too aggressively creates instability that erodes long-term character mastery investment.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Broad god/hero viability across the roster. No small cluster of picks is so dominant it appears in the majority of games. The meta shifts meaningfully with each patch cycle. Playing a god you enjoy is a preference choice, not a competitive sacrifice, at most skill levels. Ban/pick phases create genuine choice rather than being consumed by a fixed must-ban list. | Your preferred pick is viable without requiring a meta justification. The roster feels alive. |
| 4 | Healthy meta with occasional patches that create temporary balance gaps. Most gods are viable. An S-tier exists but playing outside it is a minor disadvantage rather than a severe one. Patches address dominant states reasonably quickly. | Meta is generally fair. Brief post-patch imbalances resolve without defining the season. |
| 3 | A clear tier above the rest across most skill levels. Patches address imbalance but with a lag — broken states persist long enough to define 2–3 week periods of play. At higher skill levels, playing off-meta picks is a meaningful competitive sacrifice. | The meta influences picks more than preference does at competitive levels. Playing your favourite is viable but costs you. |
| 2 | Narrow meta with a small set of dominant gods and builds that appear in most games at competitive ranks. The smaller player base amplifies the visibility of imbalanced picks — you see the same overpowered options every session. Patches are slow to address dominant states. | The same gods appear every game. Playing outside the tier isn't viable at competitive levels. |
| 1 | Solved meta where a handful of picks dominate all ranks. Balance updates are absent or consistently fail to shift the dominant tier. The smaller player base means every game feels like an exercise in mirror-matching the same broken picks. | Character choice is determined by the tier list. The roster's breadth is irrelevant to competitive play. |

# Metric: Aiming & Skill Expression
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = manual aiming adds a meaningful, rewarding mechanical skill layer on top of MOBA strategy, 1 = aiming is either trivialised or so punishing that the third-person perspective doesn't create the intended skill expression

## Why This Matters

Aiming is the defining mechanical distinction of third-person MOBAs — the skill layer absent from every other MOBA sub-genre. In top-down MOBAs, abilities are directed by clicking or pointing with the cursor at relatively close range; in third-person MOBAs, players aim their camera to direct attacks and abilities in real time, introducing a mechanical competency borrowed from shooter games. The sub-genre occupies a spectrum: at one end, Deadlock, where "your aim and gunplay skill is just as if not more important than your positioning"; at the other, Smite, which "focuses less on gun play and more on skills and MOBA style strategy."

The quality of the aiming system determines whether the third-person perspective delivers the distinct competitive experience it promises. The best execution — somewhere between the extremes — is where aiming skill creates a meaningful dimension of expression that rewards practice, but doesn't so completely dominate outcomes that MOBA strategy is irrelevant. Ability hitboxes need to be generous enough that near-misses don't feel completely unpunished, but tight enough that hitting difficult shots feels earned. The gap between a skilled aimer and an unskilled one should be large enough to matter competitively, but not so large that it trivialises game knowledge differences. In third-person MOBAs that get this balance right, a well-aimed difficult play feels fundamentally different from anything the top-down sub-genres can deliver.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Aiming skill creates a meaningful, rewarding dimension of competitive expression. Landing difficult shots or hitting complex ability sequences requires genuine practice and feels earned when executed. The gap between a skilled aimer and an unskilled one is large enough to matter competitively but doesn't trivialise MOBA strategy — game knowledge and team coordination are still necessary to win. Hitboxes are tuned so near-misses feel close rather than unfair. The aiming layer enables plays impossible in top-down MOBAs. | Landing a difficult shot or ability chain feels different from anything a top-down MOBA can deliver. Aiming is the reason you chose this sub-genre. |
| 4 | Aiming skill is meaningful and rewarding. The gap between skilled and unskilled aimers is visible and affects outcomes. Hitboxes feel fair — hits register when expected, misses feel like misses rather than bugs. MOBA strategy still matters alongside aiming. A few abilities have hitboxes that feel over or under-tuned, but the overall aiming system delivers on its promise. | Aiming is a real skill dimension. Getting better at it noticeably improves your performance. |
| 3 | Aiming exists as a mechanic but doesn't create significant competitive differentiation. Hitboxes are generous enough that aiming precision is a minor factor — most players connect most of the time regardless of aim skill. The third-person perspective feels like a presentation choice more than a mechanical one. MOBA strategy dominates outcomes. | Aiming is present but not a reason to choose this sub-genre. The perspective is cosmetic rather than mechanically meaningful. |
| 2 | Aiming is frustrating rather than rewarding. Hitboxes are inconsistent — hits don't register reliably, or misses register as hits, creating a disconnect between player input and game outcome. The aiming layer creates confusion rather than skill expression. Or conversely: auto-lock mechanics so aggressive that aiming skill is completely irrelevant. | Aiming doesn't work as a skill dimension. It either can't be engaged with meaningfully or fails to register player input correctly. |
| 1 | Aiming is either completely trivialised (auto-aim makes it irrelevant) or so broken that it adds frustration with no skill expression. The defining mechanical feature of the sub-genre is absent or non-functional. | The sub-genre's unique mechanic doesn't exist here. This is a MOBA that happens to use a third-person camera without committing to what that perspective offers. |

# Metric: Matchmaking & Ranked Quality
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = accurate, reasonably fast matchmaking that creates competitive games despite smaller player pools, 1 = long queues or skill gaps so large that competitive play is impossible

## Why This Matters

Matchmaking quality is a particular pressure point for third-person MOBAs because the player bases of most titles in this sub-genre are significantly smaller than top-tier classic MOBAs. Smite, Deadlock, and Supervive compete with League of Legends and Dota 2 for the same MOBA-interested player base, and smaller numbers mean the matchmaking system has less data to work with and fewer options at each skill tier. The research shows players actively track this: "smite 2 servers" at +30% is a direct signal that server quality and queue performance are monitored.

The trade-off a smaller player base forces is simple: wait longer for an accurate match, or accept a larger skill gap for a faster one. There is no clean answer, and the community around third-person MOBAs is aware of this. Players comparing Smite and Deadlock describe them as distinct enough to both be worth playing: "League over Dota... why not both? they are all different games with unique identity." But that goodwill only persists if ranked play is functional enough to make the investment feel worthwhile. A ranked mode that consistently produces mismatched games due to a thin pool will accelerate player attrition rather than slowing it.

The research identifies ranked progression as a core MOBA retention driver: "it feels really rewarding to carry your teammates to victory, to build up your skills as a player, and to advance in the ranked system." Third-person MOBAs need to deliver on this despite their smaller populations.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Queue times are acceptable at most skill levels given the player base size. Games feel competitive — skill gaps in lobbies are narrow enough that outcomes depend on play quality. Rank progression tracks improvement accurately over a session of games. The system handles smaller player pools at high ranks gracefully — queue times extend but match quality is maintained. | Ranked feels like a practice tool even in a smaller game. The matchmaking makes the smaller pool feel like a large one. |
| 4 | Generally fair matchmaking with queue times appropriate to the game's scale. Occasional outlier games but they are exceptions rather than the norm. Rank progression is understandable. High-rank queues are slower but still resolve. | Mostly fair. The smaller player base creates occasional friction that does not define the experience. |
| 3 | Noticeable skill gaps appear in a meaningful minority of games. Queue times are acceptable at mid-ranks but extend significantly at extremes, sometimes long enough to degrade match quality. Rank does not always feel like an accurate reflection of skill. The smaller player base is visibly a factor. | Functional but the player base limitations are felt. You can still climb but the system sometimes puts you in uncompetitive lobbies. |
| 2 | Frequent skill mismatches or long queues that force large skill gap compromises. High-rank play is significantly degraded by queue constraints. Rank progression feels disconnected from performance. The smaller player base is a consistent problem rather than an occasional limitation. | Ranked is frustrating. The player base limitations mean you spend more time waiting and playing mismatched games than improving. |
| 1 | Broken matchmaking — queue times are long enough to be a deterrent, skill gaps in lobbies are so large that competitive play is impossible, or the player base is too small for the matchmaking system to function. | You cannot play ranked meaningfully. The player base is not large enough to support the feature. |

# Metric: Matchmaking & Ranked Quality
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = accurate, reasonably fast matchmaking that creates competitive games despite smaller player pools, 1 = long queues or skill gaps so large that competitive play is impossible

## Why This Matters

Matchmaking quality is a particular pressure point for third-person MOBAs because the player bases of most titles in this sub-genre are significantly smaller than top-tier classic MOBAs. Smite, Deadlock, and Supervive compete with League of Legends and Dota 2 for the same MOBA-interested player base, and smaller numbers mean the matchmaking system has less data to work with and fewer options at each skill tier. The game is then forced to a harder trade-off: wait longer for an accurate match, or accept a larger skill gap for a faster one. The research shows players actively search "smite 2 servers" (less than 1, +30%) — a direct signal that server quality and queue performance are tracked.

The research identifies ranked progression as a core MOBA retention driver regardless of sub-genre: "it feels really rewarding to carry your teammates to victory, to build up your skills as a player, and to advance in the ranked system." In third-person MOBAs with smaller player bases, maintaining functional ranked at high skill levels is a particular challenge — queues at extreme ranks can extend to the point where the game relaxes match quality constraints significantly, creating lobbies that feel fundamentally uncompetitive.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Queue times are acceptable at most skill levels given the player base size. Games feel competitive — skill gaps in lobbies are narrow enough that outcomes depend on play quality and the mechanical/strategic layer, not on lobby mismatch. Rank progression tracks improvement accurately over a session of games. The system handles smaller player pools at high ranks gracefully — queue times extend but match quality is maintained. | Ranked feels like a practice tool even in a smaller game. The matchmaking does the work of making the smaller pool feel like a large one. |
| 4 | Generally fair matchmaking with queue times appropriate to the game's scale. Occasional outlier games — a noticeable skill gap or a rank bracket that doesn't feel accurate — but these are exceptions rather than the norm. Rank progression is understandable. High-rank queues are slower but still resolve. | Mostly fair. The smaller player base creates occasional friction that doesn't define the experience. |
| 3 | Noticeable skill gaps appear in a meaningful minority of games. Queue times are acceptable at mid-ranks but extend significantly at extremes, sometimes long enough to degrade match quality. Rank doesn't always feel like an accurate reflection of skill. The smaller player base is visibly a factor in matchmaking quality. | Functional but the player base limitations are felt. You can still climb but the system sometimes puts you in uncompetitive lobbies. |
| 2 | Frequent skill mismatches or long queues that force large skill gap compromises. High-rank play is significantly degraded by queue constraints. Rank progression feels disconnected from performance. The smaller player base is a consistent problem rather than an occasional limitation. | Ranked is frustrating. The player base limitations mean you spend more time waiting and playing mismatched games than improving. |
| 1 | Broken matchmaking — queue times are long enough to be a deterrent, skill gaps in lobbies are so large that competitive play is impossible, or the player base is too small for the matchmaking system to function at any level. | You can't play ranked meaningfully. The player base isn't large enough to support the feature. |

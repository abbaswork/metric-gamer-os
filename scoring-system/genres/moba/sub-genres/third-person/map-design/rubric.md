# Metric: Map Design
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = map design that leverages the 3D environment with vertical play, cover systems, and objective placement that create distinct strategic options, 1 = flat or uninteresting maps that ignore the third-person perspective's spatial potential

## Why This Matters

Map design in third-person MOBAs must serve two functions that top-down MOBAs don't share equally. The map must function as a competitive MOBA backdrop — lanes, objectives, strategic routing — while also being designed for the third-person perspective's spatial dynamics: cover that matters, terrain height that changes engagement angles, sight line management that rewards positioning knowledge. A well-designed third-person MOBA map isn't just a classic MOBA map viewed from behind; it is a 3D space where vertical positioning and cover usage are part of the strategic vocabulary.

Deadlock's map is specifically praised on this axis: "the map is far more interesting than Smite's. It is very vertical and doesn't feel flat at all." The verticality makes the map function more like a level from an action game than a traditional MOBA arena — routes through the map use height as a strategic dimension. Smite's maps are more traditionally MOBA-structured but adapted for the third-person view through wider lanes that accommodate the less precise directional awareness of a perspective camera. Both approaches can work; the quality is measured by whether the map actively uses the perspective's spatial potential or treats it as a cosmetic presentation choice.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Map design actively leverages the third-person perspective — vertical play is meaningful, cover positions create real strategic choices, and height differences change engagement dynamics visibly. Lane and jungle design supports the MOBA strategy layer without fighting the perspective's spatial demands. Objective placements create consistent strategic conflict moments. The map feels like it was designed for third-person play, not adapted from a top-down layout. | Positioning in 3D space matters. The map rewards learning where to be as much as who to pick. |
| 4 | Strong map design that uses the third-person perspective well in most areas. Some vertical play and cover mechanics create real strategic options. Objectives create regular conflict moments. A few sections of the map are flat or don't leverage the perspective, but the overall design is clearly built for 3D play. | Good map design. The perspective advantages are real and rewarded in most situations. |
| 3 | Functional map design that serves the MOBA genre without actively using the third-person perspective's spatial potential. Lanes and objectives work as expected but height and cover mechanics are minimal or don't create meaningful strategic differentiation. The map plays similarly to how it would from a top-down view. | The map works as a MOBA backdrop but doesn't add anything specific to the third-person experience. |
| 2 | Map design that actively works against the third-person perspective. Sight lines are cluttered by camera position, cover placement creates confusion rather than strategy, or the map layout causes consistent disorientation. The map feels like a top-down MOBA layout dropped under a third-person camera without adaptation. | The map is harder to navigate in third-person than it would be from above. The perspective costs you information without compensating with spatial depth. |
| 1 | Map design ignores the third-person perspective entirely. Flat, undifferentiated terrain with no spatial strategy. Objectives are poorly placed. The map is an arena that happens to be viewed from behind, not a 3D strategic environment. | The map is a backdrop. The perspective's spatial potential is entirely unused. |

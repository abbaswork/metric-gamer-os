# Metric: Teamplay & Social
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = social infrastructure that makes friend groups the natural play unit and retains the community within a smaller player base, 1 = social systems that make group play harder or fail to build community around a game that needs it

## Why This Matters

Third-person MOBAs with smaller player bases are more dependent on social infrastructure than classic MOBA giants. When a game has millions of players, a solo queue player always finds a match; when the player base is smaller, retention of friend groups and communities becomes critical. A player who plays Smite or Deadlock with a consistent friend group is structurally more likely to stay active than a solo player who drifts away when queues get longer. The social layer is what buffers the player count decline cycle: when solo players leave, the friend-group players stay longer because the social commitment outlasts the individual's enthusiasm.

The research identifies social play as a primary driver across all MOBAs: "can play with friends" and "group game: friends will try to get other friends to play together." This is the genre's virality mechanism. In third-person MOBAs, this mechanism is even more important because the games don't have the marketing budgets or player bases to drive organic discovery. Growth primarily runs through friend group recruitment — and that recruitment only happens if the game makes group play easy and rewarding.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Party formation is seamless. The game actively supports and retains friend groups — in-game friend invitations, party queues, and clan or guild systems create a persistent social layer beyond individual match sessions. Communication tools cover tactical and social needs without requiring external apps. Post-game the group stays together. The social infrastructure actively supports the community-building that smaller player base games need to sustain themselves. | Playing with friends is easy and the game rewards it. The social layer is what keeps you coming back even when solo motivation dips. |
| 4 | Party play works well with minor friction. Group queues are reasonably fast. Communication tools cover most tactical needs. Friend-adding and group formation are easy. A community layer (clans, clubs, guilds) exists and functions. | Playing with friends works. The social systems support it without being the game's standout feature. |
| 3 | Party systems are functional but create friction. Group queues are noticeably slower in a smaller player pool. Communication tools are minimal — the group needs external apps for anything beyond basic tactical pings. Community systems exist but are thinly populated. | You can play with friends but the social infrastructure doesn't compensate for the smaller player base. Coordination requires Discord regardless. |
| 2 | Significant party friction in the context of a smaller player pool — slow group queues, limited party size caps, or poor communication tools. The game doesn't build community — players maintain external connections but the game provides no social infrastructure to strengthen them. | Group play works with workarounds but the game doesn't support it. Social retention rests on external tools the game doesn't provide. |
| 1 | No meaningful social systems in a game that needs them most. Solo-only effectively. No community or group layer. In a small player base, the absence of social infrastructure accelerates player count decline — friend groups can't be anchored to the game when the game doesn't support them. | The game has no social layer. Community formation happens entirely outside the game, if at all. |

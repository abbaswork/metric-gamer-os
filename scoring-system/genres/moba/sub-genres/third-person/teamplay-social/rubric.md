# Metric: Teamplay & Social
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = social infrastructure that makes friend groups the natural play unit and retains the community within a smaller player base, 1 = social systems that make group play harder or fail to build community around a game that needs it

## Why This Matters

Third-person MOBAs with smaller player bases are more dependent on social infrastructure than classic MOBA giants. A player who plays Smite or Deadlock with a consistent friend group is structurally more likely to stay active than a solo player who drifts away when queues get longer. The social layer buffers the player count decline cycle: when solo players leave, friend-group players stay longer because the social commitment outlasts the individual's enthusiasm.

The research identifies this mechanism across all MOBAs: "group game: friends will try to get other friends to play together with them, so a bit of virality going on there." The QUT study on MOBA player behaviour confirms this: "the ability to play with friends is salient when beginning play and during periods of engagement," and social factors brought 22.99% of players back to MOBA play after gaps. This mechanism is even more important for third-person titles because they do not have the marketing budgets or player bases to drive organic discovery. Growth primarily runs through friend group recruitment, and that recruitment only happens if the game makes group play easy and rewarding.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Party formation is seamless. The game actively supports and retains friend groups — in-game friend invitations, party queues, and clan or guild systems create a persistent social layer beyond individual match sessions. Communication tools cover tactical and social needs without requiring external apps. Post-game the group stays together. The social infrastructure actively supports the community-building that smaller player base games need to sustain themselves. | Playing with friends is easy and the game rewards it. The social layer is what keeps you coming back even when solo motivation dips. |
| 4 | Party play works well with minor friction. Group queues are reasonably fast. Communication tools cover most tactical needs. Friend-adding and group formation are easy. A community layer (clans, clubs, guilds) exists and functions. | Playing with friends works. The social systems support it without being the game's standout feature. |
| 3 | Party systems are functional but create friction. Group queues are noticeably slower in a smaller player pool. Communication tools are minimal and the group needs external apps for anything beyond basic tactical pings. Community systems exist but are thinly populated. | You can play with friends but the social infrastructure does not compensate for the smaller player base. Coordination requires Discord regardless. |
| 2 | Significant party friction in the context of a smaller player pool. The game does not build community — players maintain external connections but the game provides no infrastructure to strengthen them. | Group play works with workarounds but the game does not support it. Social retention rests on external tools the game does not provide. |
| 1 | No meaningful social systems in a game that needs them most. Solo-only effectively. In a small player base, the absence of social infrastructure accelerates player count decline. | The game has no social layer. Community formation happens entirely outside the game, if at all. |

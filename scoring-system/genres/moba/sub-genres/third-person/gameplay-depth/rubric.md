# Metric: Gameplay Depth
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = multiple learnable mastery layers including 3D spatial awareness and MOBA strategy that sustain improvement over months, 1 = skill ceiling reached quickly with no meaningful progression beyond basic mechanics

## Why This Matters

Third-person MOBAs add a mechanical mastery layer absent from classic and hero-brawler sub-genres: three-dimensional spatial awareness and manual aiming. In Deadlock, "your aim and gunplay skill is just as if not more important than your positioning" — the game demands competence across two distinct skill domains simultaneously. Smite sits at the other end of the spectrum: "focusing less on gun play and more on skills and MOBA style strategy." This spectrum defines the sub-genre's depth range: at one end, the third-person perspective is a presentation layer over traditional MOBA mechanics; at the other, it introduces shooter-level mechanical skill as a core competency.

The strategic depth that classic MOBAs develop through wave management and item builds exists in third-person MOBAs too, but at a different scale — the smaller player bases of most third-person titles mean the community knowledge base is less exhaustively mapped than LoL or Dota 2's, which can make depth discovery feel more organic. The 3D environment also adds a spatial reasoning layer that top-down MOBAs don't have: vertical positioning, cover usage, and three-dimensional ability leading are learnable skills specific to the sub-genre.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Distinct mastery layers that take months to develop simultaneously: mechanical skill (aiming, ability leading, 3D positioning), strategic knowledge (rotation timing, objective priority, team composition), and spatial reasoning (vertical positioning, cover usage, sight lines). Skilled play is visibly different from average play across all these dimensions. The skill ceiling is high enough that a player with 500 hours still has clear things to work on. | You can see multiple ways in which better players outplay you. Improvement has distinct parallel tracks. |
| 4 | Clear depth across mechanical and strategic layers. Most third-person-specific skills are meaningful. A few elements plateau quickly but the core mastery system sustains several months of consistent improvement. High-skill play is noticeably better in multiple dimensions. | The game rewards learning across both the mechanical and strategic axes. You improve visibly over sessions. |
| 3 | Basic depth in both mechanical and strategic layers but both plateau at moderate investment. Three-dimensional spatial awareness adds something over classic top-down MOBAs but the additional depth is modest. Experienced players reach a stable plateau within a few months. | Deeper than a hero brawler, shallower than the deepest classic MOBAs. Good for players who want a skill curve without a multi-year commitment. |
| 2 | Mechanical aiming skill is the primary differentiator but strategic depth is thin. The MOBA layer doesn't add meaningful complexity on top of the shooter mechanics — it's primarily a shooting game with MOBA structure rather than a genuine hybrid. Improvement plateaus quickly for both aim-skilled and strategy-focused players. | The depth of the sub-genre isn't realised. It plays like a simplified shooter with MOBA labels. |
| 1 | Surface-level mechanics in both dimensions. Aiming is trivialised by generous hitboxes or ability auto-lock. Strategic layer is absent. Skill stops differentiating outcomes within the first few hours. | No meaningful depth. Outcomes are determined by character picks, not by player skill. |

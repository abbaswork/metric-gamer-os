# Metric: Roster Depth
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = large, characterful roster where each pick feels mechanically and visually distinct in the 3D environment, 1 = thin roster where picks are interchangeable and character identity is weak

## Why This Matters

Third-person MOBAs use the perspective to build a stronger connection between player and character than top-down games allow: "the third-person perspective creates a much stronger connection to your character than isometric games, as if the closer perspective imparts some sort of psychic connection." Character design in this sub-genre carries extra weight — you see your character from behind for the entire match, which means animation quality, silhouette, and visual personality matter more than in classic MOBAs where characters are thumbnail-sized on screen.

Smite uses this through mythological pantheons: gods from Greek, Norse, Egyptian, Chinese, and dozens of other traditions, each with abilities themed around their mythology. Deadlock uses original character design with distinct visual and mechanical identities. The roster must be deep enough to offer meaningful choice within the sub-genre's specific skill expression axis: in third-person MOBAs, every character's kit interacts with the manual-aiming layer differently — some characters rely on fast, close-range abilities that don't require much aim, others demand precision skillshots at range. Roster depth means having multiple options across this spectrum.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Large roster where each character feels visually and mechanically distinct in the 3D environment. The aiming spectrum is well-represented — close-range, mid-range, and precision-range characters all have multiple viable options. Character personality, animation quality, and visual design justify the time you spend seeing them from behind. New character additions expand underrepresented playstyles. Draft diversity is meaningful — the available pool is large enough that bans create genuine choice rather than formality. | You develop a relationship with your character. The roster is deep enough that different players can express different identities. |
| 4 | Strong roster variety with good representation across different aiming demands and playstyles. Most archetypes have multiple options. Character visual and animation quality is strong for the core roster even if newer additions are thinner. Draft diversity is real. | Good variety. You'll find a main but the roster has enough to explore adjacent styles. |
| 3 | Adequate roster but limited depth in some playstyle categories. The aiming spectrum isn't fully represented — one range category has significantly better options than others. Character visual quality is inconsistent. Draft diversity is functional but experienced players rotate through viable picks quickly. | The roster works but depth thins fast outside the most popular archetypes. |
| 2 | Small roster with limited playstyle variety in the third-person context. Some fundamental aiming archetypes are underrepresented or missing. Character visual and animation quality is inconsistent enough that the third-person perspective doesn't strengthen character connection — it just makes the limitations more visible. | Roster variety is thin. A small set of picks dominates and the rest feel incomplete. |
| 1 | Minimal roster that doesn't support competitive draft diversity. Character visual quality doesn't justify the third-person perspective's intimacy. The same picks dominate every game. | Character selection is cosmetic. The perspective's potential for character connection isn't realised. |

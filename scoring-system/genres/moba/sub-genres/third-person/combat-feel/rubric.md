# Metric: Combat Feel
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Third-Person
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = responsive, physically satisfying combat where the third-person perspective makes every hit, dodge, and ability land feel meaningful, 1 = sluggish or disconnected combat where the perspective adds visual noise without improving satisfaction

## Why This Matters

Third-person MOBAs make a specific promise through the perspective choice: you will feel more connected to the action than you would from above. "The third-person view creates a much stronger connection to your character than isometric games, as if the closer perspective imparts some sort of psychic connection" (Steam community discussion on Smite 2). That promise has to be delivered through combat. When a god's ability lands in Smite, the camera proximity makes the animation quality, impact sound, and hit response immediately visible in a way that a League of Legends ability isn't. When Deadlock's gunplay fires, the weapon handling needs to feel right from the first frame.

The combat feel bar is therefore higher in this sub-genre than in top-down MOBAs. Players chose the third-person format for the immersive action experience. If the combat itself does not feel satisfying and responsive, the perspective becomes a liability — visual noise in team fights, difficulty tracking threats from behind your character — rather than an asset. The community discussion around Smite versus Deadlock notes the difference between the two: Deadlock "is more like Overwatch or TF2, where the focus is on gun play and shooter style strategy," while Smite "focuses less on gun play and more on skills and MOBA style strategy." Both need to feel good to play; they just calibrate the combat feel benchmark against different reference points.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The third-person perspective actively enhances combat satisfaction. Weapon handling and ability animations feel physically weighted and responsive. Hit confirmation is clear and rewarding — landing a difficult shot or a precisely timed ability registers with audio and visual feedback that validates the skill. Dodging and movement feel fluid. In team fights the camera proximity does not obscure important information — visual design manages perspective claustrophobia well. | The perspective makes every good play feel better. Combat feels like an action game that also happens to be a MOBA. |
| 4 | Good combat feel with the perspective adding genuine value. Most abilities and weapon interactions feel responsive and satisfying. Hit feedback is clear. Some visual noise in dense team fights but readable most of the time. Movement feels fluid in standard combat scenarios. | Feels good to play. The perspective enhances the experience without routinely working against you. |
| 3 | Adequate combat feel but the perspective does not consistently add value over top-down alternatives. Some abilities feel underwhelming or the audio feedback is inconsistent. Team fight readability suffers noticeably in dense encounters. Movement has some clunkiness. | The perspective is neutral rather than enhancing. Combat works but does not feel like a reason to choose this sub-genre over top-down. |
| 2 | The third-person perspective creates more problems than it solves. Visual noise in combat is a regular issue. Hit and ability feedback is weak enough that landing a good play is not communicated satisfyingly. Movement or camera handling creates disorientation in common combat situations. | The perspective is a liability. Combat readability is worse than a top-down alternative. |
| 1 | Severely poor combat feel. Input lag, missing hit feedback, camera handling that creates constant disorientation, or visual chaos in all team fights. The third-person perspective makes the game harder to play without compensating with feel. | Combat does not work. The perspective actively harms the experience. |

# Metric: Teamplay & Social
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = deep social infrastructure that makes friend groups the natural play unit, 1 = social systems that make coordinated group play harder than solo

## Why This Matters

The research identifies social play as one of the primary reasons players enter and stay in classic MOBAs: "can play with friends", "group game: friends will try to get other friends to play together with them, so a bit of virality going on there." The genre's virality mechanism runs through friend groups — one player joins, recruits others, and the group dynamic becomes the retention engine. This is the "low barrier to entry" advantage working in social context: because the game is free, the ask to friends is "just try it" rather than a purchase commitment.

Social systems that support this — easy party formation, in-game friend list integration, communication tools that work without external apps — amplify the core reason players stay. Poor social systems work directly against the genre's appeal: if forming and queuing with a group of 5 is significantly slower than solo, or if communication tools are minimal enough that teams need Discord to coordinate basic plays, the game is forcing its primary retention driver through external infrastructure it doesn't control. The communication systems in classic MOBAs — ping wheels, quick-chat presets, vision pings — are a core part of coordinated play for players who don't use voice, which is most of the player base in solo/duo queues.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Forming and queuing with a group of up to 5 is seamless — in-game friend invitations work immediately, party queues are reasonably fast, and playing with friends is clearly the optimised experience. The ping and communication system covers most tactical needs without requiring voice: direction pings, danger signals, objective calls, and quick-chat presets cover the fundamentals. Post-game the party stays together by default. Adding new people from a good game is simple. | Playing with friends is the natural mode. The game actively keeps your group together. |
| 4 | Party play works well with minor friction. Group queues take slightly longer than solo but aren't punishing. Communication tools cover most needs. Friend-adding post-game is easy. Minor party management friction that doesn't impact the actual play experience. | Playing with friends works. The systems support it even if they weren't explicitly built around it. |
| 3 | Party systems are functional but create friction — group queues are noticeably longer, party sizes are limited below 5, or communication tools are minimal enough that external apps become the natural choice. Adding friends mid-game is possible but easy to miss. Playing with a group works but the game doesn't make it the preferred mode. | You can play with friends but the game doesn't reward it or actively support it. You'll end up in Discord regardless. |
| 2 | Significant party friction — group queue penalties are large, party size is hard-capped below what the game mode requires, or communication tools are so basic that coordination requires external apps. Friend list integration is clunky or missing. The experience for a group of 5 is meaningfully worse than for solo players. | Playing with friends requires workarounds. The game doesn't support groups well. |
| 1 | No meaningful social systems. No party functionality, no communication tools beyond basic chat, no friend list integration. Every session begins as strangers with no infrastructure to form ongoing relationships or continue playing with people you had good games with. | Solo is the only real mode. Group play is entirely external to the game. |

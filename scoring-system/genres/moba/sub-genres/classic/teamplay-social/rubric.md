# Metric: Teamplay & Social
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = social infrastructure that makes friend groups the natural play unit, 1 = systems that make coordinated group play harder than solo

## Why This Matters

The research identifies social play as one of the primary entry points into classic MOBAs: "group game: friends will try to get other friends to play together with them, so a bit of virality going on there." The genre grows through friend recruitment. One person joins, pulls in their friends, and the social dynamic becomes the reason they all stay.

QUT research studying MOBA player behaviour confirms this: the study found that "the ability to play with friends is salient when beginning play and during periods of engagement" and that "social factors caused a significant proportion (22.99%) of players to return to MOBA play." Critically, social factors were seldom the reason players quit — meaning the social layer is an engine for retention even when the player's individual motivation dips. A friend group keeps playing together longer than any individual player would play solo.

This creates a clear design requirement. Systems that make party formation fast and painless, that keep a group together post-match, and that give teammates the tools to coordinate without requiring an external app, directly support the genre's primary retention mechanism. Systems that create friction — long group queue times, hard party size caps, no ping communication — work against it.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Forming and queuing with a group of up to 5 is seamless — in-game friend invitations work immediately, party queues are reasonably fast, and playing with friends is clearly the optimised experience. The ping and communication system covers most tactical needs without requiring voice: direction pings, danger signals, objective calls, and quick-chat presets cover the fundamentals. Post-game the party stays together by default. Adding new people from a good game is simple. | Playing with friends is the natural mode. The game actively keeps your group together. |
| 4 | Party play works well with minor friction. Group queues take slightly longer than solo but are not punishing. Communication tools cover most needs. Friend-adding post-game is easy. Minor party management friction that does not impact the actual play experience. | Playing with friends works. The systems support it even if they were not explicitly built around it. |
| 3 | Party systems are functional but create friction. Group queues are noticeably longer, party sizes are limited below 5, or communication tools are minimal enough that external apps become the natural choice. Adding friends mid-game is possible but easy to miss. Playing with a group works but the game does not make it the preferred mode. | You can play with friends but the game does not reward it or actively support it. You'll end up in Discord regardless. |
| 2 | Significant party friction — group queue penalties are large, party size is hard-capped below what the game mode requires, or communication tools are so basic that coordination requires external apps. Friend list integration is clunky or missing. The experience for a group of 5 is meaningfully worse than for solo players. | Playing with friends requires workarounds. The game does not support groups well. |
| 1 | No meaningful social systems. No party functionality, no communication tools beyond basic chat, no friend list integration. Every session begins as strangers with no infrastructure to form ongoing relationships or continue playing with people you had good games with. | Solo is the only real mode. Group play is entirely external to the game. |

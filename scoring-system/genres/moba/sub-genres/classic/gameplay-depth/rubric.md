# Metric: Gameplay Depth
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = virtually unlimited skill ceiling with distinct, learnable mastery layers, 1 = skill ceiling reached within weeks with no meaningful progression beyond the basics

## Why This Matters

"So easy to pick up but the depth for further improvement being so vast" is the phrase that recurs across the research when players explain why classic MOBAs hold them for thousands of hours. The genre's defining appeal is that it rewards different layers of mastery simultaneously: individual mechanics (last-hitting efficiency, champion-specific combos, ability timing), lane dynamics (matchup knowledge, wave management, trading patterns), macro play (rotation timing, vision control, objective priority), and draft theory. The research describes this explicitly: "laning is very complex, you have to know the matchups, you have to know how to manipulate waves, how to leave lane to help your team. Every single game is different, even if you play the same champion every time."

This creates the genre's characteristic quality — players who have invested 500 hours still see clearly what separates them from 2,000-hour players. Improvement always has a next step. Games that fail here deliver a skill ceiling players reach in weeks, after which sessions stop teaching anything new and engagement drops.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Multiple distinct mastery layers that take months to engage and years to master. Individual mechanics (last-hitting, ability combos, champion-specific techniques), lane dynamics (matchup knowledge, wave management, trading), and macro play (rotation timing, vision control, objective priority) are all meaningful and learnable. High-skill play looks clearly different from low-skill play. The learning curve is steep but consistent — every stage of improvement feels earned and opens a new layer to explore. | You can see exactly what better players are doing that you aren't. Improvement always has a next step. |
| 4 | Clear depth across mechanics and macro play. Most mastery layers are present and meaningful. A few elements are relatively shallow or solved quickly. High-skill play is visibly better but the gap between layers isn't always instructive. | The game rewards learning. You can identify what to work on and feel progress when you do. |
| 3 | Basic mechanics have depth but macro play or champion mastery caps out relatively quickly. The skill ceiling is real but reachable within months for dedicated players. Improvements plateau unless you specialise heavily into a narrow champion pool or role. | Fun to learn but the ceiling isn't far off. You'll feel competitive faster but stop improving sooner. |
| 2 | Shallow mechanics with limited mastery layers. Last-hitting, wave management, or rotations exist but the depth of each is minimal. Skill expression plateaus quickly — intermediate players and veterans perform at similar levels. | You learn what there is to learn fast. After a few weeks the game doesn't challenge you differently. |
| 1 | Surface-level mechanics only. No meaningful mastery layers beyond basic ability usage. Skill stops being a significant differentiator within the first few hours of play. Games feel determined by champion picks and random variables more than player quality. | The game plays itself past the basics. Improvement doesn't change what you're doing — only how fast you do it. |

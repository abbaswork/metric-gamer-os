# Metric: Map Design
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = map design that creates meaningful strategic variety and supports long-term competitive play, 1 = maps that collapse to known-optimal strategies with no meaningful terrain interaction

## Why This Matters

The research names map interaction as an explicit MOBA appeal driver: "MOBAs tend to design maps in ways that allow classes and champions to have interesting interaction with the environment." In classic MOBAs, the primary competitive map has to carry the whole game — it must be balanced enough to function as the backdrop for years of ranked play while being deep enough that teams can win from multiple strategic angles.

Esportstalk's breakdown of Summoner's Rift captures what a well-designed MOBA map actually does: "The beauty of the Summoner's Rift is that it contains various pathways, close corridors, choke points, and objectives over which you can (or should, rather) fight whenever possible." The jungle adds another layer: "The jungle position is incredibly flexible, and it provides ample room for creativity" — routing decisions shift based on whether lanes are winning or losing, which means the map actively responds to the game state rather than being static terrain. Timed objectives (Dragons, Baron) create predictable conflict windows that structure the mid and late game, giving teams real choices about when to force or avoid a fight.

Maps that fail on this metric collapse to a single optimal path players follow by rote. The terrain stops being a strategic environment and becomes a container.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The primary map supports years of competitive play without stagnating. Lane dynamics, jungle routing, objective placement, and base design together create enough strategic variation that teams can win from multiple angles. Timed objectives create consistent, exciting points of conflict. The jungle has multiple viable routing options rather than a single solved path. Secondary maps or rotating modes offer genuinely different gameplay experiences without requiring full game mastery. | The map feels like a designed competitive space, not just an arena. Where you fight and when is a real strategic decision. |
| 4 | Strong primary map design with clear strategic logic. Objective placement creates regular points of conflict. Minor structural imbalances exist but nothing that dominates play. Secondary map options are available and regularly played. | Good map design that supports long-term competitive play without limiting it. The terrain matters to how you win. |
| 3 | Functional map design that serves the genre without being particularly interesting. Jungle routing has one clearly optimal path that most players follow without considering alternatives. Strategic variety exists but narrows to a few proven team compositions and macro routes quickly. | Maps work as intended but don't inspire creative strategy. You learn the optimal path quickly and stick to it. |
| 2 | Map imbalances are significant enough to create exploitable dominant strategies. Certain objective positions or lane configurations structurally favour specific compositions. The primary map does not meaningfully support multiple viable macro approaches. | You play the map the way everyone else does. Strategic choice is constrained by structural map limitations. |
| 1 | Map design actively limits the game. Clear structural imbalances that skilled teams exploit every game. The terrain does not interact meaningfully with the game's mechanics — it is an arena rather than a strategic environment. Objectives function as gold drops rather than strategic anchors. | The map is a container, not a game system. Where you fight is determined by convention, not by strategic choice. |

# Metric: Map Design
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = map design that creates meaningful strategic variety and supports long-term competitive play, 1 = maps that collapse to known-optimal strategies with no meaningful terrain interaction

## Why This Matters

The research explicitly identifies map interaction as a driver of MOBA appeal: "MOBAs tend to design maps in ways that allow classes and champions to have interesting interaction with the environment." In classic MOBAs, the primary competitive map must be balanced enough to function as the backdrop for years of ranked play while being interesting enough that teams can win from multiple strategic angles. Jungle camp placement, river objective positioning, lane width, base access points, and ward locations are all design decisions that cascade through every level of play — from which champions are viable in which roles to how entire macro strategies are constructed.

The timed objective system — Dragon, Baron, Roshan equivalents — is the map's primary mechanism for creating strategic pressure: teams must contest or concede these objectives at predictable intervals, which forces conflict and creates the multi-stage game rhythm that defines classic MOBA pacing. A map that supports multiple viable macro strategies (forcing baron, split-pushing, early objective stacking) gives teams real choices about how to win. Secondary maps and modes extend the primary map's longevity by offering distinct gameplay contexts — ARAM, rotating mode equivalents — without requiring mastery of the full game.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The primary map supports years of competitive play without stagnating — lane dynamics, jungle routing, objective placement, and base design together create enough strategic variation that teams can win from multiple angles. Timed objectives create consistent, exciting points of conflict. The jungle has multiple viable routing options rather than a single solved path. Secondary maps or rotating modes offer genuinely different gameplay experiences without requiring full game mastery. | The map feels like a designed competitive space, not just an arena. Where you fight and when is a real strategic decision. |
| 4 | Strong primary map design with clear strategic logic. Objective placement creates regular points of conflict. Minor structural imbalances (one side slightly favoured, one jungle route slightly more optimal) but nothing that dominates play. Secondary map options exist and are regularly played. | Good map design that supports long-term competitive play without limiting it. The terrain matters to how you win. |
| 3 | Functional map design that serves the genre without being particularly interesting. Objective positions are standard and predictable. Jungle routing has one clearly optimal path that most players follow without considering alternatives. Strategic variety exists but narrows to a few proven team compositions and macro routes. | Maps work as intended but don't inspire creative strategy. You learn the optimal path quickly and stick to it without exploring alternatives. |
| 2 | Map imbalances are significant enough to create exploitable dominant strategies. Certain objective positions or lane configurations structurally favour specific compositions. The primary map doesn't meaningfully support multiple viable macro approaches — games funnel into the same early sequence repeatedly. | You play the map the way everyone else does. Strategic choice is constrained by structural map limitations. |
| 1 | Map design actively limits the game. Clear structural imbalances that skilled teams exploit every game. The terrain doesn't interact meaningfully with the game's mechanics — it is an arena rather than a strategic environment. Objectives function as gold drops rather than strategic anchors. | The map is a container, not a game system. Where you fight is determined by convention, not by strategic choice. |

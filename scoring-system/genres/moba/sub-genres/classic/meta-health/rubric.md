# Metric: Meta Health
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = diverse meta where most champions are viable and no strategy is mandatory, 1 = solved meta where a small set of picks dominates every game

## Why This Matters

Tier list searches dominate every classic MOBA in the research — "honor of kings tier" at +90%, "lol arena tier list" at +130% — because the current meta directly affects whether a player's preferred champion is a viable choice. Players check tier lists before queuing because they know the balance state determines what's worth playing.

When a meta goes bad, the symptoms are specific. A zleague.gg analysis of the League of Legends Season 14 meta describes what stagnation looks like in practice: "repetitive champion picks (Azir, Akali, Orianna)" across roles without meaningful rotation, while "diversity in champion selection and strategic experimentation" is what a healthy meta actually looks like. Dexerto reported the Season 2024 item overhaul as a case study in what goes wrong when balance fails: players called the changes a "huge mistake as new items ruin balance," with Tristana reaching 93% pick and ban presence in professional play. That kind of dominance narrows the draft to a checklist exercise and punishes players for picking favourites.

The research captures the positive version too: players name "it constantly changes, metas, new champs, reworks" as one of the specific reasons they stay. Stagnation is the explicit failure mode. Patch cadence is inseparable from meta health — too slow and broken states define seasons, too aggressive and long-term character investment feels pointless.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Wide champion viability at most ranks. Bans eliminate outliers, not mandatory picks. Multiple champions are competitively viable in each position. Meta shifts meaningfully with each patch cycle — last patch's S-tier is not automatically this patch's S-tier. New champions and reworks integrate without trivialising existing options. Playing off-meta is a preference choice, not a competitive handicap. | Your champion is a liability only at the extreme high end, not across casual and mid ranks. You can play a wider range and still compete. |
| 4 | Healthy meta with occasional patches that overcorrect or create temporary tier gaps. Most champions remain viable across ranks. An S-tier exists but playing outside it is a minor disadvantage rather than a severe one. Bans matter but are not consumed by 3-4 must-bans every game. | Meta is generally fair. Brief post-patch imbalances resolve without defining the extended experience. |
| 3 | A clear tier above the rest but still multiple viable picks per position. Patches address balance issues but not always swiftly — broken states persist for 1-2 patches. At higher ranks, playing the meta starts to feel like the cost of winning. | You can deviate from the meta but it costs you. The tier list is a recommendation at most ranks, a requirement at high ranks. |
| 2 | Narrow meta with must-ban champions and clearly correct composition archetypes. Patches are slow or fail to address dominant strategies. Players feel compelled to play specific champions regardless of preference to avoid a structural disadvantage. | The meta dictates your picks more than your preference does. Off-meta play is a meaningful competitive handicap. |
| 1 | Solved meta where 5-10 champions dominate across all ranks. Every lobby features the same picks and bans. Champion balance is absent, stagnant, or consistently fails to shift the dominant set. Playing the correct picks matters more than individual skill. | If you're not playing the correct champions, you're losing to meta rather than to the opponent. |

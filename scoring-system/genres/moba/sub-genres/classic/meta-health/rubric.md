# Metric: Meta Health
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = diverse meta where most champions are viable and no strategy is mandatory, 1 = solved meta where a small set of picks dominates every game

## Why This Matters

Tier list queries dominate search interest across every classic MOBA in the research — "honor of kings tier" (+90%), "lol arena tier list" (+130%), "mlbb tier list" among the consistently high searches — because the meta state directly affects whether a player's preferred champion is a viable choice or an automatic disadvantage. Players check tier lists before queuing because they know the game's balance determines what's worth playing.

A healthy meta supports broad pick diversity: bans eliminate genuine outliers rather than mandatory must-bans, most champions can theoretically function at most skill levels, and the meta evolves meaningfully with each patch cycle. A broken meta — one where a small cluster of champions dominates every position — reduces the draft to a checklist exercise and punishes players for picking favourites. The research is explicit: players love that "it constantly changes, metas, new champs, reworks" — stagnation is the failure mode. Patch cadence is inseparable from meta health: "patches could reset or start a completely new meta." A game that patches too slowly creates despair in the current broken state; too aggressively creates instability that makes long-term investment feel pointless.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Wide champion viability at most ranks. Bans eliminate outliers, not mandatory picks. Multiple champions are competitively viable in each position. Meta shifts meaningfully with each patch cycle — last patch's S-tier isn't automatically this patch's S-tier. New champions and reworks integrate without trivialising existing options. Playing off-meta is a preference choice, not a competitive handicap. | Your champion is a liability only at the extreme high end, not across casual and mid ranks. You can play a wider range and still compete. |
| 4 | Healthy meta with occasional patches that overcorrect or create temporary tier gaps. Most champions remain viable across ranks. An S-tier exists but doesn't feel mandatory — playing it is optimal, not required. Bans matter but aren't consumed by 3–4 must-bans every game. | Meta is generally fair. Brief imbalances after patches resolve without defining the extended experience. |
| 3 | A clear tier above the rest but still multiple viable picks per position. Patches address balance issues but not always swiftly — broken states persist for 1–2 patches. At higher ranks, playing the meta starts to feel like the cost of winning. | You can deviate from the meta but it costs you. The tier list is a recommendation at most ranks, a requirement at high ranks. |
| 2 | Narrow meta with must-ban champions and clearly correct composition archetypes. Patches are slow or fail to address dominant strategies. Players feel compelled to play specific champions regardless of preference to avoid a structural disadvantage. | The meta dictates your picks more than your preference does. Off-meta play is a meaningful competitive handicap. |
| 1 | Solved meta where 5–10 champions dominate across all ranks. Every lobby features the same picks and bans. Champion balance is absent, stagnant, or consistently fails to shift the dominant set. Playing the correct 5 picks is more important than individual skill. | If you're not playing the correct champions, you're losing to meta rather than to the opponent. |

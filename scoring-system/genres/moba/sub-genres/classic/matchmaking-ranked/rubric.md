# Metric: Matchmaking & Ranked Quality
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = accurate, fast matchmaking that creates competitive games where outcomes reflect play quality, 1 = wildly inconsistent skill gaps or a rank system that does not track skill

## Why This Matters

The research names ranked progression directly as a retention driver: "it feels really rewarding to carry your teammates to victory, to build up your skills as a player, and to advance in the ranked system." But that reward only works when the matchmaking creates lobbies where outcomes reflect play quality rather than lobby luck.

Classic MOBAs face a specific structural problem: in a 5v5 format, one severely mismatched player affects nine others. This multiplies the damage of a bad match relative to games with fewer players. The community frustration around this is well-documented. A zleague.gg analysis describes what goes wrong: players face "insurmountable challenges and frustrations, especially when facing opponents of much higher skill levels" as a result of the "drastic skill gap between their actual level and the assigned rank." At the highest levels the problem compounds differently — Riot reset Master, Grandmaster, and Challenger ranks across multiple regions in 2026 following "months of instability, inconsistent LP gains, and widespread frustration from Apex-tier players" (Dotesports).

Good ranked solves for this by creating games close enough that losses point to something the player can fix. When matchmaking fails, ranked becomes a source of frustration rather than growth.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Games consistently feel competitive — opponents and teammates are at skill levels close enough that outcomes depend on play quality. Rank progression accurately reflects improvement over a sample of games. Queue times are acceptable at most skill brackets. The rank system is transparent — you can reason about why you're gaining or losing rank. New-player calibration places players near their actual skill level within the first session. | Ranked feels like a practice tool. Wins feel earned and losses point to something improvable. |
| 4 | Generally fair matchmaking with occasional outlier lobbies. A significant mismatch appears sometimes, or a rank plateau that doesn't feel skill-accurate. Queue times are fine at most ranks, longer at extreme brackets. The rank system is understandable even when it doesn't feel perfectly fair in a given session. | Mostly fair. The rare bad lobby doesn't define the experience. |
| 3 | Noticeable but not game-breaking inconsistency. Lobby skill gaps appear frequently enough to feel like a factor in outcomes. Rank progression sometimes feels disconnected from actual performance across a session. Queue times are manageable but not fast at most ranks. | Ranked is functional but frustrating more often than it should be. You can still climb but the system works against you at times. |
| 2 | Significant skill gaps appear regularly — games often feel decided before they begin. Rank inflation, poor MMR calibration, or smurf concentration means rank doesn't meaningfully reflect skill. Long queue times degrade match quality. New players are placed against experienced ones at rates that damage the learning experience. | Ranked feels like a lottery. Outcomes are too often determined by matchmaking rather than how you played. |
| 1 | Matchmaking is broken or effectively absent. Skill differences in lobbies are so large that competitive play is impossible. The rank system does not track skill. Queue times are so long that players accept any match quality to get a game. | You can't improve from ranked. Games are too uneven to be instructive. |

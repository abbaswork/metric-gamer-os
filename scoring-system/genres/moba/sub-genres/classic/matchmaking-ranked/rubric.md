# Metric: Matchmaking & Ranked Quality
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = accurate, fast matchmaking that creates competitive games where outcomes reflect play quality, 1 = wildly inconsistent skill gaps, slow queues, or a rank system that doesn't track skill

## Why This Matters

The research identifies ranked progression as a core MOBA retention driver: "it feels really rewarding to carry your teammates to victory, to build up your skills as a player, and to advance in the ranked system." Ranked mode is where that progression is validated — but only when matchmaking creates lobbies where skill differences are tight enough that outcomes reflect play quality rather than queue luck.

Classic MOBAs face specific matchmaking challenges that other genres don't. A 5v5 team composition means one severely mismatched player in a lobby affects nine others — the structural damage of one outlier is multiplied across the whole match. Rank inflation cycles, MMR decay, and placement calibration issues create situations where the visible rank stops reflecting actual skill, eroding trust in the system even for players who are performing correctly. At high ranks, queue times extend to the point where the game begins relaxing match quality constraints, creating lobbies where rank diversity is visibly large. Good ranked creates the "practice tool" dynamic: games are consistently close enough to teach, wins feel earned, and losses point to something improvable.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Games consistently feel competitive — opponents and teammates are at skill levels close enough that outcomes depend on play quality. Rank progression accurately reflects improvement over a sample of games. Queue times are acceptable at most skill brackets. The rank system is transparent — you can reason about why you're gaining or losing rank. New-player calibration places players near their actual skill level within the first session. | Ranked feels like a practice tool. Wins feel earned and losses point to something improvable. |
| 4 | Generally fair matchmaking with occasional outlier lobbies — a significant mismatch or a rank plateau that doesn't feel skill-accurate. Queue times are fine at most ranks, longer at extreme brackets. The rank system is understandable even when it doesn't feel perfectly fair in a given session. | Mostly fair. The rare bad lobby doesn't define the experience. |
| 3 | Noticeable but not game-breaking inconsistency. Lobby skill gaps appear frequently enough to feel like a factor in outcomes. Rank progression sometimes feels disconnected from actual performance across a session. Queue times are manageable but not fast at most ranks. | Ranked is functional but frustrating more often than it should be. You can still climb but the system works against you at times. |
| 2 | Significant skill gaps appear regularly — games often feel decided before they begin. Rank inflation, poor MMR calibration, or smurf concentration means rank doesn't meaningfully reflect skill. Long queue times degrade match quality. New players are placed against experienced ones at rates that damage the learning experience. | Ranked feels like a lottery. Outcomes are too often determined by matchmaking rather than how you played. |
| 1 | Matchmaking is broken or effectively absent. Skill differences in lobbies are so large that competitive play is impossible. The rank system doesn't track skill — players climb or fall based on factors beyond their control. Queue times are so long that players accept any match quality. | You can't improve from ranked. Games are too uneven to be instructive. |

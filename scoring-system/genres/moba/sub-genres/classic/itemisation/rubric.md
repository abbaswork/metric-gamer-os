# Metric: Itemisation & Build Depth
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = deep, flexible item system that rewards live strategic adaptation per game, 1 = scripted build paths per champion with no meaningful in-game decisions

## Why This Matters

Itemisation is the strategic layer that separates classic MOBAs from every other sub-genre — hero brawlers and third-person MOBAs either remove items entirely or use significantly simplified systems. The Google Trends data signals this clearly: "anti shield item lol" at +450% search interest shows players actively engaging with specific item-level strategic decisions mid-game, not just following a pre-game guide. When a player searches for "anti shield item," they're asking: "given what the enemy just built, what do I build now?" That live adaptation question is the item system functioning as intended.

Counter-building — switching item purchases in response to what the enemy team is constructing — is a layer of skill expression that persists across every game regardless of champion. Dota 2's item system rewards players who understand the build tree deeply enough to construct situationally optimal items from components. League of Legends' mythic/legendary tiering asks players to commit to a strategic direction early and adapt the later items to the game state. Both reward players who read the enemy composition and adjust, rather than following a solved guide. When the item system is shallow or fully solved, this in-game strategic layer collapses: every game starts and ends with the same 6 items regardless of what the enemy is building. A new item release that opens up a previously unexplored build path on an underperforming champion is one of the most visible ways classic MOBAs stay fresh between major content updates.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Multiple viable build paths per champion that produce meaningfully different outcomes. Counter-building is impactful — buying the right item against specific threats noticeably changes match dynamics. Item interactions create strategic possibilities players discover rather than find in a guide. New item additions regularly unlock previously unexplored builds on existing champions. The build decision is a live, active layer every game — what you buy in game 10 against a fed enemy bruiser is a different answer than what you bought in game 1 against a control mage. | Your build is a decision, not a lookup. What you buy in response to the enemy feels like real strategy. |
| 4 | Good build variety with clear situational paths. Counter-building is meaningful. Most champions have 2–3 viable builds that perform differently. New items occasionally shift the meta. Some champion builds are over-solved but most retain meaningful decision points across different game states. | Build diversity is real. You adapt your purchases per game and it makes a difference. |
| 3 | Some build variety but an optimal path exists for most champions that experienced players follow by default. Counter-building is possible but the deviation from the standard build rarely shifts outcomes enough to feel decisive. Item additions don't meaningfully expand the strategic space for most champions. | You follow the guide most of the time. Deviating is occasionally right but it's rarely clearly better. |
| 2 | One correct build per champion that the community has fully solved. Counter-building exists in theory but the correct play is almost always to complete core items first. Item variety is cosmetic rather than strategic — different first items on the same champion converge to the same power level at full build. | You copy the pro build and follow it. Any deviation is usually a mistake rather than a decision. |
| 1 | No meaningful itemisation depth. Item selection is scripted, randomised, or so simplified that no strategic decision is made during the match. The build layer doesn't function as a strategic system — it is an automatic upgrade path. | Items are power increases, not decisions. |

# Metric: Monetisation Model
**Genre:** MOBA
**Sub-genre:** Classic
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = fully cosmetic-only spending with a generous free experience, 1 = pay-to-win elements or a free experience so restricted it effectively requires payment to be competitive

## Why This Matters

Classic MOBAs are free-to-play without exception. The research is blunt about why: "if it wasn't free and had less playerbase I wouldn't play it." Players accept F2P on one condition: that nothing affecting match outcomes is locked behind payment.

In practice, the fairness debate in classic MOBAs centres on champion access, not cosmetics. A player who cannot access a champion pool large enough for draft-based competitive play is structurally disadvantaged, even if no individual purchase gives a direct power advantage. Gametyrant's analysis of League of Legends puts it plainly: the core issue is "the lengthy grind to unlock all champions" as a frustration point, while the counterpoint is that "the core competitive elements remain accessible to all players, and success is primarily determined by skill and strategy." Dota 2 takes the opposite approach — all heroes are free from day one, with monetisation based entirely on cosmetics. Tradeit.gg describes this as a "completely free-to-play model with all heroes available from the start, and monetization based solely on cosmetic items." Both models coexist in the genre, and players compare them actively.

The gap between these approaches is what the rubric scores: whether the free experience is complete enough to be genuinely competitive, or whether unlock pace creates a second-tier experience that nudges players toward spending.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Nothing affecting gameplay is behind a paywall. Champions are earnable through play at a pace where a new player has a competitively viable pool within a few weeks of regular sessions. The free progression track is clear and consistent. Cosmetics are available for purchase at prices the community broadly considers fair, with a meaningful free cosmetic track alongside. | You never feel like spending is the path to winning. The free experience is complete. |
| 4 | Cosmetic-only spending with a slightly slower champion unlock pace. A new player needs a few extra weeks before they have a full competitive pool, but the path is clear and achievable without payment. Most cosmetics are reasonably priced. | Fairly free. The grind exists but it's not exploitative. Spending is for preference or cosmetics, not competitive access. |
| 3 | Cosmetic-only but unlock pace is slow enough that new players feel meaningfully behind. A full champion pool requires either significant time or moderate spending. Some cosmetics are priced high enough that the value proposition is unclear to most players. | The free path works but is noticeably slow. New players feel the gap in champion access, not just skill. |
| 2 | Unlock pace is slow enough to create real friction. New players may feel effectively compelled to spend to have a competitive champion pool for draft modes. Battle pass seasonal content creates consistent FOMO pressure even when the content is cosmetic. | The free experience feels deliberately limited. Spending becomes the obvious path to not feeling behind. |
| 1 | Pay-to-win elements are present, or the free champion pool is so small that draft-based competitive play is effectively inaccessible without payment. | The game punishes non-payers in-match. Spending is not optional for competitive play. |

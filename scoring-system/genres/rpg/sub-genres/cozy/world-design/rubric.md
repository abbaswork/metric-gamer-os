# Metric: World Design
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** Cozy
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = art direction and sound design are cohesive and inviting, and the map rewards exploration with genuinely distinct areas, 1 = the world is visually and aurally generic and offers nothing worth exploring beyond the first area

## Why This Matters

Cozy RPGs are sold on vibe before they're sold on systems, and the research file is direct about that: Stardew Valley's "cosy pixel-art style feels nostalgic" and its "dynamic and cosy soundtrack" are both named specifically as things players love, independent of any mechanic. Google Trends data in the research file adds a second, less obvious signal — Palia's biggest breakout search terms are "palia highlands" (+3600%) and "palia royal highlands" (+3350%), both new explorable zones added post-launch. That's a direct measure of how much a new area to explore can move player interest in this sub-genre, well beyond ordinary content-update hype.

External analysis explains *why* pixel art specifically works so well for this sub-genre rather than just being a budget choice: "a pixel art world is detailed enough to feel real, but abstract enough to feel safe." That tension — legible and atmospheric without being stressful or visually noisy — is exactly what a cozy world needs to do, since the genre's whole pitch is relaxation. A world that's gorgeous but visually overwhelming, or that has one good biome and nothing else worth walking to, both fail this metric for different reasons.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Art style and music are cohesive and distinct enough to be recognisable from a screenshot or a few bars of the soundtrack alone. The map has multiple, visually distinct areas worth returning to (not just passing through), and at least one expansion-worthy region exists or is clearly plausible. Stardew Valley's farm-town-mines-Ginger Island spread, paired with its seasonal soundtrack changes, is the reference point. | You'll want to just wander sometimes, with no quest active, purely because the world is nice to be in. |
| 4 | Strong, cohesive art and sound with good area variety, but one zone clearly got less attention than the rest (a reused tileset, a forgettable track). The overall world still feels worth exploring. | You'll have a favourite area and a "fine, I guess" area, but nowhere will feel like a chore to visit. |
| 3 | The art style is pleasant and consistent but not distinctive — it would fit into several other games in the sub-genre without standing out. Music is present and unobtrusive rather than memorable. The map has a couple of areas but limited reason to revisit most of them. | It looks and sounds fine. You won't screenshot it unprompted. |
| 2 | Art assets are inconsistent in quality or style across areas, or the soundtrack is sparse/repetitive enough to become background noise quickly. The map is mostly one functional area with minor cosmetic variation elsewhere. | After the first few hours, the world stops giving you anything new to look at. |
| 1 | The world is visually flat or mismatched (clashing styles, low-effort assets) and the soundtrack is minimal or grating on repeat. There's effectively one area, and nothing about the space invites exploration for its own sake. | You'll only go where a quest marker tells you to, and you won't want to be there. |

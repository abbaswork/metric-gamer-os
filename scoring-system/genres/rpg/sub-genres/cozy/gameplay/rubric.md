# Metric: Gameplay
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** Cozy
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = the core loop hands you a constant, varied stream of tangible rewards that pulls you back day after day, 1 = the loop is repetitive busywork with no meaningful sense of growth

## Why This Matters

Cozy RPGs live or die on their day-to-day loop, because there's no combat skill ceiling or competitive ladder carrying the experience — the loop itself has to be the reason you keep playing. The research file's framing of Stardew Valley is exact on this: it "gives constant, micro-dopamine hits. Players are constantly building, crafting, or unlocking something new, making it highly addictive," while also balancing "slow-paced relaxation with satisfying, goal-driven progression." Those two things — relaxation and goal-driven progression — sound like they pull against each other, but the best cozy loops resolve that tension rather than picking one side.

External analysis of the sub-genre backs this up with the actual mechanism: cozy farming games work through "incremental reward systems, where every action yields immediate, tangible feedback like crops growing day by day or friendship meters visibly increasing." The reward doesn't have to be large — it has to be frequent, visible, and tied directly to an action the player just took. A loop that makes you wait too long between visible payoffs, or that repeats the same action without varying the payoff (water crops, sell crops, water crops, nothing new), loses the thing that makes the genre work.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Every core activity (farming, crafting, fishing, foraging, etc.) feeds visibly into at least one other system, so a single session produces several kinds of progress at once. New unlocks, recipes, or upgrades arrive often enough that you're rarely doing the exact same task with no new payoff in sight. Stardew Valley is the reference point here — crop growth, shop unlocks, tool upgrades, and community-centre goals all advance from the same daily actions. | You'll lose track of time without meaning to. Every task feels like it's building toward several things at once, not just itself. |
| 4 | The loop has real variety and a steady unlock pace, but one activity type (e.g. combat in the mines, or a single crafting tier) lags behind the others in payoff. The core farming/building/social loop still carries the experience comfortably. | You'll have a favourite activity and a least-favourite one, but you'll never feel like you're just killing time. |
| 3 | The loop functions and gives you things to do, but rewards arrive on a flatter schedule — you can predict exactly what tomorrow looks like because today looked the same as yesterday. Unlocks exist but are spaced far enough apart that stretches of play feel like maintenance rather than progress. | The game won't bore you outright, but you'll catch yourself checking how much is actually left to unlock. |
| 2 | Several days of play pass with no new system, recipe, or unlock — you're repeating the same 2–3 actions because there's genuinely nothing else to do yet. Rewards exist but are disconnected from the action that earned them, so progress doesn't register as a payoff in the moment. | You'll start treating sessions as chores to clear rather than something you wanted to do. |
| 1 | The loop is one or two actions repeated with no escalation, no new unlocks, and no visible feedback connecting what you did to what changed. There's no goal-driven thread pulling you from one day to the next. | You'll stop playing not because you finished something, but because there was nothing left to look forward to. |

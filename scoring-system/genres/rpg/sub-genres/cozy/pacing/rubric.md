# Metric: Pacing
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** Cozy
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = the game's internal rhythm (day length, energy/stamina systems, session checkpoints) lets you make real progress in both a short sitting and a long one without the clock fighting you, 1 = stamina caps, ticking day timers, or grind bottlenecks force you to rush or stall regardless of how you'd like to play

## Why This Matters

This metric is about the game's own internal tempo — day length, energy/stamina systems, how progression speed holds up over hundreds of hours — not engagement mechanics layered on top of it. The research file points at this from the player side: Stardew Valley players value that they "can take the game at their own pace and aren't forced to go in a specific direction, so there's no wrong way to play the game," and that there are "lots of things to do, preventing players from getting bored" across "hundreds of hours of content." Both speak to rhythm — one to flexibility in how you spend a session, the other to whether the content holds up across the whole runtime.

External analysis of the sub-genre shows this is an active design conversation, not a solved problem. Stardew Valley's classic energy/stamina-and-day-length structure is exactly what creates the time pressure some players feel: "some players... feel pressured by the ticking clock each in-game day in traditional farming sims like Stardew Valley." Newer entries in the sub-genre design around that directly — Roots of Pacha uses "infinite stamina and adjustable day lengths to remove the 'rushing' feeling, allowing players to play entirely at their own pace," and other cozy titles drop stamina bars and inventory limits with "no penalties for skipping crops or staying out late." On the other end, games like Littlewood are praised for days that "end naturally," giving players a built-in stopping point rather than an arbitrary one. The rubric below is checking where a game lands between those two approaches: a clock that works with you, or one that works against you.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | No hard energy/stamina cap (or a generous, adjustable one), and the day/session structure has natural stopping points, so a 10-minute sitting and a 3-hour sitting both produce real, felt progress. Progression speed stays consistent across the runtime — no sudden bottleneck demands a long grind to move forward. Roots of Pacha's infinite stamina and adjustable day length, built specifically to remove the "rushing" feeling, is the reference point. | Whether you've got ten minutes or the whole evening, you'll always end the session having actually gotten somewhere. |
| 4 | A stamina or day-length system exists but is generous enough that it rarely cuts a session short before you've accomplished what you set out to do. One progression stage bottlenecks slightly (a resource grind ahead of an upgrade) but resolves within a session or two. | You'll occasionally watch the clock, but it's a minor consideration, not something you plan your session around. |
| 3 | Stamina or day-length limits are noticeable and occasionally end a session before you finish a task, but you learn to plan around them once you understand the rhythm. Progression slows at predictable points without feeling punishing. | You'll start timing your tasks to the in-game clock once you've played a while — manageable, but it's there. |
| 2 | Energy caps or short day lengths regularly force you to stop mid-task, making short sessions feel unproductive. At least one progression stage demands a grind long enough to break the game's normal rhythm. | Short sessions start to feel pointless, and at least one stretch of the game will feel like a slog before things move again. |
| 1 | Tight stamina/energy caps or short, rigid days constantly cut sessions short before anything meaningful happens, and/or a severe grind bottleneck makes moment-to-moment play feel like a chore rather than a rhythm you settle into. | You're fighting the clock more than you're enjoying the game. |

# Metric: Pacing
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** Cozy
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = you set your own rhythm, with nothing forcing a log-in, punishing time away, or rushing you toward a finish line, and enough content that boredom never sets the pace instead, 1 = the game pressures you with FOMO mechanics, daily-login requirements, or runs out of things to do well before you're ready to stop

## Why This Matters

This is arguably the defining trait that separates "cozy" from the rest of the RPG genre, and the research file is unambiguous about it. Stardew Valley's appeal is described as "Not competitive or high-stakes so acts as a peaceful wind-down for many players," with players able to "take the game at their own pace and aren't forced to go in a specific direction, so there's no wrong way to play the game." Palia's research independently confirms the same principle from a live-service angle, where the temptation to add daily-login pressure is much higher: "the game can be played at your own pace and doesn't punish players for not logging in every day." Two structurally different games (single-player vs. live-service multiplayer) landing on the same design value is a strong signal this isn't incidental.

The designer's own stated intent for the genre-defining title makes the mechanism explicit. Per Wikipedia, Eric Barone kept Stardew Valley deliberately open-ended — in contrast to Harvest Moon games that could "end" after two in-game years — specifically "so players would not feel rushed to try to complete everything," designing for players "to feel excited rather than stressed." That's the throughline this rubric scores: not the absence of structure, but the absence of pressure, combined with enough content that the lack of pressure doesn't tip into boredom (the research file's "lots of things to do, preventing players from getting bored" and "famously affordable for hundreds of hours of content" both speak to that other failure mode).

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | No daily-login requirements, no time-limited FOMO events, and no hard end-state forcing completion by a deadline. You can stop for weeks and return with nothing lost. There's enough content (seasons of activities, dozens of hours of progression) that the lack of pressure doesn't leave you bored either. Stardew Valley's open-ended design — deliberately built to avoid the "end after two years" structure of earlier farming sims — is the reference point. | You play exactly as much or as little as you want, and the game never makes you feel behind for it. |
| 4 | Mostly pressure-free, with one soft nudge — a seasonal event with a generous window, or an optional bundle/quest with a loose deadline — that's easy to ignore without real loss. | You might glance at a calendar once in a while, but you'll never feel like you have to log in today specifically. |
| 3 | No hard FOMO mechanics, but the loop runs thinner than the playtime expects — you'll notice yourself waiting for the next thing to unlock, or feel mild guilt-by-design (a wilting garden, a sad pet) that nudges you back without outright punishing absence. | You can step away, but the game leans on you a little to come back sooner than you'd planned. |
| 2 | At least one real time-pressure mechanic exists — a battle pass, a limited-time cosmetic, or a system that visibly decays (relationships souring, resources spoiling) if you're away for more than a few days. | Missing a few days starts to feel like a real cost, not just a pause. |
| 1 | Daily-login rewards, rotating time-limited content, or energy/stamina systems that punish absence are core to how the game is structured, and/or the content runs out well before you've had your fill, leaving boredom doing the opposite job. | The game either chases you when you're not playing, or runs out of reasons to play long before you're ready to stop. |

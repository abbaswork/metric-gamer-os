# Metric: Story
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** Cozy
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = every major character carries a distinct, well-written arc that gives your long-term goals emotional weight, 1 = thin, interchangeable characters with no throughline

## Why This Matters

This metric covers the writing itself — plot threads, character backstory and lore, and dialogue quality — distinct from Character Development's relationship *mechanics* above. The research file backs this as a load-bearing part of the genre's appeal: Stardew Valley players cite "deep storylines for all characters" and "deep character lore" independently of the relationship system, and Palia's research separately surfaces "funny and entertaining dialogue" as a specific draw — a signal about writing quality, not just relationship depth.

External analysis of the sub-genre explains why story matters even in games without a traditional plot: narrative is what turns a checklist of farm chores into something with stakes. Cozy games "structure progression around narrative events tied to seasons, festivals, or special events," giving long-term goals — restoring a farm, helping a town recover — emotional meaning beyond the mechanical reward loop. Spiritfarer is the clearest example of this sub-genre executing it at a high level: each character you ferry to the afterlife has their own arc, amounting to roughly 90,000 words of spirit stories, with the developers researching end-of-life care facilities specifically to write those characters with weight rather than as quest-dispensers.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Nearly every named character has a distinct backstory and arc that resolves or evolves over the course of play, written with enough specificity that you could describe each one without mixing them up. Dialogue has personality and occasional genuine wit. Spiritfarer's spirit stories — each with their own bittersweet arc and grounded in real research into loss and care — is the reference point for what this looks like done well. | You'll remember these characters after you stop playing, not just what they sold you. |
| 4 | Most of the cast has real arcs and memorable writing, but a few characters exist mainly to hand out quests with minimal backstory. The main throughline (seasonal events, a central mystery, a restoration goal) is well-written and consistent. | The story will pull you forward even if a side character or two feels like filler. |
| 3 | There's a throughline and individual character bios exist, but they read as functional rather than distinctive — competent fantasy/slice-of-life writing without much that's memorable. Dialogue is pleasant but rarely surprising. | You'll finish the story satisfied, but you probably won't quote any of it to a friend. |
| 2 | Most characters are defined by a single trait or job title with little elaboration. Any overarching plot is thin or disconnected from day-to-day play, existing mainly to justify new unlocks rather than to mean anything on its own. | You'll forget most NPCs' names within a week of finishing. |
| 1 | Characters are interchangeable quest-givers with no backstory beyond their function. There is no overarching narrative thread connecting your progress to anything beyond "next unlock." | There's nothing here pulling you forward except the to-do list. |

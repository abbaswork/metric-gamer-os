# Metric: Character Development
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** Cozy
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = NPCs have distinct personalities and relationships that visibly deepen the more time you invest in them, 1 = NPCs are static set-dressing with no relationship progression

## Why This Matters

This metric is about the *relationship system*, not the plot — how NPCs respond to you, change toward you, and unlock new content as your standing with them grows (Story, below, covers the writing and lore itself). The research file names this as one of the strongest draws of the sub-genre: Stardew Valley players highlight that they "can date, marry, and interact with the townsfolk, and develop deep relationships," and Palia's research independently confirms "NPCs and romance options are strong" — two different games, two different developers, the same theme surfacing unprompted.

What makes this concrete enough to rubric is that the best implementations gate real content behind relationship depth rather than treating "friendship" as a cosmetic number. Stardew Valley's marriage system is the clearest documented example: the game ships 12 marriageable NPCs, each with their own gift preferences, and — critically — each NPC's schedule and the locations they make available change as friendship/heart level rises, with new dialogue and meeting spots unlocking at higher levels. That's the bar this rubric is checking for: does deepening a relationship actually change what the NPC does and offers, or does the friendship meter just go up with nothing behind it?

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | A large cast of NPCs each have a distinct personality, gift/dialogue preferences, and a relationship track that unlocks new dialogue, locations, or story content at multiple stages — not just at "married"/"max friendship." Stardew Valley's 12 marriageable villagers, each with heart-level-gated schedules and meeting spots, is the reference point. | You'll have favourites within days, and you'll keep noticing new things about them months in. |
| 4 | Most NPCs have real depth and a relationship track with at least one or two meaningful unlock points, but a portion of the cast feels thinner — present for dialogue variety without much behind it. | A handful of characters will hook you completely; a few others will feel like background characters by comparison. |
| 3 | NPCs are distinguishable from each other and friendship/relationship meters move and register, but the unlocks tied to deepening a relationship are minor (a line of dialogue, a small gift) rather than meaningfully new content. | Relationships feel like a checklist to fill rather than something that changes as it deepens. |
| 2 | Most NPCs share similar dialogue patterns or personality archetypes with only surface variation (name, portrait). Relationship progress is tracked numerically but rarely changes what the NPC actually says or does. | You'll struggle to tell two NPCs apart a week after meeting them. |
| 1 | NPCs are interchangeable — same handful of dialogue lines regardless of who you're talking to, no tracked relationship progression, no romance or deepening friendship system at all. | Talking to townsfolk feels like clicking through a menu, not getting to know anyone. |

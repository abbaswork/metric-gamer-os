# Metric: Combat & Gameplay Systems
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** JRPG
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = battles demand real tactical planning around party composition and turn order, with complexity that grows across the game, 1 = battles are won by repeating the same optimal action with no meaningful decisions

## Why This Matters

The research file shows a real player segment that specifically seeks out JRPGs for their gameplay systems, not just their stories: "Some players favor the more strategic, complex gameplay systems they believe jrpgs tend to have over other rpgs," and others separately list "the exploration, the party building and management, the combat systems and the music" as core draws. Turn-based combat specifically gets called out as something the genre still does that most western developers don't: "I like turn based battle systems, which few western devs dabble with in non-indie scene," alongside an appreciation for "crazy unique and often unorthodox progression systems."

External design analysis identifies what actually separates a strategically deep turn-based system from a shallow one — and it isn't realism or visual complexity. A genuinely deep system exhibits what one design analysis calls the "Four Virtues": "emergent complexity, clarity, determinism, and tactical tools" — meaning "the immediate consequences of various tactical decisions are made clear to the player," and "the system is sufficiently deterministic that skilled play using a proper strategy will nearly always result in victory." Party variety matters structurally too: "more characters means that the player can be expected to handle much more involved combat scenarios," but only if "each individual character can be much more specialized" — i.e., distinct battlefield roles, not reskinned damage-dealers. That's what this rubric is checking: are decisions clear and consequential, and does the party actually demand different things from different members?

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Every party member fills a genuinely distinct battlefield role, turn order/timing is a planning tool you actively manipulate, and outcomes are consequential enough that a well-planned turn reliably wins while a careless one reliably costs you. Systems (skills, progression, equipment) keep introducing new tactical wrinkles well into the back half of the game rather than flattening out once you've learned the basics. | Every fight asks you to actually think, and your party composition matters as much as your reflexes — or more. |
| 4 | Strong tactical depth and clear party roles for most of the game, with one stretch where the system stops introducing anything new and a single best strategy reliably works. | Combat stays engaging for the vast majority of the game, with one patch where you're coasting on autopilot. |
| 3 | The system is legible and turn order is visible, but party roles overlap enough that several members are functionally interchangeable, and most fights have one clearly-optimal approach you settle into early. | Combat is satisfying enough that you won't skip it, but you'll find your strategy and rarely deviate from it. |
| 2 | Tactical decisions exist on paper, but unclear feedback or unreliable outcomes (randomness that overrides good planning) make it hard to tell whether a loss was a strategy failure or bad luck. Party members are similar enough in role that composition barely matters. | You'll win or lose fights without being sure why, which makes planning feel pointless. |
| 1 | Combat is decided by repeating one optimal input or grinding stat advantages rather than by any in-battle decision-making. Party composition has no real impact on outcomes. | Battles become something to click through rather than something to solve. |

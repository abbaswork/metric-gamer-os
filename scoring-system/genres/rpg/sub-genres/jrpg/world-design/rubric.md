# Metric: World Design
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** JRPG
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = distinct regions and a soundtrack that work together to make the world memorable on their own, separate from the plot, 1 = environments and music are generic backdrop with nothing distinct to recall

## Why This Matters

This metric combines setting/environment design with music, consistent with how Cozy's World Design treats art and sound as one shared atmosphere axis — the research file supports merging them here too, since players name both in the same breath. JRPGs are described as more "creative... in terms of world design, storyline, etc" than western RPGs, with "unique and fun world design, some even report them as feeling cozy," and an "attractive art style and music" cited as a core draw alongside "the feeling of adventure" from traveling "through many regions/locations." Soundtrack specifically gets named as a top-tier, independent draw — one player ranks it first among five reasons they play the genre: "1- soundtrack (most JRPGs have peak or at least good music)."

External analysis explains why music carries this much weight in the genre specifically, beyond just being pleasant background: JRPG scores function as an active narrative device, not scene-setting. "The music actively participates in the narrative," with leitmotifs that "stand in for and represent characters, actions, or items in a game and can alert the player to the importance of those objects even if not presently on screen." That's a meaningfully different job than film scoring — "players can make decisions based not only on what they are seeing, but also on what they are hearing." Combined with environment variety (the research file's "feeling of adventure" from regional travel), this rubric checks whether a world's audiovisual identity is distinct enough to be recognized on its own, independent of the plot happening inside it.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Regions are visually and culturally distinct from each other (not just palette-swapped biomes), and the soundtrack uses recurring themes tied to specific places, characters, or moments — so a piece of music alone can call a scene to mind. Final Fantasy's use of leitmotif across its Uematsu-scored entries, where a theme's reappearance signals meaning before a word of dialogue plays, is the reference point. | You'd recognize where you are from the music alone, and the world stays vivid in your memory long after you've stopped playing. |
| 4 | Strong regional variety and a memorable score, with one or two areas or tracks that feel like reused filler rather than purpose-built for that location. | Most of the world will stick with you; a stretch or two will feel like padding between the memorable parts. |
| 3 | Regions are visually differentiated but functionally similar in what they offer, and the soundtrack is pleasant and fitting without using theme or repetition meaningfully — it sets a mood without reinforcing anything specific. | The world looks and sounds fine while you're in it, but it won't follow you home. |
| 2 | Several regions reuse assets or tilesets heavily enough to blur together, and the soundtrack is sparse or repetitive enough to fade into background noise within a few hours. | After a while, areas start to feel interchangeable, and you'll stop noticing the music at all. |
| 1 | The world is visually generic with little to distinguish one area from the next, and the soundtrack is minimal, looped without variation, or absent during exploration. | Nothing about where you are or what you're hearing gives the world its own identity. |

# Metric: Story
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** JRPG
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = the main conflict is built with deliberate escalation, where each act raises stakes and revelations recontextualize what came before, 1 = a sequence of events that connects dungeons without building toward anything

## Why This Matters

Story is the single most corroborated theme in the research file, named first or as the framing reason for nearly every other draw the genre has. Players "report getting heavily invested in the storylines of jrpgs, loving how the story escalates," describe them as "bold stories" that are "very different and more creative than western rpgs," and compare playing to "reading a great fantasy book they cant put down." The draw is specifically the narrative itself — the plot's construction, the world's conflict, how stakes are built and paid off — not the NPCs inside it (that's a separate axis).

External analysis identifies the structural reason JRPG plots can do this consistently: unlike western RPGs, "JRPGs use dialogue trees, they have a more linear narrative where the main scenes, most or all of the cutscenes, and the deaths of important characters are usually unique and unavoidable, in addition to the plot almost always culminating in a single ending." That fixed, authored structure gives JRPG writers the same control a novelist has over the timing of revelation — the plot can escalate deliberately because the writer, not the player, controls when information lands. This rubric is checking whether a game uses that control to construct a plot with a genuine arc, or just uses the linear structure to string dungeons together with cutscenes.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The main conflict escalates deliberately from a grounded or local opening to stakes that feel earned at a world or existential level, with revelations that recontextualize events you thought you understood — the plot rewards you for paying attention to its earlier chapters. Final Fantasy VII — opening on a local act of eco-sabotage and escalating to a planet-scale threat, with later revelations reframing the meaning of the first act — is the reference point for this kind of authored plot construction. | You'll find yourself rethinking earlier scenes in light of later reveals, and the world's conflict will feel like it was deliberately built rather than assembled. |
| 4 | A strong, escalating main conflict with one or two stretches that exist to move you between story beats rather than build toward the next one — a filler arc or a chapter that marks time without advancing the stakes. The overall arc still lands. | The plot carries you through, with one or two stretches you'll remember as the slow parts between the important ones. |
| 3 | The conflict is coherent and moves forward but escalates predictably — the next story beat is usually visible before it arrives, and stakes rise in scale (a more powerful villain, a larger threat) without the plot changing what those stakes mean. | You'll follow the story to the end, but it won't surprise you. |
| 2 | The plot functions mainly as a delivery mechanism for the next dungeon or boss — escalation is numerical rather than narrative, with a stronger antagonist at each stage but no new meaning attached to the conflict. | You could summarize the entire plot in one sentence without losing much. |
| 1 | There's a setting and an antagonist, but no real arc — events happen in sequence without building on each other, and the ending doesn't pay off anything that was specifically constructed earlier. | The plot is wallpaper. It explains why you're here, but it doesn't give you a reason to care what happens next. |

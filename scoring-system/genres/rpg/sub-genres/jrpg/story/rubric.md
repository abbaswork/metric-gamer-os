# Metric: Story
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** JRPG
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = the plot escalates with real stakes and stays with you after the credits roll, 1 = a generic fantasy plot that exists only to connect gameplay segments

## Why This Matters

Story is the single most corroborated theme in the research file, by a wide margin. Players "report getting heavily invested in the storylines of jrpgs, loving how the story escalates," and independently describe the genre as having "bold stories," storytelling that's "very different and more creative than western rpgs," and an experience comparable to "reading a great fantasy book they cant put down." One player summarized the appeal directly: what makes JRPGs their favorite is that they're "immersive and make me feel like I'm part of the world as well as delivering a satisfying experience when you complete them." This isn't one quote among many competing themes — across nearly every Reddit factor in the research file, story is named first or named as the reason everything else matters.

External analysis explains the structural reason JRPG stories land this way: unlike western RPGs, which prioritize player-authored outcomes, "JRPGs use dialogue trees, they have a more linear narrative where the main scenes, most or all of the cutscenes, and the deaths of important characters are usually unique and unavoidable, in addition to the plot almost always culminating in a single ending." That fixed, authored structure is precisely what lets a JRPG escalate with the same control a novelist has — stakes can be built deliberately because the writer, not the player, controls the pacing of revelation. This rubric is checking whether a game actually uses that control to build something with a real arc, or just uses the structure to pad time between battles.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The plot escalates deliberately from a grounded or personal opening into stakes that feel earned, with twists that recontextualize earlier events rather than just raising the numbers. Final Fantasy VII — opening on local eco-terrorism and escalating to a planet-threatening conflict, built on revelations that change how you read earlier scenes — is the reference point for this kind of authored escalation. | You'll find yourself thinking about plot turns hours after you've put the controller down. |
| 4 | A strong, escalating main plot with one or two stretches that mark time rather than build the story (a fetch-quest arc, a filler dungeon with no narrative payoff). The ending lands even if the middle sagged. | The story carries you through, with one or two patches where you're playing for the gameplay more than the plot. |
| 3 | The plot is coherent and moves forward but escalates predictably — you can usually see the next story beat coming. Stakes rise in scale (bigger enemy, bigger weapon) more than in meaning. | You'll finish the story satisfied but not surprised. |
| 2 | The plot exists mainly to justify the next dungeon or boss fight, with escalation that's numerical rather than dramatic (a stronger villain with no new stakes attached). Twists, if present, don't recontextualize anything that came before. | You could summarize the plot in one sentence without losing much. |
| 1 | There's a setting and a goal, but no real arc — events happen in sequence without building on each other, and the ending doesn't pay off anything specific that was set up. | The story is wallpaper behind the gameplay, not a reason to keep playing on its own. |

# Metric: Character Development
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** JRPG
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = NPCs and party members each have a distinct arc that deepens through dedicated mechanics as well as cutscenes, 1 = NPCs are interchangeable roles with no arc or development beyond their combat function

## Why This Matters

This metric is specifically about NPCs and party members as individuals — their personal arcs, how they change over the course of the game, and whether the game's mechanics reflect the depth of your relationship with each of them. It's distinct from Story's focus on the main plot and world conflict. The research file names NPC development as a defining JRPG strength in its own right: players love "the conversational/dialogue heavy aspects as it helps to develop character personalities," value "games with multiple character parties," and specifically call out "character development" as a core draw alongside storytelling. Most directly, the genre is praised for games that "provide full character arcs that allow the mechanics of their game to provide a way of backing up the story of their characters" — a phrase that puts mechanics and individual NPC arcs on equal footing.

That last phrase is the precise thing this rubric is checking. Persona 5's Confidant system is the clearest genre example of mechanics directly backing up NPC investment: each NPC bond "is associated with an Arcana and gives an EXP boost to fusing Personas belonging to that Arcana," and neglecting a party NPC's bond "will run into problems later in the game since they will be missing powerful skills." Deepening your relationship with a specific NPC changes what that NPC can do for you in battle — not just what they say in a cutscene. That's the bar: does investing time in an NPC change how they function in your party, or is it a cutscene gate that plays out the same regardless of how much you engage with it?

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Most party NPCs have a dedicated arc with real narrative beats outside the main plot, and investing in a specific NPC ties directly to mechanical growth — new abilities, stat boosts, or combat options unlock as the relationship deepens. Persona 5's Confidant system, where bond rank with each NPC directly powers up your Personas and unlocks party skills, is the reference point. | You'll have a reason to spend time with each NPC that goes beyond their starting kit — and skipping that investment will cost you in combat. |
| 4 | Most party NPCs get real individual arcs and at least some mechanical payoff for investing in them (a unique skill unlocked through a dedicated NPC quest), but one or two NPCs in the roster are thinner — present for combat utility without much of their own story. | Your favourite NPCs will feel fully fleshed out; one or two will feel more like toolkit additions than people. |
| 3 | Party NPCs are distinguishable and get individual scenes or side quests, but investing more deeply in a specific NPC doesn't meaningfully change their abilities or available interactions — NPC story and combat growth run on separate, disconnected tracks. | You'll enjoy the individual NPC writing, but the game won't reward you differently for engaging with it. |
| 2 | NPCs have a defining trait or backstory revealed early but don't develop meaningfully beyond it — one or two scenes establish who they are, and they stay static for the rest of the game. | You'll know who each NPC is, but not who they became. |
| 1 | NPCs are interchangeable beyond their combat role — similar dialogue patterns across the roster, no individual arc, no mechanical link between how much you invest in them and what they offer. | Your party feels like a loadout, not a cast. |

# Metric: Character Development
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** JRPG
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = party members have arcs that visibly evolve through dedicated mechanics, not just cutscenes, 1 = party members are reskinned stat blocks with interchangeable dialogue

## Why This Matters

This metric is about party members as individuals — their arcs and how the game mechanically reflects relationship growth — distinct from Story's focus on plot and stakes. The research file names this as one of the genre's defining strengths, repeated almost word-for-word by multiple respondents: JRPGs are "often the games that I have found to be best able to provide full character arcs that allow the mechanics of their game to provide a way of backing up the story of their characters." Other players cite "storytelling, worldbuilding, and character development" together as a core draw, and specifically value "the conversational/dialogue heavy aspects as it helps to develop character personalities" and games "with multiple character parties."

The phrase "mechanics... backing up the story of their characters" is the precise thing this rubric checks for, and the genre has a well-documented example of doing it directly: Persona 5's Confidant system ties relationship rank to mechanical power, not just dialogue — "each bond the player creates is associated with an Arcana and gives an EXP boost to fusing Personas belonging to that Arcana," and neglecting a party member's bond has a real cost ("they will run into problems later in the game since they will be missing powerful skills"). That's the bar: does deepening a character relationship change what that character can actually do in your party, or is it a cutscene gate with no mechanical weight behind it?

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Most party members have a dedicated arc with real narrative beats, and relationship/bond depth is tied directly to mechanical growth — new skills, stat boosts, or combat options unlock as you invest in a character, not just new dialogue. Persona 5's Confidant system, where bond rank directly powers up your Personas, is the reference point. | You'll have a reason to care about your party beyond their stats — and skipping that investment will visibly cost you in battle. |
| 4 | Most main party members get real arcs and at least a partial mechanical tie-in (a unique skill unlocked through a character quest), but one or two members are thinner — present for combat utility without much story behind them. | Your favourite party members will feel fully realized; one or two will feel more like toolkit additions. |
| 3 | Party members are distinguishable and get individual scenes or side content, but relationship depth doesn't meaningfully change their kit or abilities — character moments and combat growth happen on separate, disconnected tracks. | You'll enjoy the character writing, but investing in a character won't change how they play. |
| 2 | Party members have a defining trait or backstory blurb but little development beyond it — one or two scenes, then they remain static for the rest of the game. | You'll remember who they are, but not who they became. |
| 1 | Party members are interchangeable beyond their combat role — similar dialogue patterns, no individual arc, no mechanical link between story investment and character growth. | Your party feels like a loadout, not a cast. |

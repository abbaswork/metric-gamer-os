# Metric: Length & Value
**Genre:** RPG
**Sub-genre:** JRPG
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = the length is filled with content that escalates rather than repeats, making a long runtime feel like value rather than padding, 1 = the runtime is stretched with repetitive filler that makes the game feel longer than its content justifies

## Why This Matters

Length is one of the few themes in the research file framed explicitly in terms of value, not just enjoyment: players like JRPGs "for the lengthy games so its more cost effective overall," and specifically want "to be able to get lost in a game for days or weeks to help them escape into a different world without worrying that a single play session has taken them to the halfway point, which also makes them feel like they're getting better value for money." That second quote is doing two things at once — naming escapism as the emotional payoff of a long game, and naming cost-per-hour as the practical justification for it.

External analysis confirms this is a real, commonly-cited value calculation, not a niche preference: "In a market where games can cost $70, many players look for a high hour-to-dollar ratio." But the same analysis draws the line this rubric is built around — length alone doesn't guarantee value: "a tightly paced 20-hour RPG can sometimes be more rewarding than a 100-hour game filled with repetitive tasks." The research file's "Free JRPGs" category (Genshin Impact, Honkai: Star Rail, Zenless Zone Zero, Wuthering Waves) and live-service games generally have every incentive to stretch playtime without adding content — which is exactly the failure mode this metric is built to catch. A long game only scores well here if the length comes from genuine content density, not from padding.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | A long runtime (40+ hours) is filled with content that keeps introducing new story beats, areas, or systems rather than repeating earlier ones — the back half of the game doesn't feel like a stretched-out version of the front half. Dragon Quest XI S's structure, where new regions, party abilities, and plot developments keep arriving deep into a 100-hour campaign, is the reference point for length that earns itself. | You'll lose entire days to this game and feel like you got more than your money's worth, not like you were kept busy. |
| 4 | Strong content density for most of the runtime, with one identifiable stretch (a padded mid-game dungeon crawl, a fetch-quest chapter) that exists mainly to extend playtime rather than add anything new. | The length mostly feels earned, with one section you'll remember as the slow part. |
| 3 | The game is reasonably long and has a fair amount of content, but noticeably leans on repetition (similar dungeon layouts, recycled enemy encounters) to fill a meaningful chunk of its runtime. | You'll get a lot of hours out of it, but a chunk of them will feel like more of the same rather than something new. |
| 2 | A significant portion of the runtime is backtracking, grinding, or repeated content with minimal new story or systems attached — the game is padded enough that a shorter cut would lose little. | You'll start to feel like the game is killing time rather than telling its story. |
| 1 | The length comes primarily from repetition — grinding, filler dungeons, or fetch quests with no narrative or mechanical payoff — rather than from genuine content. The runtime feels inflated relative to what the game actually has to offer. | You'll finish wondering what you actually got out of all those hours. |

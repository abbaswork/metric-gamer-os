# Metric: Skill Ceiling
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = a genuinely high mastery ceiling with low enough entry friction that players survive long enough to discover it, 1 = shallow mastery or a physics learning curve that alienates newcomers before they find the game

## Why This Matters

What draws people to platform fighters is not just that they are easy to play — it is that they are hard to master. Brawlhalla players describe a skill ceiling that is "high but not unobtainable," where aerial reads, combo extensions, DI, and off-stage play take a substantial amount of time to genuinely develop. That ceiling is the long-term reason to stay. The accessibility dimension matters because a ceiling that new players can never reach stops being a feature and starts being a wall. The design challenge is sequencing the two: simple enough at entry that players survive their first hour, deep enough at the top that veterans have somewhere to go.

Off-stage survival and recovery are essential mechanics for new players to pick up: understanding blast zones and jump-count management determines whether early sessions feel participatory or frustrating. The clearest sign of entry friction being too high is new players dying to the physics rather than to opponents. A well-designed platform fighter has enough stage safety and generous blast zone sizing that this doesn't happen — Brawlhalla achieves this through "simple controls and no huge information wall." The ceiling dimension is what separates good platform fighters from ones that fade quickly: "skill ceiling is high but not unobtainable" is a compliment, not a mixed review.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The ceiling is genuinely high — aerial reads, off-stage play, and combo extensions take a substantial amount of time to master and the game rewards that investment with visible performance differences. Entry friction is low enough that new players are landing hits and understanding the goal within the first match, dying to opponents rather than to the physics. | You're participating immediately. A year later you're still improving. |
| 4 | The mastery ceiling is real and clearly surfaced over time. Veterans have meaningful room to grow and skilled play is visibly better than casual play. New players take a little longer to find their footing — a few early stocks are lost to the physics — but training tools or in-game guidance close that gap quickly. | Getting started is smooth. The ceiling is real and rewards long-term investment. |
| 3 | The ceiling exists but takes deliberate effort to discover. The game doesn't surface its depth well and casual players can plateau without realising there is more to learn. Entry friction is moderate — platform physics take longer than they should to become intuitive. | The depth is real but you have to find it yourself. The first hour is harder than it needs to be. |
| 2 | The skill ceiling is either shallow enough that dedicated players stop improving within a few weeks, or the entry physics are steep enough that new players struggle to tell whether they are losing to the game or to their opponent. One dimension is failing. | Either mastery runs out quickly or getting started is too punishing. The game doesn't deliver on both ends. |
| 1 | Shallow mastery and a hostile entry experience. The platform physics are unintuitive enough that new players can't get traction, and advanced play doesn't meaningfully separate from casual play once the basics are learned. | The physics are the obstacle before any opponent is, and clearing them doesn't reveal much depth on the other side. |

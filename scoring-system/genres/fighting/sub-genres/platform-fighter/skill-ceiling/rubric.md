# Metric: Skill Ceiling
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = a genuinely high mastery ceiling with low enough entry friction that players survive long enough to discover it, 1 = shallow mastery or a physics learning curve that alienates newcomers before they find the game

## Why This Matters

What draws people to platform fighters is not just that they are easy to play — it is that they are hard to master. Brawlhalla players describe a skill ceiling that is "high but not unobtainable," where aerial reads, combo extensions, DI, and off-stage play take a substantial amount of time to genuinely develop. That ceiling is the long-term reason to stay. The accessibility dimension matters because a ceiling that new players can never reach stops being a feature and starts being a wall. The design challenge is sequencing the two: simple enough at entry that players survive their first hour, deep enough at the top that veterans have somewhere to go.

Off-stage survival and recovery are essential mechanics for new players to pick up: understanding blast zones and jump-count management determines whether early sessions feel participatory or frustrating. A well-designed platform fighter has enough stage safety and generous blast zone sizing at the early level that new players die to opponents, not to the physics. Brawlhalla achieves this through "simple controls and no huge information wall" — button-based specials and a weapon system that reduces the concept space enough that new players are participating and hitting things within minutes.

The ceiling dimension is what separates good platform fighters from ones that fade quickly. A game that is easy to pick up but shallow at mastery has only solved half the problem. The community signal is clear: "skill ceiling is high but not unobtainable" is a compliment, not a mixed review.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The skill ceiling is genuinely high. Aerial reads, combo extensions, off-stage play, and DI take a substantial amount of time to master and the game actively rewards that investment with visible performance differences between skill levels. Entry friction is low enough that new players are landing hits and understanding the goal within the first match — controls are discoverable, the physics communicate themselves, and new players die to opponents rather than to the edge — losing early stocks to the physics rather than to an opponent is the clearest sign of entry friction being too high. | You're participating immediately. A year later you're still improving. |
| 4 | The mastery ceiling is real and clearly surfaced over time. Veterans have meaningful room to grow and skilled play is visibly better than casual play. New players take a little longer to find their footing — early stocks are sometimes lost to the edge — but training tools or in-game guidance help close that gap quickly. | Getting started is smooth. The ceiling is real and rewards long-term investment. |
| 3 | The ceiling exists but takes deliberate effort to discover. The game does not surface its depth well and casual players can plateau without realising there is more to learn. Entry friction is moderate: platform physics, particularly aerial drift and blast zone positions, take longer than they should to become intuitive. | The depth is real but you have to find it yourself. The first hour is harder than it needs to be. |
| 2 | The skill ceiling is either shallow enough that dedicated players stop improving within a few weeks, or the entry physics are steep enough that new players struggle to tell whether they are losing to the game or to their opponent. One of the two dimensions is failing. | Either mastery runs out quickly or getting started is too punishing. The game does not deliver on both ends. |
| 1 | Shallow mastery and a hostile entry experience. The platform physics are unintuitive or inconsistent enough that new players cannot get traction, and advanced play does not meaningfully separate from casual play once the basics are learned. | The physics system is the obstacle before any opponent is, and clearing it does not reveal much depth on the other side. |

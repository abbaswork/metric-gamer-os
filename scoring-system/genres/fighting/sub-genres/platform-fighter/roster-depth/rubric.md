# Metric: Roster Depth
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = large, distinctive roster where player identity and playstyle form around individual picks, 1 = minimal roster where character choices feel interchangeable

## Why This Matters

In platform fighters, roster depth carries the same identity function as in any fighting game — "The roster is the primary thing that draws players into a game, people need to be fans of the characters" — but with a platform-fighter-specific dimension: weapon or ability sets that change how aerial movement and combos feel. In Brawlhalla, each Legend (character) is defined by two weapons, and while the weapon movesets are shared, the stats and signature moves create distinct play patterns: "Every character is unique because of the sigs and stats, but you always can always rely on the two weapons with light attacks." This means roster depth in a platform fighter includes not just character count but weapon/archetype variety.

Brawlhalla's roster is the high-water mark in the research: brawlhalla legends at 88 search interest (+90%) is the single strongest signal in the fighting research file. Players search their main constantly. The roster has grown past 90 Legends and continues expanding — "constant updates" is a cited reason for the game's ongoing appeal.

The viability dimension also applies here: a large roster where most Legends share the same top-tier weapons or are outclassed by a small competitive pool is functionally a much smaller roster for players who care about more than aesthetics.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Extensive roster covering a wide range of archetypes — different weight classes, movement speeds, aerial approaches, and weapon or ability types create genuinely distinct playstyles. Picking a main is a meaningful identity decision. Viability is spread broadly enough that off-meta characters are real choices. The roster grows post-launch with additions that bring new weapon types, movement patterns, or archetypes rather than just adding headcount. | You'll spend real time deciding who to main. Discovering new characters stays interesting long after launch. |
| 4 | Strong roster with real variety across archetypes and weapon types. A few characters feel redundant or underdeveloped but the overall cast gives most players someone to genuinely invest in. Post-launch content has added meaningfully. | You'll find your main without much friction. The cast has personality even if a few slots are filler. |
| 3 | Functional roster covering expected archetypes without surprise. Character variety exists on paper but a significant portion share similar aerial approaches or effective strategies. A few weapon types or movement archetypes dominate character select in practice. | The roster gets the job done. Picking a character is a practical decision more than an expressive one. |
| 2 | Shallow roster with limited genuine variety. Multiple characters share kits closely enough that playing as different ones doesn't produce different experiences. Competitive viability clusters around a few picks, shrinking effective choice further. | You'll run out of interesting picks quickly. The roster isn't the reason to stay. |
| 1 | Minimal roster that fails to serve the genre. Characters are barely distinguishable or so few that player identity can't form around them. | The cast is the game's most obvious weakness from the moment you open character select. |

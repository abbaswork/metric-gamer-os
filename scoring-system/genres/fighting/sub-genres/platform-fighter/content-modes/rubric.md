# Metric: Content & Modes
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = rich modes, training tools, and ongoing post-launch content with platform-fighter-specific depth, 1 = bare-bones versus mode with nothing to support long-term engagement

## Why This Matters

Platform fighters share the same core content challenge as any fighting game — "players are more likely to stick with a game that has satisfying single-player content" — but with specific mode types that matter most in this sub-genre. Platform fighters attract groups of local players in a way traditional fighters don't, making local party modes (free-for-all, teams, special rule variants) a meaningful part of the genre's content offering. Brawlhalla's content volume is a direct cited reason for its ongoing appeal: "constant updates", "the grind is rewarding", "It has a HUGE esports scene that the devs lean into", "tons of battlepasses and cosmetics."

Training tools in platform fighters need to cover aerial mechanics specifically — not just combo notation but DI practice, off-stage scenarios, recovery options. A training mode that only covers ground-level basics misses the most important skill dimensions of the sub-genre.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Rich mode variety covering competitive 1v1, team play, and local party modes — with special rule variants that change the game meaningfully (stock, timed, teams, items). Training mode covers aerial mechanics, combo practice, and recovery scenarios — not just ground-level basics. Single-player content offers a complete experience. Active post-launch support: new characters, stages, and modes arrive regularly. The cosmetic system gives players long-term goals without affecting gameplay. | The game gives you a reason to play regardless of who you're playing with — alone, locally, or online. |
| 4 | Strong mode variety with one or two gaps. Local modes work well. Training mode covers the essentials. Post-launch support has been meaningful. Single-player content is present but not deep. | The content holds you for months. The gaps are noticeable once you've exhausted what's there. |
| 3 | Adequate modes at launch with limited depth. Training mode covers basics without aerial-specific tools. Local modes exist but don't vary much. Post-launch updates have been infrequent or mostly cosmetic. Single-player content is thin or absent. | The content holds you for the first weeks. The game doesn't give you many reasons to return after that. |
| 2 | Thin mode offering at launch. Training mode is minimal — a practice dummy without useful data. Local party modes are limited or missing. Post-launch support hasn't added meaningful content. | The game shows its edges fast. It works as a 1v1 mode and not much more. |
| 1 | Bare-bones at launch with no meaningful post-launch support. Versus mode only, no training tools, no local party modes, nothing added after release. | You've seen everything the game offers within the first few sessions. |

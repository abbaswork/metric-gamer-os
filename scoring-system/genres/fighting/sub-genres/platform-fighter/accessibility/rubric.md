# Metric: Accessibility
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = new players are immediately participating and enjoying, mastery ceiling remains genuinely high, 1 = physics learning curve alienates newcomers before they find the game

## Why This Matters

Platform fighters have a different accessibility challenge than traditional fighters. The barrier isn't motion inputs — platform fighters typically have simple, button-based specials. The barrier is **platform movement physics**: weight, aerial drift, jump timing, fastfall, and the concept of blast zones rather than health bars. These are intuitions that don't transfer from any other game genre and can only be learned through play.

Brawlhalla is the benchmark example of this solved well: "easy to pick up, harder to master", "simple controls and no huge information wall", "skill ceiling is high but not unobtainable." Its simple weapon-based control scheme means new players are participating and hitting things within minutes, while advanced aerial mechanics, combo routes, and off-stage reads take hundreds of hours to genuinely master.

The specific failure mode for platform fighters is the **early off-stage death loop**: new players repeatedly lose stocks by walking or drifting off the edge before understanding blast zones, or failing to recover because they don't yet understand jump-count management. This is discouraging in a way that has nothing to do with being outplayed — it's the physics system teaching players in the most punishing way possible. A well-designed platform fighter has enough stage safety and generous blast zone sizing at the early level that new players die to opponents, not to the physics.

The ceiling dimension matters equally. Brawlhalla's "skill ceiling is high but not unobtainable" — the aerial reads, combo extensions, DI, and off-stage play take real long-term investment to develop. A platform fighter that's easy to pick up but shallow at the top has only solved half the problem.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | New players understand the goal (knock opponents off stage) immediately and are landing hits within the first match. Controls are simple enough to be discoverable — specials, jumps, and basic attacks work without a tutorial. The physics feel intuitive: character weight communicates itself, aerial drift is predictable, and the blast zones make sense. New players die to opponents, not to the edge, within the first hour. At high level, the skill ceiling is genuinely high — aerial reads, combo extensions, and off-stage play take hundreds of hours to master and the game rewards that investment. | You're participating immediately. A year later you're still improving. |
| 4 | Controls are accessible and new players are contributing within a session. The physics take a little longer — early stocks are lost to edge accidents, but players learn the blast zones within the first few matches. Training tools or in-game guidance help close the gap. The skill ceiling is real but not fully surfaced through in-game tools. | Getting started is smooth. Veterans have meaningful room to grow. |
| 3 | Controls are simple but the platform physics — particularly aerial drift and blast zone positions — take longer than they should to become intuitive. New players spend their early matches dying to the stage more than opponents. The skill ceiling exists but discovering it requires knowing to look for it. | The first hour is harder than it needs to be. The depth is real but you have to find it yourself. |
| 2 | The physics learning curve is steep enough that new players struggle to tell whether they're losing to the game or to their opponent. Aerial drift, weight, or blast zone sizing are inconsistent or poorly communicated. The early experience is dominated by the physics system rather than player competition. | The game doesn't cooperate with newcomers. The early experience is friction without payoff. |
| 1 | Platform physics are unintuitive or inconsistent enough that new players can't get traction. Recovery and blast zone systems aren't learnable through normal play. Players disengage before the game gives them a reason to stay. | The physics system is the obstacle before any opponent is. |

# Metric: Competitive Balance
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = healthy meta where a meaningful number of characters are viable at high level and off-stage interactions are interactive on both sides, 1 = dominant character tier or one-sided off-stage dynamics remove meaningful choice

## Why This Matters

Competitive balance in platform fighters operates across two distinct axes that do not exist in traditional fighters. The first is character and roster viability: how many options are genuinely playable at high level? The benchmark is precise: "a multiplayer game is balanced if a reasonably large number of options available to the player are viable, especially during high-level play" (Sirlin.net). The second is off-stage balance: platform fighters are unique in that losing a stock often happens off the edge, not on the stage. If recovery options are so weak or edgeguard tools so dominant that off-stage exchanges are one-sided, the game's most skill-expressive phase breaks down.

Character viability expresses itself differently depending on the game's structure. In Rivals of Aether 2, the balance question is straightforwardly about characters: the June 2026 meta has Clairen considered the best in the game due to "insane range, stunning tippers, fast pressure, and deadly auto-combos," while the gap to the rest of the cast is still moderate enough that "there are no bad characters, but some clearly outshine the rest." In Brawlhalla, the balance question operates at the weapon level as well as the character level: strong weapons mean more stable matchups across multiple legends, and weapons like Gauntlets and Scythe have maintained the highest presence at esports level across multiple meta cycles. The weapon dimension is Brawlhalla-specific but reflects the same underlying balance question: how many of the game's competitive options are genuinely viable?

Off-stage balance is the platform-fighter-unique dimension. Edgeguarding "begins once a character is knocked off-stage and is the attempt to prevent your opponent from recovering" (SmashWiki). The balance concern is whether the tools on each side of this exchange are proportionate. Recoveries with effectively unlimited range, or edgeguard tools that cover every recovery option without counterplay, create a one-sided dynamic that removes skill expression from the game's most contested phase. Brawlhalla stats illustrate why first-stock advantage compounds: matches where a player secures the first KO carry a 68.3% win probability, rising to 74.1% at high Elo. A first-stock advantage gained through an imbalanced off-stage tool is therefore not just a single inequity — it shapes the match from that point forward.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | A meaningful number of characters are viable at high level — the tier list exists but the gap between tiers is narrow enough that off-meta play is a real competitive choice. Off-stage interactions are two-sided: viable recovery options exist, edgeguard tools have counterplay, and the off-stage phase rewards skill on both sides. Patches respond to documented imbalances without overcorrecting. First-stock advantages feel earned rather than guaranteed by toolkit asymmetry. | Who you play matters for identity and style. The meta shapes high-level play without defining the experience below it. |
| 4 | Mostly balanced with a clear top tier that is not mandatory. Off-meta characters are viable with skill. Off-stage interactions are mostly two-sided with one or two recovery options or edgeguard tools that are notably stronger than the rest but not dominant. Patches address the most egregious imbalances within a reasonable cycle. | The tier list is real but not a ceiling. Skilled off-meta play beats weaker top-tier play regularly. |
| 3 | Clear tier clustering. A subset of characters or weapon types appear significantly more often at high level than roster share would suggest. Off-stage interactions have a few dominant options that experienced players consistently route for, but counterplay still exists. Off-meta play is possible but carries a measurable cost. | The tier list will find you before long. Off-stage you will start recognising the same patterns being run on you. |
| 2 | The character or weapon meta is narrow enough that competitive play converges on the same set of picks. Off-stage interactions have dominant options with limited counterplay on the defending side. The tier list gap is wide enough that off-meta play is a consistent disadvantage at any level of play where opponents know the meta. | Ranked play teaches you the tier list faster than it teaches you the game. Off-stage often feels like a foregone conclusion. |
| 1 | One character cluster or toolkit option is dominant enough to remove meaningful choice. Off-stage has effective one-sided solutions at competitive level with no viable counterplay. The imbalance has been publicly identified with no credible fix in progress. | Character select is the match's most important moment. What you pick matters more than how you play. |

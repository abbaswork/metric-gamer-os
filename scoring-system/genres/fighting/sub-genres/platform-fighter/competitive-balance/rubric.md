# Metric: Competitive Balance
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = healthy meta where character and weapon choices feel meaningful, 1 = dominant tier removes meaningful choice

## Why This Matters

Competitive balance in platform fighters carries the same core concern as in traditional fighters — players hate discovering their main is "booty" — but with platform-fighter-specific dynamics. In Brawlhalla, balance operates at the weapon level as much as the character level: if two weapons are universally superior, the entire roster collapses to whichever Legends carry those weapons. Brawlhalla was specifically praised for avoiding this: "It's extremely balanced at high ranks, which isn't really the case for a lot of other platform fighters" — the comparison is to the observable ranked experience, not design intent.

The off-stage balance dimension is also specific to this sub-genre: if one recovery option or edge-guard tool is dominant enough to remove counterplay from off-stage exchanges, it effectively breaks the most skill-expressive part of the game. A recovery that is unbeatable, or an edge-guard that covers every option, turns the game's richest tactical moment into a foregone conclusion.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Healthy meta at both character and weapon levels — no weapon type is so dominant that character select collapses to whoever carries it. Off-stage exchanges feel interactive with viable options on both sides. Tier lists exist but the gap is narrow enough that off-meta play is a real choice, not a handicap. Patches respond to documented imbalances without creating new ones. | Who you play matters for identity, not survival. Going off-meta is possible without feeling punished for it. |
| 4 | Mostly balanced with a few known strong weapon types or characters. Off-meta play is viable. The top tier is clear but not mandatory — skilled players win off-meta regularly. Off-stage interactions have a few dominant options but counterplay exists. | The meta shapes high-level play without defining your experience below it. |
| 3 | Clear tier clustering visible in ranked results. Certain weapon types appear significantly more often than roster share would suggest. Off-stage interactions have a few known dominant options that experienced players route for. Off-meta play is possible but carries a measurable cost. | The tier list is real and you'll run into it before long. |
| 2 | The weapon or character meta is narrow enough that competitive play regularly converges on the same set of picks. Off-stage interactions have dominant options with no reliable counterplay. The tier list gap is wide enough that off-meta play is a consistent disadvantage. | Ranked play teaches you the tier list faster than it teaches you the off-stage game. |
| 1 | One weapon type or character cluster is dominant enough to remove meaningful choice. Off-stage play has effective one-sided solutions at competitive level. The meta has broken down in ways the community has publicly flagged with no credible fix in progress. | Character select is the match's most important moment. What you pick matters more than how you play. |

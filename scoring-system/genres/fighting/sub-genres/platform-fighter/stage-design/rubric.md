# Metric: Stage Design
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = rich, varied stage pool that creates distinct neutral game experiences and a healthy competitive selection, 1 = stages are interchangeable backdrops with no impact on gameplay

## Why This Matters

Stage design is unique to platform fighters — in traditional fighters, the stage is a backdrop. In platform fighters, it's a gameplay dimension. Platform height, quantity, and spacing directly change how the neutral game is played: a stage with one central platform creates different approach angles than one with three platforms at varying heights. Stage blast zone sizing determines how long stocks last. Ledge positions affect recovery options and edge-guarding angles.

The competitive stage pool introduces a strategic layer absent from traditional fighters. Not all stages function as neutral ground: "Some stages should be counterpick stages exclusively because they are less 'Neutral' than other stages, offering certain characters significant advantages over other characters." The principle: "The neutrality of a stage is inversely proportional to the degree to which the stage skews the matchup between any two characters." A healthy stage pool means matchups play out differently based on stage choice — counterpick stages are selected deliberately because the layout favours a character or punishes an opponent's. This adds a strategic dimension before the match begins.

Moving platforms and stage hazards create variety but raise fairness questions specific to this genre. Stages with active hazards are either embraced as casual elements or banned by competitive consensus. How the game handles this split determines whether the full stage list is usable or effectively reduced to a small competitive subset by community convention. Casually, stage variety keeps sessions fresh — playing the same stage every match is the fastest way to reduce a platform fighter to its most basic level.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Stage layouts vary enough that matchup dynamics shift noticeably by stage — a high-platform stage plays differently from a flat one and players can feel it. The competitive pool is broad enough that stage selection is a genuine strategic decision before the match starts. Hazard stages feel playful rather than arbitrary in casual play. Post-launch stages expand the pool without just adding more of the same. | Stage select is a genuine decision, not a formality. Different stages feel like different games within the same ruleset. |
| 4 | Strong stage variety with a solid competitive pool. A few stages feel clearly superior or inferior in competitive play but the overall selection is healthy. Hazard stages are fun in casual contexts. Blast zone sizing is broadly consistent. | Stage variety is a genuine strength with a few weak entries. The competitive pool is workable. |
| 3 | Adequate stages but limited configuration variety — platform layouts repeat enough that most stages feel like minor variants of each other. The competitive pool narrows quickly to 3-4 frequently played stages. Hazard stages are either always banned or always on by convention, reducing the usable pool further. | The stages serve the game without adding to it. Players stop noticing stage variety fairly quickly. |
| 2 | Few viable stages with minimal layout variety. The competitive pool collapses to 1-2 stages. Most hazard stages feel unfair rather than fun — the chaos doesn't feel earned. | Stage select doesn't matter because there's rarely a meaningful choice. |
| 1 | Stage design doesn't function as a gameplay system. Stages are effectively identical or so unfair that play collapses to a single arena. Stage select is cosmetic. | The stages are backdrops, not gameplay. |

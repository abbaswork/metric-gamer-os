# Metric: Combat Feedback
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = aerial hits, kill confirmation, and movement all communicate clearly enough that the system feels authored, 1 = aerial play feels uncertain and outcomes feel like the game's decision rather than yours

## Why This Matters

Platform fighters are primarily aerial games — most combat happens off the ground, and the movement system is the game. "Movement feels good" is how Brawlhalla players describe the experience when it works, and the research on what makes fighting games satisfying comes back to the same place: "Control. Or at least the illusion of it."

In practice this means several things that don't exist in other fighting sub-genres. Kill confirmation — the moment a launch move connects near the blast zone — is the highest-stakes feedback moment in any match, and it needs to be unambiguous. Players need to know the stock is gone before the respawn animation, not because of it. Jump arcs need to be consistent across jump counts so off-stage play can be planned rather than guessed. Knockback direction needs to be readable at the moment of contact — unexpected survivals at high percentage and kill moves that feel random rather than earned are the symptoms of knockback that isn't communicating. And dodges need to feel legible to both the person using them and the person reading them.

When all of this works, the aerial system stops being something you think about and becomes a language you read in real time. When it doesn't, plays that should feel authored start feeling like the game's fault.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Hits feel physical and distinct — aerial impacts produce a clear felt response, kill moves feel categorically different from strong non-kill hits, and you know the stock is gone before the respawn animation confirms it. Jump arcs are consistent enough to plan off-stage play. Knockback direction is readable at contact. Dodges are legible and punishable with the right read. | The aerial system stops being something you think about. It becomes a language you read in real time. |
| 4 | The aerial system mostly communicates clearly. Kill confirmation is present but occasionally less distinct than it should be, or one or two characters feel inconsistently weighted against the rest of the cast. Knockback is readable in most situations with occasional exceptions. Inputs register reliably. | You feel in control quickly. The exceptions stand out because the baseline is solid. |
| 3 | The aerial mechanics work without fully communicating. Kill confirmation exists but doesn't feel categorically different from a strong hit — stock losses are confirmed by the animation more than by the impact. Knockback direction is roughly readable but produces enough unexpected outcomes to feel uncertain at high percentages. Movement feels functional without feeling good. | The physics work. You won't be stopped by them, but you won't always trust them either. |
| 2 | Off-stage play starts feeling like guesswork. Kill confirmation is absent or delayed enough that stock losses regularly feel like the game's decision rather than yours. Knockback direction is unreliable enough that high-percentage situations feel unpredictable. Input drops affect aerial sequences. | Off-stage deaths start feeling like the game's fault more than your own. |
| 1 | The aerial system fights back before any opponent does. Inputs drop in aerial sequences, aerial physics produce unpredictable outcomes, kill confirmation is absent, and hits don't clearly distinguish from whiffs. You can't develop reliable reads on anything the game is doing. | The game fights back before any opponent does. |

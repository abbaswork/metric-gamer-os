# Metric: Gameplay Depth
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Platform Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = rich off-stage and aerial game that rewards reads and long-term mastery, 1 = shallow with no meaningful tactical layer beyond basic hitting

## Why This Matters

Platform fighters have a fundamentally different tactical structure from traditional fighters. The win condition is physical displacement rather than health depletion — you win by launching opponents beyond the blast zones — which creates a distinct set of skill dimensions centred around off-stage play, stock management, and aerial positioning rather than frame data and footsies.

The dimensions that define depth in this sub-genre:

**Edge-guarding:** Once an opponent is off stage, both players enter an entirely separate game. The edge-guarder chooses between going off-stage (high risk, high reward), covering options from the ledge, or waiting on stage. The recovering player chooses their recovery angle, timing, and whether to challenge the edge-guarder directly. This is a distinct tactical conversation that requires reading your opponent — not just executing.

**DI (Directional Influence):** The ability to influence knockback trajectory while being hit is a survival skill. Defenders use DI to escape combos at low percent and extend survival windows at high percent. Attackers must read and account for opponent DI to maintain follow-up opportunities — the same exchange plays out on both sides of every hit. Learning what good DI feels like is a long-term process — and recognising it in opponents is a high-skill read.

**Stock management:** Platform fighters create substantial opportunities to shift strategy based on distinct game states. Playing with a lead requires different risk calibration than playing from a deficit — a game that rewards this adaptation gives players a meaningful strategic dimension beyond landing the next hit.

**Platform tech:** Using platforms to extend combos, escape pressure, or change aerial approach angles creates variety within established matchups. Platform layout and a player's ability to use it separates intermediate from advanced play.

Brawlhalla earned specific community praise for getting this right: "It's extremely balanced at high ranks, which isn't really the case for a lot of other platform fighters" — the balance is tied directly to off-stage play being genuinely interactive rather than one-sided.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Off-stage play is a genuine two-sided tactical dimension — the recovering player has multiple viable recovery angles and the edge-guarder has multiple intercept options, making the exchange a read rather than a formula. DI meaningfully extends survival and creates a skill layer that rewards long-term investment. Platform tech opens combo routes and escape paths that intermediate players miss. Stock management creates distinct game states: risk tolerance correctly shifts based on the count. The highest levels feel like chess played at high speed. | You'll still be learning this game a year in. Each opponent teaches you something new about the off-stage game. |
| 4 | Clear tactical depth in both neutral and off-stage. Edge-guarding exists as a viable two-sided strategy with counterplay. DI is meaningful. Platform tech works and rewards learning. A few situations feel predetermined — specific recoveries are effectively unbeatable edge-guards, or one approach option is overtuned. | High-level play is genuinely engaging. The gaps exist but don't define the experience. |
| 3 | Tactical depth is present but the off-stage game is underdeveloped — recovery is strong enough that edge-guarding rarely pays off, or edge-guards are strong enough that recoveries rarely succeed. Stage control creates some pressure without making positioning feel decisive. DI exists but the difference between good and bad DI isn't perceptible enough in play to drive learning it. | The depth is there for players who pursue it. Normal play doesn't push you toward it. |
| 2 | Off-stage play is effectively one-sided — either recoveries are too strong to contest or too weak to matter, removing the interactive element. Blast zones are generous enough that stock management doesn't shape decision-making. Platform tech exists but produces no meaningful outcome difference. | The game reduces to whoever lands the kill confirm first. Stock count doesn't change how it plays. |
| 1 | No meaningful tactical layer beyond basic aerial hitting. Off-stage is not a game state. Stock count doesn't change play. Platform positions create no pressure. Mastery produces no observable difference in outcomes against average play. | The skill ceiling arrives within hours. There's nothing left to learn after the basics. |

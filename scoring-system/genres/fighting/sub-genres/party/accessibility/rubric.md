# Metric: Accessibility
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Party Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = anyone picks up a controller and is having fun within one match, 1 = requires explanation and learning time before it becomes enjoyable

## Why This Matters

Accessibility in party fighters has a completely different benchmark than in competitive fighting games. The question isn't "can a new player compete in ranked mode within a week" — it's "can someone who hasn't played a video game this year pick up a controller at a house party and immediately be having fun." The genre lives or dies by this. Party fighters are sold on the premise of group entertainment — if one or two people at the table need to sit out a few rounds while others explain the controls, the format has already failed.

The controls need to map to physical intuition. Gang Beasts achieves this because punching, grabbing, and throwing correspond to buttons that feel like those actions — players who don't know the button layout can usually figure out the core interactions through experimentation within minutes. The goal needs to be immediately apparent without explanation. And critically, a new player's first few matches need to feel fun even when losing — the chaos needs to be entertaining independently of outcome.

Unlike competitive fighting games, there's no meaningful skill ceiling concern here — the party fighter genre doesn't need to reward hundreds of hours of investment. But the floor matters enormously: if the game takes 20 minutes to explain before it's enjoyable, it isn't a party fighter.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Someone who hasn't played a video game in years picks up the controller and is participating and laughing within the first match. Controls map to physical intuition — punch, grab, throw are discoverable through experimentation without instruction. The goal is immediately apparent. Losing is still entertaining. The first match is the best possible advertisement for the second. | You can hand anyone a controller and immediately have a good time. Explaining the game is optional. |
| 4 | Controls are accessible but one or two mechanics benefit from a brief verbal explanation before they click. New players are having fun by the end of the first match, with a small friction window at the very start. | Most people pick it up immediately. A quick "press this to grab" gets the last person up to speed. |
| 3 | New players can participate but need 5-10 minutes of explanation or experimentation before the game becomes reliably fun. The first match or two may feel confusing. The chaos doesn't land until the controls are understood well enough to make intentional choices. | It gets there — but the setup time is longer than a party fighter should require. |
| 2 | Controls require enough learning time that new players spend their first session figuring out inputs rather than enjoying the game. The genre assumption is that players already know game conventions that casually or non-gamers don't have. | The game works for people who already play games. It's not actually a party game without some prior gaming background. |
| 1 | The controls and mechanics create enough initial confusion that new or casual players disengage before the game has a chance to be fun. Not suitable for mixed-experience groups without significant upfront investment. | The accessibility promise of the genre isn't met. This isn't a pick-up-and-play game. |

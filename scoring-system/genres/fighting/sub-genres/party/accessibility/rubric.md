# Metric: Accessibility
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Party Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = anyone picks up a controller and is having fun within one match, 1 = requires explanation and learning time before it becomes enjoyable

## Why This Matters

Accessibility in party fighters has a completely different benchmark than in competitive fighting games. The question is not "can a new player compete in ranked mode within a week" — it is "can someone who has not played a video game this year pick up a controller at a house party and immediately be having fun." The genre lives or dies by this standard.

Intuitive controls are the mechanism. Gang Beasts achieves this because punching, grabbing, and throwing correspond to buttons that feel like those actions — players who don't know the button layout discover the core interactions through experimentation. The goal needs to be immediately apparent without explanation, and the research confirms players do this: the volume of searches for how to drop kick, punch, and throw in Gang Beasts exists because the payoff of discovering those interactions through play is real enough to motivate looking them up after the fact.

Losing must also be entertaining. In a party fighter, the chaos needs to produce entertainment independently of outcome — "sharing moments of failure with friends or other players creates laughter, empathy, and stronger social bonds." A new player losing their first match should still be enjoying what happened to them. The research captures the balance precisely: "allowing newbs to play without making it feel like a punishment to vets while being deep enough to enjoy for years to come."

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | No one needs to explain the game. Someone who hasn't played picks up a controller, finds what the buttons do through trying them, and is having fun before the first match ends. Losing is part of the entertainment — the chaos produces moments worth reacting to regardless of outcome. | Hand anyone a controller. You don't need to explain it first. |
| 4 | The game clicks for most people without explanation, though one or two mechanics take a moment to discover. New players are having fun before the first match ends — the brief learning window doesn't get in the way of the group's experience. Losing is still entertaining. | Most people find it immediately. The one person who needs a tip is having fun within minutes. |
| 3 | New players can participate but the game doesn't quite land until someone explains it or they've had a few matches to figure out the controls. The chaos is fun once you're in it — getting there takes longer than a party fighter should. | It gets fun once players are in it, but you'll spend the first few minutes explaining rather than playing. |
| 2 | The game works for people who already play games. Casual or non-gamers spend their first session figuring out what to do rather than enjoying what's happening. The genre promise of pick-up-and-play doesn't hold for mixed-experience groups. | Works for gamers. Doesn't hold up as a party game without some gaming background in the room. |
| 1 | Not a pick-up-and-play game. The controls and mechanics create enough initial confusion that casual players disengage before the game has a chance to be fun. The accessibility promise of the genre is not met. | This is not actually a party game. The entry barrier is too high for the context it's designed for. |

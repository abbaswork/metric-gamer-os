# Metric: Accessibility
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Party Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (observable signals)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = anyone picks up a controller and is having fun within one match, 1 = requires explanation and learning time before it becomes enjoyable

## Why This Matters

Accessibility in party fighters has a completely different benchmark than in competitive fighting games. The question is not "can a new player compete in ranked mode within a week" — it is "can someone who has not played a video game this year pick up a controller at a house party and immediately be having fun." Party games "solve a specific, difficult design problem: create an experience that works simultaneously for people who have never played a strategy game in their lives and for people who own a hundred of them" (neutronium.games). The genre lives or dies by this standard.

Intuitive controls are the mechanism. "Intuitive gameplay lowers the entry barrier for new players, allowing them to quickly grasp the basics and start enjoying the game without feeling overwhelmed by complex rules or mechanics" (brainsnackgames.com). Gang Beasts achieves this because punching, grabbing, and throwing correspond to buttons that feel like those actions — players who do not know the button layout discover the core interactions through experimentation. The goal needs to be immediately apparent without explanation.

Losing must also be entertaining. "Sharing moments of failure with friends or other players creates laughter, empathy, and stronger social bonds" (bitewinggames.com). In a party fighter, the chaos needs to produce entertainment independently of outcome — a new player losing their first match should still be enjoying what happened to them.

## Observable Signals

| Signal | What to check |
|---|---|
| Discoverable controls | Core actions (attack, grab, throw) are performable through experimentation without reading a tutorial or being told button assignments |
| Immediately apparent goal | A new player can identify what they are supposed to do within the first match without explanation |
| Entertaining loss state | Losing produces a reaction (laughter, surprise, social commentary) rather than frustration — the chaos entertains independently of outcome |

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | All three signals present. Core actions are discoverable through experimentation — a player who has never seen the game can find punch, grab, and throw without instruction. The goal is immediately apparent. Losing is entertaining — the chaos produces moments worth reacting to regardless of outcome. | You can hand anyone a controller and immediately have a good time. Explaining the game is optional. |
| 4 | Controls are accessible but one or two mechanics benefit from a brief verbal explanation before they click. New players are having fun by the end of the first match, with a small friction window at the very start. Losing is still entertaining. | Most people pick it up immediately. The entertaining loss state and clear goal carry players through the brief learning window. |
| 3 | New players can participate but require some explanation or experimentation before the game becomes reliably fun. The chaos does not land until the controls are understood well enough to make intentional choices. | It gets there — but the setup time is longer than a party fighter should require. |
| 2 | Controls require enough learning time that new players spend their first session figuring out inputs rather than enjoying the game. The genre assumption is that players already know game conventions that casual or non-gamers do not have. | The game works for people who already play games. It is not actually a party game without some prior gaming background. |
| 1 | The controls and mechanics create enough initial confusion that new or casual players disengage before the game has a chance to be fun. Not suitable for mixed-experience groups without significant upfront investment. | The accessibility promise of the genre is not met. This is not a pick-up-and-play game. |

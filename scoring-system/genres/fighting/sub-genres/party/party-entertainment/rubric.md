# Metric: Party Entertainment
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Party Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = the game generates stories players retell after the session ends, 1 = sessions are forgettable in the moment and leave no impression after

## Why This Matters

Party fighters are judged by a single standard that no other fighting sub-genre is: whether they generate moments worth retelling. "Did you see what just happened?" is the metric. The genre's most successful examples — Gang Beasts, Stick Fight — have specific reputations built entirely on the moments they produce: the player hanging off a moving object who gets rescued by accident, the four-person melee on a collapsing stage that ends with everyone falling simultaneously. These aren't competitive achievements — they're stories.

The design mechanism behind this is documented: party games succeed through humor as intrinsic motivation rather than scores and points — "the points don't matter" (alexiamandeville.medium.com). The moments that become retellable emerge from player interaction with physics and environment rather than from designer-controlled outcomes: "it's different each time based on the group of players you have, the inside jokes or knowledge of the group." The best party games become "friendship tests, inside-joke factories" (wokewaves.com) — the session produces material that outlasts the session itself.

Gang Beasts demonstrates the specific formula: "The game's simple mechanics make it easy to jump into, while its chaotic nature guarantees endless laughs and memorable moments" (thenvm.org). The controls are deliberately unwieldy, but the outputs are specific and retellable — the physics create "a dynamic alliance between conflict and chaos" where every match produces something that couldn't have been scripted. The key design distinction: pure randomness generates forgettable chaos. Party entertainment generates *specific* memorable moments that arise from the interaction of player choices with physics and environment. The best moments feel both surprising and logical in retrospect — "of course that happened given what everyone was doing."

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The game generates specific, memorable moments that players retell after the session ends. Situations arise that couldn't have been scripted — a last-second escape, an accidental elimination chain, an absurd physics outcome that everyone saw coming and still couldn't prevent. Moments feel earned even when they're ridiculous: the physics obeyed rules, the players made choices, the outcome surprised everyone anyway. "Did you see what just happened?" fires multiple times per session. Players suggest another round before the current one fully ends. | The game becomes the story. You remember specific moments weeks later. |
| 4 | Regularly produces memorable highlights with occasional flatter sessions. The moments are there but not every match delivers one. The baseline between highlights is still fun without being particularly memorable. | Fun is consistent. The highlights happen often enough to keep the group coming back. |
| 3 | Produces occasional memorable moments but the typical match doesn't generate the storytelling impulse. Players enjoy themselves in the moment without being drawn to retell it. Another round gets suggested because the game is fun, not because something specific just happened that needs to be replayed. | Enjoyable without being memorable. Good enough to keep playing; not quite enough to become the group's go-to. |
| 2 | Fun in the moment but memorable moments are rare enough that sessions don't generate stories. The same outcomes and situations repeat without surprising anyone after the first few sessions. | The entertainment is consistent but thin. Nothing happens that makes you want to tell someone about it. |
| 1 | Sessions leave no impression. The game produces no memorable moments — wins and losses are interchangeable, outcomes are forgettable, and no situations arise that produce surprise or laughter specific to what just happened. | The game is playable but not entertaining in the way the genre promises. |

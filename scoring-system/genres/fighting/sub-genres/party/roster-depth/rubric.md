# Metric: Roster Depth
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Party Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = varied cast that creates different group dynamics and enough options that players develop preferences, 1 = so few choices that character select is meaningless

## Why This Matters

Roster depth in party fighters serves a different function than in competitive ones. Character attachment and meta viability are secondary concerns — what matters is whether the roster creates genuine variety in how sessions play out. Gang Beasts' roster of characters is small but visually distinct; Brawlhalla has 90+ Legends but in casual party contexts what matters is whether different characters play differently enough to generate different moments.

The floor in party fighters is low: players don't need to main a character for hundreds of hours. But they do develop preferences — "I always pick the big one", "I want the weird-looking one" — and enough distinct options to give every player at the table their own identity makes the game stickier. Visual distinctiveness matters more here than in competitive contexts: a character that looks funny or unusual creates group commentary before the match even starts.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Enough distinct characters that every player at a group session can find one they identify with visually or personality-wise. Different characters create at least somewhat different play patterns — size, weight, or movement create variety even if the controls are the same. Character designs are visually interesting enough to generate table commentary before the match starts. Options grow post-launch. | Everyone at the table has a preference and can state why. The character select screen generates conversation. |
| 4 | Good variety with a few characters that feel like reskins or slight variations. Most players find someone they gravitate toward. Visual designs are clear and distinctive even if a few are forgettable. | The roster works. Players develop preferences and stick to them. |
| 3 | Functional roster with limited variety. Most characters look or play similarly enough that choice is primarily aesthetic. Players pick the same character most sessions because the differences don't feel meaningful enough to switch. | The roster doesn't add much. Character select is a formality rather than a moment. |
| 2 | Very limited roster where choices feel constrained from the start. Multiple characters feel interchangeable. Groups run out of interesting character options quickly. | There's barely a choice to make. The roster is a weakness rather than a feature. |
| 1 | Minimal cast that fails to serve even the party context. So few options that player identity can't form around a pick, and visual distinctiveness isn't strong enough to generate preference. | The roster is an afterthought. Character select adds nothing to the experience. |

# Metric: Presentation & Artstyle
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Party Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (observable signals)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = visual identity communicates the game's premise at a glance and characters remain trackable during four-player chaos, 1 = premise is unclear from appearance alone or characters are indistinguishable during chaotic play

## Why This Matters

Presentation in party fighters carries a burden that no other fighting sub-genre faces: the visual identity must sell the game to an entire group simultaneously, including members who do not play video games. Traditional and competitive fighters are chosen by individual players making an informed purchase decision. Party fighters are chosen in a group context, often at a gathering, where someone sees the game on a screen and the group either collectively decides to try it or moves on. "Looks fun" is the selection criterion and it must land in seconds. Visual theme "communicates genre and mood at a glance" (digitaledge.org) — in the party fighter context, this means communicating that the game is physics-based, accessible, and immediately silly without requiring text or explanation.

The second party-fighter-specific dimension is the spectator experience. In competitive or traditional fighters, everyone present is typically playing. In a party fighter, not everyone holds a controller at all times — the visual presentation must entertain people who are not actively playing. The visual presentation must entertain people who are not holding a controller. Gang Beasts succeeds at both: "the bright and bold visuals make it easy for players to distinguish between characters and identify hazards within the arenas" while the gelatinous characters with "exaggerated movements and expressions are designed to evoke laughter" from players and observers alike (punogames.com). The visual design works at the controller and from the couch simultaneously.

The third dimension is functional: with four players fighting in close quarters under physics-driven chaos, characters must remain visually trackable. Research on multiplayer game character design confirms that "distinct silhouette design accelerates target identification in dynamic environments" and that games with silhouette-unique characters achieve meaningful improvements in player tracking under visual complexity (medium.com, character readability in TF2 and Overwatch). In a party fighter, losing track of your character in a four-player pile is not just a frustration — it removes the player's sense of authorship over what's happening, which is the genre's core promise.

## Observable Signals

| Signal | What to check |
|---|---|
| Premise communication | The visual style communicates "physics-based party brawler" from a screenshot or brief look without text or explanation; a non-player seeing it for the first time can infer the tone |
| Character visual distinctiveness | Each playable character has a distinct silhouette and colour palette that remains distinguishable during four-player chaos |
| Animation legibility during chaos | Key actions — hits, grabs, throws, falls — are readable from animation alone in a four-player match; physics interactions are visually clear rather than visually noisy |
| Environmental readability | Stage hazards, kill zones, and interactive elements are visually distinct from background; a player can identify what will hurt them without prior knowledge of the stage |
| Spectator visual engagement | The game is visually entertaining to observe without playing; animation style and character design produce a watchable experience for people waiting for their turn |

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | All five signals present. The visual style communicates the game's premise immediately — a non-player can infer it is silly, physical, and accessible from a glance. All playable characters are silhouette and colour distinct enough to track individually in four-player chaos. Physics interactions and key actions read clearly from animation. Stage hazards and kill zones are legible without prior knowledge. The animation and character design are visually entertaining to observe from outside. | The game sells itself from across the room. People watching want to play. Players never lose themselves in the chaos. |
| 4 | Four of five signals present. Most commonly: environmental readability or spectator engagement is underdeveloped while premise communication, character distinctiveness, and animation legibility are strong. A few stage elements require a moment to parse, or the spectator experience is passive rather than actively entertaining. | The presentation delivers on its core functional requirements. One weak signal is noticeable but does not undermine the experience. |
| 3 | Three signals present. Characters are visually distinct in isolation but harder to track during dense four-player moments. Premise communication works but requires more than a glance. Or animation legibility during chaos is inconsistent — clear in one-on-one exchanges but visually noisy in full four-player fights. | The presentation does its job at lower player counts but starts working against the game in full chaos. |
| 2 | Two or fewer signals present. Character visual differentiation is weak enough that players regularly lose track of themselves during four-player fights. The visual style does not clearly communicate the game's premise. Stage elements blend with background or are not legible without prior knowledge. | The presentation works against the genre. Tracking the action takes concentration that should be going toward enjoying it. |
| 1 | Multiple signals absent or broken. Characters are not visually distinguishable during chaos. The visual style communicates nothing about the game's premise or tone. Stage hazards and interactive elements are not readable. | The presentation undermines the experience before the physics even get started. |

# Metric: Gameplay Depth
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Party Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = rich emergent variety that creates different situations every session, 1 = every match plays out the same

## Why This Matters

Depth in party fighters is not frame data, neutral game, or tier lists. It's emergence — the degree to which the interaction between players, physics, and environments generates situations that couldn't have been scripted. Gang Beasts earns its reputation not through competitive balance or combo systems but through the absurdity of two players hanging off a moving truck, or a player trying to throw someone off a building and accidentally taking themselves with them. The depth question in party fighters is: does this game reliably generate situations that surprise everyone in the room?

This is distinct from randomness. Pure randomness (events happen regardless of player input) isn't depth — it's noise. Emergence happens when the systems respond to player decisions in unexpected but physically logical ways. The best party fighter moments feel earned even when they're absurd: the physics made sense, the players made choices, and the outcome was still a surprise.

Variety of situations depends on maps, environmental objects, physics interactions, and the range of things that can cause player eliminations. A party fighter with three maps all set on a flat platform has lower ceiling than one with moving hazards, interactive objects, and varied terrain — even if neither requires competitive skill to enjoy.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The game consistently generates unexpected situations that arise from the interaction between players, physics, and environments — not from random events happening to passive players. Different maps and environmental elements create genuinely distinct sessions. Players influence outcomes through decisions even when those outcomes are surprising. After 100 matches you still encounter situations you haven't seen before. The storytelling impulse fires multiple times per session: "Did you see what just happened?" | No two sessions feel the same. The game generates its own best moments. |
| 4 | Regularly produces varied and surprising situations with occasional sessions that feel flatter. The highlight moments are there but the baseline between them is more predictable. Different maps create meaningfully different dynamics. | Fun is consistent. The memorable moments happen often enough to keep bringing people back. |
| 3 | Produces occasional surprising moments but the typical match doesn't generate the storytelling impulse. The same physics interactions and environmental elements create the same situations after enough play time. Fun is reliable without being particularly memorable. | Enjoyable in the moment. You won't retell what happened. |
| 2 | Situations repeat quickly. The physics interactions are limited enough that you've seen every type of outcome within a few sessions. Maps don't create meaningfully different dynamics. The game is fun but not surprising after early sessions. | The initial chaos wears off. Repeated sessions feel increasingly familiar. |
| 1 | Every match plays out essentially the same. No emergence, no memorable moments. The outcome varies but the situations don't. Players are entertained by winning or losing, not by what happened during play. | The game produces no stories. Sessions are interchangeable. |

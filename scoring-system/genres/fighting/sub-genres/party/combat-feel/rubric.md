# Metric: Combat Feel
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Party Fighter
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = hits feel physical and satisfying within the chaos, controls respond clearly enough to feel intentional, 1 = the physics feel completely arbitrary and disconnected from inputs

## Why This Matters

Combat feel in party fighters doesn't require the frame-perfect hit stop and precision feedback of competitive fighting games. It requires something different: the physical interactions need to feel *legible*. When you grab someone and throw them, it should feel like you grabbed and threw them — the audio, animation, and physics response should confirm the action clearly enough that the moment reads as intentional even when the outcome is chaotic. Gang Beasts is specifically noted in the research for how frequently players search for "how to drop kick, punch, throw" — the physics interactions are learnable and satisfying specifically because they communicate clearly when they work.

The balance here is specific to the sub-genre: party fighters deliberately use exaggerated, physics-heavy animation that isn't tuned for competitive precision. A character flailing while trying to regain balance is a feature, not a bug — it creates the comedic moments the genre runs on. But the core inputs (attack, grab, throw) need to confirm clearly enough that players feel authorship over what happens, even when the physics take over from there.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Core interactions — punching, grabbing, throwing — produce clear audio-visual confirmation that makes each action feel intentional. The physics take over from there in satisfying, readable ways. Exaggerated animation communicates what's happening even when it's chaotic. Hits have enough impact feedback that landing one feels different from whiffing. The feel of the game reinforces the comedy: the physics are exaggerated but legible. | You feel like you did that. The chaos feels authored even when the outcome surprises you. |
| 4 | Core interactions feel solid with occasional moments where the audio-visual feedback doesn't quite match what happened. The physics produce readable outcomes most of the time. A few interactions feel more random than physical. | The controls feel intentional most of the time. The exceptions are noticeable but rare. |
| 3 | Core interactions work without producing strong satisfaction. Hits confirm but don't pop. Grab and throw mechanics work without generating the physical comedy the genre runs on. The physics produce outcomes but they read as random rather than physically caused. | The game is playable. The combat does its job without creating the satisfying moments that make party fighters memorable. |
| 2 | Core interactions don't confirm clearly enough to feel intentional. It's genuinely unclear whether a hit connected or missed. The physics produce outcomes that feel arbitrary — unconnected from what you were trying to do. The chaos isn't funny, it's confusing. | You can't tell if you're doing things or things are happening to you. |
| 1 | The physics feel completely disconnected from inputs. Actions don't produce readable responses. No moment of combat — hit, grab, or throw — produces a satisfying confirmation. The game feels broken rather than chaotic. | The controls work against the genre's central promise. Nothing feels physical or authored. |

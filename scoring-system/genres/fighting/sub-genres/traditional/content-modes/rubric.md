# Metric: Content & Modes
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Traditional
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = rich modes, training tools, and ongoing post-launch content that give players reasons to stay, 1 = bare-bones with nothing beyond the base versus mode

## Why This Matters

Content depth is the mechanism that separates fighting games that retain players from ones that spike and drop. The research makes the structural cause explicit: "Players are more likely to stick with a game that has satisfying single-player content." Dragon Ball FighterZ — a game with strong IP and good fundamentals — still saw "concurrent players on Steam fall off rapidly after release," and the analysis traces this directly to a lack of content that non-competitive players could engage with long-term.

Reddit discussions named specific content types as essential: "Many qol options (replays, tutorials, pinned combos, combo trials, if possible character tutorials), good netcode, rich content both singleplayer and multiplayer. Multiple modes, deep customization." Training tools matter specifically because "transparent frame data, hitbox visualization, and performance statistics help players understand why they lost, reducing frustration" — they're a retention tool, not just a practice feature.

Brawlhalla's cadence demonstrates what post-launch support looks like at the high end: "constant updates", "the grind is rewarding", "It has a HUGE esports scene that the devs lean into." Google Trends confirms players actively track this: brawlhalla schedule (+150%), brawlhalla patch (+50%), sparking zero new dlc (+800%), dragon ball fighterz new dlc (+450%). Players monitor what's coming next — the promise of future content is itself a retention driver.

Soul Calibur 2 and Smash Bros Melee are cited as historical benchmarks for single-player content depth done correctly — games that gave non-competitive players a complete experience. Street Fighter 6's World Tour mode and Battle Hub represent the modern equivalent of that commitment.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Rich content that serves multiple player types. Single-player or story mode offers a complete experience for players who don't engage with competitive modes. Training tools go beyond a basic practice mode — combo trials, frame data display, hitbox visualization, and character-specific tutorials actively help players improve. Multiple modes serve different play contexts (casual, ranked, local, arcade). Post-launch support is active and meaningful: new characters, modes, and balance patches arrive on a regular cadence. | Weeks pass before you feel like you've seen everything. Returning after time away means there's something new waiting. |
| 4 | Strong content with one or two notable gaps. Single-player content exists and offers real play time. Training tools are solid — frame data is accessible and combo practice works. Post-launch support has been meaningful, though not every content area hits the same standard. | The game gives you enough to stay engaged for months. You'll notice what's missing once you've exhausted what's there. |
| 3 | Adequate content at launch that doesn't go deep in any area. Single-player content is present but short or thin — an arcade mode with no story weight. Training tools cover the basics without surfacing the systems that matter for improvement (no frame data, no hitbox display). Post-launch updates have been infrequent or primarily cosmetic. | The content holds you for the first weeks. Eventually you run out of reasons the game itself gives you to stay. |
| 2 | Thin at launch with little to do outside of versus matches. Single-player content is token or absent. Training tools are minimal — a practice dummy with no data. Post-launch support has not added meaningful new modes or tools. | The game shows its edges fast. It works as a versus mode and not much more. |
| 1 | Bare-bones at launch with no meaningful post-launch support. The versus mode is the entire game. No single-player content, no useful training tools, nothing added after release. | You've seen everything the game has to offer within the first few sessions. |

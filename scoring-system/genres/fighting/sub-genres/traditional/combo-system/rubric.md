# Metric: Combo System
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Traditional
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = a way in from day one and a ceiling worth chasing for years, 1 = execution blocks play before it begins or the system is too shallow to engage with

## Why This Matters

Combos are the payoff when you land a hit. In a traditional fighter, getting a hit in without a combo system behind it is like scoring a goal that doesn't count — the reward is missing. How well the system is designed determines whether new players can deal real damage, whether experienced players have something worth mastering, and whether there's room between those two ends to develop a style that's genuinely yours.

The research captures both failure modes. On entry: "new players struggle to 'shoot their gun' at all and don't stand a chance against someone who can" — when the execution barrier is too high, new players never experience the game at all. On depth: "landing airtight combos is fun" and "freedom of expression and decision-making — having various options for mechanics like movement and combos is super important" — without a real ceiling, the game has nothing to offer once you've learned the basics.

Street Fighter 6 is the current benchmark: multiple sequences per character, simpler options that deal slightly reduced damage, harder options that reward the investment without requiring impossible precision in a real match. Dragon Ball FighterZ took the other route — auto-combos that let new players convert immediately, at the cost of a lower damage ceiling.

A well-designed combo system gives players a reason to open training mode, a way to express individual style even against someone at the same skill level, and enough depth that there's still something to find years in.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | New players can deal real damage from the first few hours without needing to master the game first — simple sequences are competitive, not just survival tools. Harder sequences reward the investment without requiring impossible precision under match pressure. The gap between simple and advanced is wide enough that two players at the same skill level can express completely different styles through how they combo, and there's still something new to find years later. | You're dealing real damage from the start. A year later you're still finding new ways to play. |
| 4 | Clear ways in for new players with real improvement paths. Some characters have deeper systems than others. The difference between simple and advanced is meaningful without being discouraging — most players can land their preferred sequence in a real match with practice. | The system rewards the time you put in. There's a real execution layer worth developing. |
| 3 | Most characters have one main sequence with small variations, not much creative space between beginner and experienced play. The system works without giving you much reason to go deeper than the basics. | Combos work. They don't pull you any deeper. |
| 2 | Either landing a combo in a real match is so demanding that most players can't convert reliably, or the system is so simple that there's one sequence per character and nothing to explore. Simple options deal noticeably less damage with no clear path forward. | The execution barrier blocks play, or the system doesn't reward learning. |
| 1 | The combo system doesn't reward learning. Either too hard to land anything in a real match, or so simple that nothing is left to develop. The execution layer adds frustration or boredom rather than depth. | The combo system works against competitive depth rather than creating it. |

# Metric: Gameplay Depth
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Traditional
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = rich tactical system that rewards reads, spacing, and long-term mastery, 1 = shallow with no meaningful skill layer

## Why This Matters

Gameplay depth is the dimension that separates fighting games from button-mashing brawlers — and players are precise about what it actually means. From Reddit's "what makes a good fighting game" discussions: "Neutral must feel interactive, and like I can actually maneuver around people just flinging themselves at me" and "diverse Defense options." The recurring frustration is games that let one aggressive playstyle dominate without meaningful answer. The positive formulation is equally clear: "Characters over systems, neutral over constant aggression, kits feeling like the entire thing can be used rather than just a few moves being best."

Game design analysis sharpens this further. Depth in fighting games "comes from meaningful interaction, not mechanical overload." The great ones are built on "reading, anticipating, and countering your opponent; understanding exactly where and when to use just the right move; and spacing and timing it all just right — all systems and moves exist to further the mind games you can play with your opponent." The keyword is mind games, not muscle memory. Mechanical execution is the floor; reads and spacing are the ceiling.

The failure mode is specific: a game where "high-effort play doesn't produce proportionally better results than low-effort play." When the neutral game lacks meaningful defensive options and aggressive play is simply correct, the skill layer collapses to whoever presses the right button more often. Brawlhalla earned specific praise for avoiding this: "It's extremely balanced at high ranks, which isn't really the case for a lot of other platform fighters" — in part because the neutral game rewards positioning and edge-guarding over raw button pressure.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The neutral game is genuinely interactive — defensive tools, movement options, and spacing work as real answers to aggressive approaches, not just delay tactics. Frame advantage creates a meaningful turn structure: knowing you're plus or minus shapes your decisions. Combo expression allows creative routing without limiting players to one optimal path. Reads and anticipation — not just speed — are the primary deciding factors at high level. Mastery is a long-term, genuinely rewarding pursuit. | You'll still be learning this game a year in. Playing against a new opponent reveals something new about the system every time. |
| 4 | Clear tactical depth with multiple viable approaches. The neutral is interactive and defensive tools function. Frame advantage informs decisions even if it isn't always surfaced explicitly. A few situations or playstyle gaps exist but don't define the experience. The skill ceiling is real and worth pursuing. | High-level play is genuinely engaging. A few rough edges don't undermine the core experience of reads and execution mattering. |
| 3 | Mechanics are present but the system doesn't consistently reward reads over aggression. Defensive options exist but feel undertuned — they slow down pressure rather than fully answering it. Frame advantage exists but its tactical implications are blurry in play. Depth is accessible to players who actively pursue it but doesn't surface naturally through normal matches. | You can play on feel and get results. Going deeper is available but the game doesn't push you toward it. |
| 2 | One dominant playstyle or approach solves most situations reliably. Defensive tools are inadequate enough that aggression is simply correct most of the time. Combo expression is narrow — one route handles everything. Knowing the game deeply doesn't reliably outperform knowing it shallowly. | High-effort play doesn't produce proportionally better results than low-effort play. The skill ceiling arrives fast. |
| 1 | No meaningful tactical depth. One approach works, defensive options don't change outcomes, and the gap between a new player and an expert is primarily reaction time rather than game knowledge. Reads, spacing, and positioning produce no real advantage. | The system doesn't reward investment. You plateau almost immediately after learning the basics. |

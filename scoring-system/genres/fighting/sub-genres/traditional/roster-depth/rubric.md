# Metric: Roster Depth
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Traditional
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = vast, distinctive roster where player identity forms around individual picks, 1 = minimal roster where character choices feel negligible

## Why This Matters

Roster depth is the most consistently searched dimension of fighting games across the research, and game design analysis confirms why: "The roster is the primary thing that draws players into a game, people need to be fans of the characters." Players form "long-term emotional attachments to specific fighters" — the character you main isn't just a gameplay preference, it's an identity within the community. This is confirmed by Google Trends: "brawlhalla legends" registers 88 search interest with a 90% trend increase — higher than searches for the game's own name, which suggests that for many players the character comes first and the game follows. "brawlhalla characters" follows at 78 search interest. Across Reddit discussions of what makes a great fighting game, roster came up in virtually every response: "big roster", "Characters over systems", "Every character is unique because of the sigs and stats."

Roster depth also has a quality dimension, not just a quantity one. Community discussions in 2025 surfaced a recurring tension: "the more must-haves demanded in every sequel's roster, the less newcomers and uncommon characters players get to see." A large roster of indistinguishable characters is worse than a smaller one with strong archetypes. The question a rubric should answer is whether the cast gives players enough distinct identities to form real attachments — not just whether the number is high.

The floor matters too. A game with 100 characters where 20 are competitively viable is functionally a game with 20 characters for the players who care about more than aesthetics.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Extensive roster with characters that each feel meaningfully distinct in playstyle, movement physics, and visual identity. The cast spans enough archetypes — rushdown, zoner, grappler, charge-based, motion-based — that player identity forms naturally around a main. Viability is spread broadly enough that off-meta picks are real choices, not sacrifices. The roster grows post-launch with additions that bring something genuinely new. | You'll spend real time deciding who to main. New character reveals are events you care about. |
| 4 | Strong roster with clear variety across archetypes and visual identities. A few characters feel redundant or underdeveloped, but the overall cast gives most players someone to genuinely invest in. Post-launch content has added to the roster. | You'll find your main without much friction. The cast has personality even if a few slots are filler. |
| 3 | Functional roster that covers the expected archetypes without surprise. Characters fulfil roles without distinction — you can tell the grappler from the zoner, but few feel memorable enough to generate attachment. Variety exists on paper but sameness creeps in across the cast. | The roster gets the job done. Picking a character is a practical decision more than an expressive one. |
| 2 | Shallow roster with limited variety. Multiple characters share kits or visual design closely enough to feel like reskins. Choosing a main feels constrained by how few distinct options exist. Viable picks cluster even further, shrinking real choice. | You'll run out of interesting picks quickly. Playing as different characters doesn't change much. |
| 1 | Minimal roster that fails to serve the genre. Characters are barely distinguishable or so few in number that player identity can't form around them. The cast is the game's most obvious weakness from the moment you open character select. | There's no one to get attached to. The roster reads as an afterthought. |

# Metric: Presentation & Artstyle
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Traditional
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = strong, consistent visual identity with readable screen and impactful audio-visual design, 1 = presentation actively hurts the experience

## Why This Matters

Presentation is named as a baseline requirement in fighting game discussions — not a bonus layer but an entry filter: "good character designs and graphics most people won't even try the game." This is before a player has thrown a single punch. The visual and audio identity of the game is the first promise it makes, and players have learned to read whether it will be kept.

More specifically, players call out visual clarity as a direct gameplay concern, not just an aesthetic one: "A readable screen that's not so packed with details and effects." In fighting games, visual clutter isn't just unpleasant — it's disorienting during the moments that require the sharpest reads. If special effects obscure the hitbox area of an attack, or stage lighting makes characters hard to silhouette, the presentation is actively degrading the gameplay layer. This is a different failure mode from simply looking bad.

Audio design is called out independently as a structural element of how fights feel: "satisfying gameplay (hard to describe. good animation and audio cues help)" — and from the combat feel research, this extends further. The gamedeveloper.com combat impact analysis confirms that sound "transforms" how hits feel: "Turn the volume up and the animation may suddenly seem more painful." But in the presentation rubric, the concern is about consistent identity and scale — does the overall audio design communicate the tone and weight of the game, not just whether individual hits confirm?

Games like Dragon Ball FighterZ (Arc System Works' cel-shaded animation), Granblue Fantasy Versus: Rising (faithfully adapted from the Granblue art direction), and Street Fighter 6 (bold, high-contrast character art) are frequently cited in community discussions as examples of strong identity. Brawlhalla's skins register 43 search interest — players actively search for cosmetics, which only happens when the visual identity is strong enough to be worth customising.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Strong, consistent visual identity that extends across every character, stage, and menu. Character designs are immediately readable and memorable — silhouettes are distinct enough to identify from a thumbnail. Special effects enhance rather than obscure: you can always read where the hitbox area of a move falls even during the most intense exchanges. Sound design amplifies every hit with appropriate weight; audio alone communicates what landed and how hard. The game has an aesthetic personality players want to customise and engage with. | The game looks as good as it plays. You could screenshot any moment and it would look intentional. |
| 4 | Clear visual identity with strong highlights. A few characters or stages don't match the overall standard. Effects are generally readable but one or two moves or stages push the visual density high enough to cause occasional confusion. Sound design is solid throughout with some standout moments. | The presentation holds up. You'll notice the exceptions but they don't undermine the overall impression. |
| 3 | Functional presentation. Characters are identifiable and stages are distinct enough to feel different. Sound design does its job without standing out — hits confirm without producing a strong response. No strong visual identity emerges, but nothing actively distracts. | The game looks fine. You won't think about how it looks — which is both the strength and the ceiling of the presentation. |
| 2 | Visual clutter or effect density makes the screen hard to parse at the moments that matter most. Character designs lack distinctiveness enough that you occasionally need a moment to identify an opponent's move. Sound design is weak or generic — hits don't confirm with confidence. | Chaos becomes confusion rather than excitement. The presentation works against the gameplay layer at key moments. |
| 1 | Presentation actively hurts the experience. Screen is regularly unreadable during exchanges. Character designs are undifferentiated or off-putting enough to create detachment from the roster. Audio feedback is absent or misleading. The game looks or sounds like a prototype rather than a finished product. | The presentation doesn't just fail to help — it gets in the way of playing the game. |

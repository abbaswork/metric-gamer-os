# Metric: Accessibility
**Genre:** Fighting
**Sub-genre:** Traditional
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = welcoming entry without sacrificing the skill ceiling, 1 = alienating new players and hollow for veterans

## Why This Matters

Accessibility is the most structurally difficult problem in fighting game design, and it's the genre's biggest driver of player loss. Research from critpoints.net is direct: fighting games are "intrinsically hard to understand" due to unintuitive control schemes and invisible properties like frame advantage that govern turn order — properties that players don't know exist, let alone can act on. The consequence is described precisely: "makes players feel that their decisions have little impact on the outcome of a match." New players "struggle to shoot their gun at all and don't stand a chance against someone who can."

The motion input problem is specific: quarter-circle inputs are "completely unique to the genre of traditional fighting games" with no transferable skills from other games, and "can take hours to weeks of practice" before reliable execution. When the controls themselves take that long to learn, players quit before reaching the game underneath them. Dragon Ball FighterZ attracted massive audiences through its IP but "concurrent players on Steam fall off rapidly after release" — suggesting the controls and learning structure failed to retain them past the initial hook.

The modern design response — Street Fighter 6's Modern controls — shows what solving this looks like: "allows players to complete more complicated special moves by simply pressing a designated special move button and a direction." Crucially, balance is maintained: "all of your attacks deal less damage when using Modern," ensuring accessibility doesn't remove competitive depth or make the simplified path strictly superior. SF6 still "pulls an average of almost 14,000 players at any given time on Steam, which is rarely seen in the fighting game space over a year after launch" — a direct result of onboarding new players successfully.

Reddit expressed the ideal from the player side: "Allowing newbs to play without making it feel like a punishment to vets while being deep enough to enjoy for years to come." Brawlhalla achieved this specifically: "easy to pick up, harder to master", "simple controls and no huge information wall", "skill ceiling is high but not unobtainable."

There are two distinct failure modes: too impenetrable (players drop the game before finding the depth) and too shallow (mastery arrives too quickly). A score of 5 means the game solves both.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | New players can get their first win within the first session and understand why they lost in the second. Tutorial and training tools teach mechanics in context — not as a front-loaded manual, but as "on demand, just in time" explanations that appear when they're relevant. Control options lower the execution barrier without removing strategic depth (damage scaling, simplified specials with trade-offs). At high level, the ceiling is genuinely high — mastery takes hundreds of hours and continues to reward. | You're not lost at the start, and you're not bored at the top. The game grows with you at every stage. |
| 4 | Good onboarding with a few gaps. New players can get started and find early wins without hitting an invisible wall. Control options exist but don't fully close the gap. Training tools are present but don't actively guide improvement — players have to know what to practice. The skill ceiling is real but not fully surfaced through in-game tools alone. | Getting started takes some friction but the game cooperates once you're moving. Veterans have meaningful room to grow. |
| 3 | New player experience is rough but survivable. The game doesn't actively teach the mechanics that matter — frame data, hit advantage, block strings — and players figure these out through extended friction rather than guided discovery. The ceiling is there but reaching it is a self-directed project. | Expect a hard first few hours with no map. The depth is real once you find it, but you're finding it alone. |
| 2 | High barrier to entry with no meaningful in-game path through it. New players face mechanics — invisible turn structures, complex input sequences, opaque system interactions — with no explanation or scaffolding. Meanwhile, experienced players find the ceiling arrives earlier than expected. One of the two failure modes dominates. | The first experience punishes in ways that have nothing to do with skill. It puts people off before they can reach what the game is actually about. |
| 1 | Alienating to new players with little reward for veterans. Controls are complex for complexity's sake, in-game guidance is absent, and the depth doesn't compensate for the cost of learning it. Both failure modes apply simultaneously. | The difficulty works against the game. Players leave before they play it. |

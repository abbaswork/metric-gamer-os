# Metric: Monetisation Model
**Genre:** Fighting — Free Niche
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = monetisation is purely cosmetic and unintrusive, 1 = monetisation is predatory or pay-to-win

## Why This Matters

Free-to-play fighting games face an inherent tension between sustainable revenue and fair competition. Brawlhalla ("tons of battlepasses and cosmetics and it's free") is cited as a model that resolved this well — the cosmetics are monetised but the gameplay isn't gated. The free-to-play fighting game space is crowded with games that get this wrong.

The line between fair and predatory is well-documented: "If not balanced carefully, this can lead to a pay-to-win dynamic, where players who spend more have an unfair advantage" (ixiegaming.com). Battle passes compound the concern: "Players who don't purchase the pass might feel left out or disadvantaged." Loot boxes generate the sharpest backlash because random chance combined with spending creates the sense of manipulation that drives the most negative community sentiment. The distinction players draw is clear: cosmetic purchases let players express identity without competitive consequence; anything that affects match outcomes breaks the implicit contract of a fair free-to-play game.

This rubric covers the monetisation model as a system independent of the roster (scored separately in Roster Monetisation). It measures whether the spending layer is cosmetic-only, whether free players access the same competitive tools as paying ones, and whether the model creates a measurable two-tier experience.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Monetisation is entirely cosmetic. No gameplay advantage is purchasable. Battle pass content is cosmetic-only. Free players have access to everything that affects match outcomes. | You'll never feel like your wallet is affecting your matches. |
| 4 | Cosmetic-first monetisation with minor quality-of-life advantages available for purchase (XP boosts, convenience features). No purchase affects competitive outcomes. | The game nudges you toward spending without ever feeling like it punishes you for not. |
| 3 | Mostly cosmetic monetisation with a few grey-area purchases that offer marginal advantages or accelerated progression. Spending money isn't required but it's noticeably faster. | Free play works. Paying speeds things up without changing the game. |
| 2 | Meaningful gameplay progression is accelerated significantly by spending. Time-gated content, energy systems, or consumable advantages create a two-tier experience. Non-paying players feel the friction. | Free play is a slower, more limited version of the full game. The game treats spending as the expected path. |
| 1 | Pay-to-win or pay-to-compete elements that directly affect match outcomes. Purchasing players have a measurable in-match advantage over free players. | The monetisation is a gameplay disadvantage for non-paying players. Competing without spending is a handicap. |

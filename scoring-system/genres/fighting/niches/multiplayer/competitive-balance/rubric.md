# Metric: Competitive Balance (Multiplayer)
**Genre:** Fighting — Multiplayer Niche
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = live meta is healthy and character choice is meaningful, 1 = live meta is broken and character choice is predetermined

## Why This Matters

This rubric replaces the genre-level Competitive Balance rubric for campaigns focused specifically on online ranked play. The distinction: competitive balance at genre level measures the quality of the meta design; this niche rubric measures the live state of the meta as it actually exists in ranked play right now. A game with a well-designed balance system can still have a broken live meta due to missing patches, specific character exploits discovered post-launch, or ranked mode incentives that reward degenerate strategies.

Tier lists reflect "the current state of the characters in an ever-evolving setting" — and in ranked play, that live state directly shapes who you face and what strategies you encounter at every level. When a patch lands and shifts character viability, it "could reset or start a completely new meta" (betting.us). This rubric scores the current live state, not the patch notes intent. A game being patched responsively after meta problems emerge scores higher than one with the same theoretical balance but months of acknowledged dominance left unaddressed.

The concern that pushes players toward tier lists is specific: "Tier lists can often be seen as controversial as it could make new players think they need to play certain characters to succeed" (betting.us). In practice, at the extremes of ranked play this stops being a misconception and becomes an accurate read. Reddit confirmed the standard: "It's extremely balanced at high ranks, which isn't really the case for a lot of other platform fighters" — the comparison is explicitly about what the ranked ladder actually looks like, not what the design intends.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The ranked ladder reflects genuine skill and match-up knowledge. No strategy or character cluster dominates win rates enough to make off-meta play feel punished. Patches respond to identified imbalances without creating new ones. The meta evolves through player discovery rather than exploit-finding. | Who you play matters; how you play matters more. The ladder feels like a fair test of skill. |
| 4 | Mostly healthy live meta with known outliers that skilled players can manage. Off-meta picks appear in top 100 results. Recent patches have addressed the most cited issues even if residual gaps remain. | Playing the game you want to play is viable. The meta shapes decisions without dictating them. |
| 3 | Visible tier clustering in ranked results. A handful of characters appear significantly more than their roster share would suggest. Off-meta play is possible but carries a measurable disadvantage at higher ranks. | The meta is a real factor in climb strategy. You'll likely notice what everyone else is running before long. |
| 2 | The ranked ladder has a clear dominant tier that experienced players converge on. Match variety collapses at higher ranks. Community sentiment reflects frustration with the current state and patches are slow or ineffective. | Climbing ranked means learning the tier list before learning the game. |
| 1 | The live meta is broken. One character or strategy wins at a rate that removes meaningful competition. The ranked mode actively discourages character diversity. Community has publicly called the meta out and no fix is in sight. | Ranked play is a formality. The outcome is largely determined by character select. |

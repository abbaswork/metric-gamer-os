# Metric: Car Customization
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Arcade Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = deep visual and performance customization expressive enough to make every build feel personal, 1 = no meaningful customization beyond a default skin

## Why This Matters

research.md's Reddit list names "customization" as a standalone factor players use to judge arcade racing games — distinct from "Cool cars" and "Variety of vehicles," which belong to the roster question. The distinction matters: a game can have hundreds of cars and still fail at customization if the options are cosmetic-only, and a game with a modest roster can excel here if the depth per car is extraordinary.

Need for Speed: Underground is the genre's reference standard. The game let players change performance parts (engine, NOS, drivetrain, suspension, tires), body kits, vinyls, custom bumpers, spoilers, wheels, window tints, and underglow — and the customization wasn't just popular, it reportedly turned players into real-life car modifiers and helped the game sell over 15 million copies. That's the bar: customization deep enough that it becomes a reason to own the game independently of the racing itself. The opposite failure mode is a game that surfaces a "customize" screen but limits it to palette swaps and a single spoiler option with no performance relevance — technically present, functionally meaningless.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Covers both performance (engine, handling, drivetrain, suspension, tires, NOS) and deep visual options (body kits, liveries/vinyls, underglow, custom bumpers, wheels, window tints). Tools are expressive enough that no two garages look alike, and the options are attainable through normal play rather than locked behind real-money purchases. | You can spend as much time in the garage as on the track — and the build you make genuinely feels like yours. |
| 4 | Strong customization on one axis and decent on the other — for example, a rich visual editor but shallow performance tuning, or full performance modification with limited cosmetic expression. Alternatively, deep on both axes but a meaningful share of options gated behind a paywall or steep grind. | The depth is there, but you'll notice an obvious ceiling on whichever axis is weaker, or hit a wall on the content you actually want. |
| 3 | Covers the basics on both axes — colour selection, a handful of part swaps, basic performance upgrades — without going deep on either. Options feel like checklist items rather than creative tools. | You can make a car look and feel different, but you're not going to lose an evening in a vinyl editor. |
| 2 | Customization is mostly cosmetic palette-swaps with little to no performance impact, or options exist on paper but most are locked behind aggressive monetisation that most players won't clear through normal play. | The customize button is there but the content behind it is thin or inaccessible. |
| 1 | No meaningful customization beyond a fixed default skin, or the only options are choosing a colour from a fixed list with no body, performance, or visual tooling whatsoever. | What the car looks like when you unlock it is what it looks like forever. |

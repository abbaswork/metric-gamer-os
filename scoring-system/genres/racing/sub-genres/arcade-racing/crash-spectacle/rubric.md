# Metric: Crash Spectacle & Chaos
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Arcade Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = collisions and stunts are deliberately staged, physically satisfying spectacle that rewards reckless driving, 1 = wrecks are absent, visually unsatisfying, or physically broken

## Why This Matters

research.md's Reddit list names "get to do really goofy things" and "Awesome crashes" directly as factors that define a good arcade racing game — and unlike every other metric in this sub-genre, this one has no sim-racing equivalent at all (see [AI Opponent Quality](../../sim-racing/ai-opponents/rubric.md) and the rest of that sub-genre's set). Sim racing treats a crash as a failure state to avoid; arcade racing treats it as a feature to build toward, which makes this the one metric that captures something genuinely unique to the sub-genre's identity rather than a tuned-down version of a sim-racing concern.

Burnout 3: Takedown is the genre's reference implementation. Its Crash Mode is built entirely around causing the largest multi-vehicle pile-up possible for score, with damage tallied in dollar amounts, shattering windshields and flying debris rendered in detail, and an Aftertouch/Impact Time system that lets the player steer their own wreck in slow motion to chain into more destruction — reviewers describe the results as "immensely satisfying" specifically because the chaos is staged and controllable rather than random. The same design logic extends to the regular race mode: boost is earned through near-misses and driving into oncoming traffic, which means the game is constantly rewarding the player for flirting with the crash it's also built to make spectacular.

The failure mode is crashes that are chaotic without being satisfying — which usually means the underlying physics are broken rather than tuned. FlatOut 3: Chaos & Destruction is the cautionary case: reviewers found that full-speed collisions barely damaged a car while light taps could deal 40% damage, and cars would climb walls vertically on contact — bugs severe enough that critics called the physics "completely broken" and the game one of the worst of its year. Goofy, exaggerated chaos only works as a delight if the underlying rules are consistent enough for the player to feel like they caused it.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Collisions and stunts are a deliberate, rewarded part of the core loop, not an incidental failure state — damage is visually dramatic (deformation, debris, shattering) and tied to a scoring or progression system that rewards causing them well. A dedicated mode or mechanic lets players chain destruction (e.g., directing a wreck in slow motion into more cars). The chaos feels caused by the player's choices, not random. | Wrecking spectacularly is its own kind of winning, and it feels earned every time. |
| 4 | Crashes and stunts look and feel great and are clearly rewarded by the game's systems, but the dedicated spectacle mode or mechanic is thinner than the regular race's crash physics, or large pile-ups are rarer than smaller, still-satisfying collisions. | Crashing is consistently fun to watch and cause — there just isn't a whole mode built around chasing the biggest one. |
| 3 | Crashes look reasonable and register as real events, but they're mostly incidental — a penalty to avoid rather than something the game invites the player to pursue, with no dedicated scoring or mode built around causing them. | Wrecks happen and look fine, but the game isn't really asking you to make one happen. |
| 2 | Damage modelling is inconsistent or visually unconvincing — cars bounce off impacts without real weight, or destruction looks the same regardless of speed or angle — making crashes feel arbitrary rather than satisfying. | Crashes happen constantly, but they never feel like they meant anything. |
| 1 | Collision physics are broken in ways that undercut any sense of cause and effect — damage doesn't scale with impact speed, cars glitch through geometry, or wrecks are visually nonexistent. There's no spectacle to chase because the underlying rules don't hold up. | Crashing is just a bug you run into, not a moment you were going for. |

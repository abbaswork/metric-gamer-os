# Metric: Driving Feel & Mastery
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Arcade Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = controls are immediately legible and reward deep mastery, cars have distinct weight and sound character that matches their real-world type, and crashes are visually dramatic spectacle integrated into the reward loop; 1 = controls are broken, all cars feel identical regardless of class, sounds are absent or mismatched, and crashes are physically wrong or invisible

## Why This Matters

research.md's Reddit findings put "actile action and a sense of accomplishment from mastery," "take genuine effort to get good at," "Speed," "Good driving feel," "Realism," and "Awesome crashes" and "get to do really goofy things" at the center of how players judge a good arcade racing game — more than half of the eleven listed factors converge on what the car feels, sounds, and looks like to drive and crash. This metric covers all of them.

**Control feel and mastery** are the foundation. Ridge Racer built its identity on letting players drift through corners to preserve speed — a technique pioneered in video games specifically to capture Japanese mountain-road car culture — and later entries tied nitrous boost charging directly to drift quality, turning the cornering technique itself into the resource-management layer. Burnout 3: Takedown extended the same logic: boost is earned through near-misses and driving against oncoming traffic, rewarding the riskiest driving with the resource that makes the car faster. Crazy Taxi deliberately built a control scheme players could pick up immediately while reserving drift-chaining and combo stunts as the skill ceiling — easy to learn, difficult to master, by design.

**Weight feel and real-world character** are what separate a convincing arcade car from a floating cursor. "Realism" appears in research.md's Reddit list for arcade racing precisely because players aren't asking for sim-grade physics — they're asking for plausible physical character. A muscle car should feel heavier and more tail-happy than a compact sports car; an SUV should resist direction changes more than a low-slung coupe; a supercar should accelerate with a different quality of urgency than a street car. Sega Rally's surface-friction simulation (different grip on tarmac, gravel, and mud) is held up as a design milestone because it added physical credibility without sacrificing arcade accessibility — the car still felt fast and forgiving, but the road surface gave drifting a believable basis.

**Sound design** is the sensory confirmation that the physics are real. An engine note that doesn't change under load, tire screech that doesn't trigger at the limit, or collision sounds that don't scale with impact speed all signal to the player that the numbers are decorative rather than physical. Conversely, a supercar that sounds faster than a muscle car, corners that scream at the grip limit, and impacts that land with appropriate weight turn every race into an audio-physical event the player can interpret in real time.

**Crash spectacle** is the one dimension that has no sim-racing equivalent — sim racing treats a crash as a failure state; arcade racing treats it as a feature. Burnout 3: Takedown's Crash Mode is the reference implementation: damage tallied in dollars, shattering windshields and flying debris rendered in detail, and an Aftertouch/Impact Time system that lets the player steer their own wreck in slow motion to chain into more destruction. The same design logic carries into its regular race mode, where boost is earned by flirting with the crashes the game also makes spectacular. The failure mode is chaos without physical logic: FlatOut 3's reviewers found full-speed collisions barely damaged a car while light taps dealt 40% damage and cars climbed walls vertically on contact — bugs severe enough that critics called the physics "completely broken." Goofy, exaggerated crashes only read as delight when the underlying rules are consistent enough for the player to feel like they caused the carnage.

## Rubric

Scores are assessed across four axes that are averaged into a single band:
- **Control & Mastery** — responsiveness, drift/boost integration, skill ceiling
- **Weight & Physics** — distinct mass profiles per vehicle class, surface behaviour, braking and turn-in plausibility
- **Sound Design** — engine note, tire screech, impact audio; all scaling with race events and matching vehicle character
- **Crash Spectacle** — visual drama, physical consistency of damage, integration into reward loop

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | **Control & Mastery:** Core inputs are legible within the first lap; layered systems (drift-to-boost, near-miss bonuses, momentum management) give skilled players a visible ceiling to climb. **Weight & Physics:** Cars have distinct mass profiles — heavy vehicles resist direction changes and need longer braking zones, lighter cars turn sharply and lose grip earlier; surface type affects cornering grip in a perceptible way. **Sound Design:** Engine notes, tire screech, and impact sounds match each vehicle's character and scale with race events — a supercar sounds meaningfully faster than a muscle car, corners scream at the limit, collisions land with weight proportional to speed. **Crash Spectacle:** Crashes are a deliberate, rewarded part of the loop — damage is visually dramatic (deformation, debris, shattering glass), scales with impact speed and angle, and is tied to a scoring or progression system that rewards causing them well. | Every car feels like a different thing to drive, the race sounds and feels alive, and wrecking spectacularly is its own kind of winning. |
| 4 | Strong across most axes but one is noticeably weaker: a portion of the roster handles generically despite distinct weight classes, sound design is strong on engines but flat on impacts or vice versa, or crash physics look good without being integrated into a scoring or reward system. The best cars and the best moments are genuinely great but the experience isn't fully consistent. | The peak moments are excellent — there are just enough flat spots across the roster or the sound mix to remind you it's not quite complete. |
| 3 | Driving feels competent — the basics work, cars are distinguishable — but the mastery layer is thin (no drift-to-boost or near-miss system), weight differences between classes are noticeable but not expressive, sounds are functional rather than character-defining (tire screech triggers but doesn't vary by surface or car type), and crashes are incidental penalties rather than rewarded events. | It drives fine and sounds okay, but there's not much to chase once you've learned the basic line. |
| 2 | Handling is inconsistent or floaty — weight feel is absent or implausible (a heavy truck corners identically to a sports car, or braking distances don't scale with vehicle mass), sound design is generic or mismatched to events (engine note doesn't change under load, no screech at the grip limit), and crashes are visually unconvincing or physically inconsistent (damage doesn't scale with impact speed or angle). | You can finish races, but the car rarely feels like it's responding to what you're actually doing. |
| 1 | Controls are unresponsive or broken, all cars feel identical regardless of class or weight, sounds are absent or wrong (silent corners, identical engine notes for every vehicle), and crash physics don't correspond to real events — cars pass through geometry, take the same damage from a tap as a full-speed collision, or show no damage at all. | There's no driving here to get better at and no feedback telling you what's happening. |

# Metric: Driving Feel & Mastery
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Arcade Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = controls are immediately approachable but reward deep mastery of drifting, boosting, and speed management, with handling grounded enough to make the spectacle convincing, 1 = controls feel unresponsive or broken, with nothing resembling a skill ceiling to climb

## Why This Matters

research.md's Reddit findings put "actile action and a sense of accomplishment from mastery," "take genuine effort to get good at," "Speed," and "Good driving feel" at the center of how players judge a good arcade racing game — four of the eleven listed factors converge on the same idea: arcade racing's appeal lives in how the car feels to drive, not in how accurately it's modelled. Unlike sim racing's [Physics & Handling Realism](../../sim-racing/physics-handling/rubric.md), which scores against real-world vehicle dynamics, this metric scores against a different bar entirely: does driving the car make you want to get better at driving it.

The genre's foundational design language backs this up directly. Ridge Racer built its identity on letting players drift through corners to preserve speed — a technique pioneered in video games specifically to capture Japanese mountain-road car culture — and later entries tied nitrous boost charging directly to drift quality, turning the cornering technique itself into the resource-management layer. Burnout 3: Takedown generalized the same idea: boost is earned through drifting, near-misses, and driving against oncoming traffic, rewarding the riskiest driving with the resource that makes the car faster. Crazy Taxi's design went further on accessibility, deliberately building a control scheme players could pick up immediately while reserving drift-chaining and crazy-stunt combos as the skill ceiling — easy to learn, difficult to master, by design.

"Realism" appears in research.md's Reddit list for arcade racing too, which looks contradictory at first — but the community isn't asking for sim-grade physics. Sega Rally's surface-friction simulation (different grip on tarmac, gravel, and mud) is held up as a design milestone precisely because it added physical credibility without sacrificing arcade accessibility — the car still felt fast and forgiving, but the road surface gave drifting and cornering a believable basis. That's the standard this rubric scores against: enough physical plausibility that speed and drift feel earned, not a sim's worth of simulation. Big Rigs: Over the Road Racing is the cautionary case at the bottom of this scale — reviewers cited the absence of collision detection and "poor truck controls" as reasons it's remembered as one of the worst games ever made; there's no mastery curve to climb when the controls themselves don't work.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Controls are immediately legible — accelerate, brake, drift, and boost are understandable within the first lap — but layered systems (drift-to-boost charging, near-miss bonuses, momentum management) give skilled players a real, visible ceiling to climb toward. Speed feels dangerous and earned rather than just numerically high. A baseline of physical plausibility (weight shifts under braking, surface affects grip) makes going fast and sliding sideways feel convincing rather than arbitrary. | You can play it in your first five minutes, and you're still finding new technique a hundred hours in. |
| 4 | Strong, responsive handling with a genuine skill ceiling — most cars reward drift and boost technique clearly — but a portion of the roster (often the heaviest or earliest-unlocked vehicles) handles more generically, or the mastery curve flattens out once the core technique is learned. | The best cars in the game are a blast to push to the limit. A few feel like they're just along for the ride. |
| 3 | Driving feels fine and the basics work as expected, but the deeper mastery layer is thin — drifting doesn't meaningfully feed into a boost or scoring system, or near-miss/risk-reward mechanics are absent or inconsequential. Cars get you around the track competently without much to optimize. | It drives fine, but there's not much under the hood once you've learned the basic line. |
| 2 | Handling is inconsistent or floaty in ways that undercut confidence — cars feel like they're fighting the player rather than rewarding skill, drift mechanics are unpredictable, or speed feels arbitrary rather than earned through risk. | You can finish races, but good driving and bad driving don't feel meaningfully different. |
| 1 | Controls are unresponsive, broken, or absent of any technique to learn — collision and movement don't behave predictably, and there's nothing resembling a skill ceiling beyond holding the accelerator. | There's no driving here to get better at. |

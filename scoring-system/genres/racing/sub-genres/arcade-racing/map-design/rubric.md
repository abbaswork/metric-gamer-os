# Metric: Map & World Design
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Arcade Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = a richly varied, explorable world or track list built for discovery and replay, not just lap completion, 1 = a small handful of generic, navigation-restricted tracks

## Why This Matters

research.md lists "Good map" directly among the Reddit-sourced factors that define a good arcade racing game, and the signal is stronger from the search side: "Forza 6 map" and "Open World racing games" both appear as standalone Google Trends queries, and the keyword findings note recurring searches about the "location of" things in-game — players are actively researching where things are, which only happens when the world itself is treated as content worth navigating, not just a track to memorise.

This is also the clearest place arcade racing diverges from sim racing's equivalent metric, [Track Authenticity & Variety](../../sim-racing/track-authenticity/rubric.md). Sim racing scores tracks against real-world surface fidelity; arcade racing's audience isn't asking whether the map is real, it's asking whether the map rewards exploration. Forza Horizon 6's design philosophy is the clearest articulation of this: the game deliberately tasks players with "finding what they want to do next" rather than following icon-to-icon waypoints, uses a fog-of-war system that reveals the map only as you actually drive it, and layers a persistent, server-instanced Car Meet space on top of the open world where players congregate and spin up impromptu races — none of which has anything to do with how accurately a road surface is modelled.

The failure mode is an open world in name only. Need for Speed: Undercover nominally shipped an open-world city, but reviewers specifically called out that "the open world setting is misused" because many races were closed off from free roam rather than actually integrated into it — the world existed but didn't function as something worth exploring. That's the gap this rubric scores: a map can be large and still fail this metric if it doesn't give players a reason to go off the planned route.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | World or track list is large and varied enough to reward exploration on its own — shortcuts, hidden routes, and points of interest exist outside the critical path, and the map actively encourages players to go off-route rather than just funnelling them between waypoints. Multiple distinct environments or track types (city, off-road, coastal, mountain, etc.) are represented. | You'll find races and routes that nobody pointed you toward. |
| 4 | A large, varied map or track roster with strong environmental variety, but free exploration is restricted in places, or a portion of the world exists mainly as scenery between objectives rather than something with its own things to find. | Most of the world rewards a detour. A few areas are clearly just connective tissue. |
| 3 | A reasonable number of distinct tracks or a world of decent size, but layout variety is limited (similar environments repeated), or the map functions mainly as a route between events rather than a space worth exploring for its own sake. | The tracks do the job, but you're unlikely to go looking for something extra in them. |
| 2 | Track count is thin or the open world is nominal — large sections are closed off from free roam, or environments blur together with little to distinguish one from the next. | It's called open world, but most of it isn't actually open to you. |
| 1 | A handful of generic, interchangeable tracks or a world that exists mainly as a backdrop with no exploration value — navigation is heavily restricted and there's nothing to find off the critical path. | You're driving the same handful of roads no matter how you approach it. |

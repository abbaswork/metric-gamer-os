# Metric: Multiplayer & Online Racing
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Arcade Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = multiplayer is reliable and socially engaging, with real tools to keep races fun and clean, 1 = multiplayer barely functions or is dominated by unaccountable griefing

## Why This Matters

research.md's Reddit list for arcade racing doesn't name multiplayer directly, but the Google Trends data carries the signal instead: "Multiplayer racing games" and "Online racing games" both appear as standalone queries, alongside "Arcade racing versus sim racing games" — a comparison query that itself suggests players are actively weighing what kind of multiplayer experience they want from each side of the genre. That distinction matters for how this metric is scored: see [Multiplayer & League Ecosystem](../../sim-racing/multiplayer-leagues/rubric.md) for how sim racing's audience expects a structured competitive ladder. Arcade racing's audience isn't asking for a rating system or a league directory — the trend data points toward a more casual bar: does online or local multiplayer simply work, and is it fun to be in a lobby.

Forza Horizon's Car Meet is the genre's clearest example of what arcade multiplayer looks like when it's built as a social space rather than a competitive ladder — a live, server-instanced area where dozens of players gather to show off cars and spin up impromptu drag races on the spot, with EventLab/CoLab tools letting the community build and share custom multiplayer events anywhere on the map. That's the delight case: multiplayer as a hangout that generates its own activities, not just a queue into a race.

The recurring complaint across the genre's online modes is the opposite experience: unaccountable ramming and griefing. Forza Motorsport's own community forums describe "online ramming... killing this game" with "0 accountability," and F1 25 players report that intentional crashing makes public lobbies functionally unplayable because there's no in-game report system to act on it. The contrast case is constructive: titles that ship even a basic protest or report tool for ranked play give players a way to push back on intentional wrecking, which is the minimum infrastructure the complaint side of this metric is checking for.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Online and/or local multiplayer is stable and easy to get into, and the game gives players real tools to keep it enjoyable — a report or protest system with teeth, lobby/skill filtering, or a social space (a hangout area, drop-in events) that generates activities on its own rather than just queuing into races. | Hopping into multiplayer is easy, and the game actually backs you up if someone's wrecking it for everyone. |
| 4 | Multiplayer is reliable and generally fun, with some moderation or social tooling in place, but it's thinner than the genre's best (e.g., a report system with no real teeth, or a social hub with limited activity variety). | Multiplayer works and is fun most of the time, with the occasional lobby you just have to leave. |
| 3 | Multiplayer functions — races fill and run without major technical issues — but there's little to no tooling to manage bad actors, and no real social layer beyond the race itself. | It does the job. Don't expect the lobby to police itself, and don't expect much beyond the race. |
| 2 | Multiplayer is frequently undermined by ramming or griefing with no effective way to report or avoid it, or technical issues (poor matchmaking, connection problems) make races hard to complete reliably. | Getting into a race is hit or miss, and so is getting through one without someone wrecking it on purpose. |
| 1 | Multiplayer is barely functional — connection issues prevent races from completing, or public lobbies are dominated by unaccountable griefing with no tools to report, avoid, or escape it. | Online play isn't really an option if you want a clean race. |

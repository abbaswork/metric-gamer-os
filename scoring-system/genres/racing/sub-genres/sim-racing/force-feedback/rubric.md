# Metric: Force Feedback & Wheel Support
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Sim Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = deep native support across the wheel ecosystem with FFB that communicates grip and load in real time, 1 = narrow hardware compatibility and FFB that's flat or disconnected from the car

## Why This Matters

This is the most search-driven metric in the sub-genre's research. The keyword findings call out "queries about the best wheels and setup to play sim racing games on" as a top pattern, and four of the Google Trends entries are wheel hardware brands or products by name — Logitech, Moza Racing, Fanatec, and "Sim Racing wheels" itself as a standalone query. No other sim-racing theme in the research has this many dedicated hardware-brand search terms. Players are not just asking which game to play; a meaningful share are asking which wheel to buy and how to set it up, which makes wheel support and force feedback quality a primary purchase-adjacent concern, not a secondary one.

The reasoning the community gives for why this matters is consistent across sources: force feedback is described as the channel that turns "playing a game into something that feels like driving an actual car," because it's the only system that reports tire grip and load back to the player in real time rather than after the fact — sensing the wheel go light as the tires near their limit, or feeling resistance build through a loaded corner. Wheel hardware coverage is the prerequisite for any of that to reach the player at all: a sim with excellent physics is wasted on a setup the player's specific wheel base can't talk to properly.

Because the install base spans entry-level belt-driven wheels (Logitech) through high-end direct-drive bases (Fanatec, Moza, Simucube), this metric has to score both breadth of compatibility and depth of FFB tuning — a title that only does one well (broad compatibility with shallow generic FFB, or excellent FFB tuning on three supported wheel bases) is leaving real player experience on the table either way.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Native, plug-and-play support spans budget through high-end wheel bases (e.g., Logitech, Moza, Fanatec, Simucube) with deep in-sim FFB tuning — gain, damping, and separate effect channels for road texture, kerbs, and slip. FFB distinctly telegraphs grip loss, weight transfer, and surface change, to the point experienced players can drive by feel alone. Pedal and load-cell hardware get full per-axis calibration. | Whatever wheel you own, plugging it in gets you the real experience — and the wheel is genuinely telling you what the tires are doing. |
| 4 | Broad support across most major wheel brands with solid default FFB profiles out of the box. A few niche or very new wheel bases need third-party tools or community configs to feel right. The big moments — lock-up, oversteer — come through clearly even if the finest surface detail is muted. | Most setups just work well. Owners of newer or less common hardware may need to hunt down a community guide first. |
| 3 | Major brands are supported, but tuning is shallow — gain and overall strength sliders exist without deeper per-effect control. FFB confirms that something is happening at the tires without giving much resolution on what exactly. | The wheel works and gives you the basics, but you won't get the fine-grained feel the genre's best titles offer. |
| 2 | Support is limited to a handful of wheel bases, or requires workaround drivers and community patches to function at all. FFB is present but generic — similar regardless of car or tire state — or clips and oscillates unpredictably under load. | If your wheel isn't on the short list of well-supported hardware, expect a rough setup process and a feed that doesn't tell you much. |
| 1 | Wheel support is an afterthought. Major hardware brands have broken or absent FFB, force feedback is flat regardless of what the car is doing, or the game is clearly built around gamepad/keyboard input with wheel support bolted on as an afterthought. | A wheel buys you a steering input device here, not a feedback channel. |

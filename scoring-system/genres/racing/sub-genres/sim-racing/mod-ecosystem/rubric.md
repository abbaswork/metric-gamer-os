# Metric: Mod Ecosystem & Community Content
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Sim Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = a deep mod ecosystem with developer-facilitated tools where community content scales the game far beyond its official release, 1 = a completely closed platform where official content is the only option

## Why This Matters

research.md's keyword findings call out "Lots of queries about mods for assetto corsa" as a top search pattern, and three of the Google Trends entries are specifically about mod tooling rather than the game itself: "assetto corsa mod," "assetto corsa content manager," and "custom shaders patch." No other dimension of the sub-genre generates this kind of tool-specific search traffic — players aren't just looking up cars or tracks, they're researching the infrastructure around community content. That's the signal: modding in sim racing isn't a niche hobby, it's a primary way the audience extends and maintains the value of a title long after release.

Assetto Corsa is the reference case. Its community has produced over 7,700 car mods and 1,200 track mods across dedicated platforms (AssettoWorld, AcMods, OverTake.gg, ModLand), representing more content than any official sim racing title has shipped. The infrastructure that makes this usable is itself community-built: Content Manager — a free third-party launcher trusted by hundreds of thousands of players — reduces mod installation to dragging a ZIP file onto its window, which auto-detects the mod type and handles placement automatically. Custom Shaders Patch (CSP) goes further: it extends Assetto Corsa's core engine with dynamic lighting, weather physics including rain, improved tire heating, dynamic track temperatures, VR support, and brake migration physics — effectively modernizing a 12-year-old game to near-contemporary standards, and acting as a foundational dependency that many other mods build on top of. The combined effect is that the drift, touge, and time-attack communities have made Assetto Corsa their permanent home specifically because its mod ecosystem enables disciplines and venues that no official title would greenlight.

The deliberate absence of modding is a real and accepted trade-off at the other end of the scale. iRacing's closed platform — no user-submitted content, all cars and tracks controlled by official development — is explicitly justified by competitive integrity: a closed asset pipeline guarantees that every car every driver in every online race is identical, which is the premise the rating system and official esports depend on (see [Multiplayer & League Ecosystem](../multiplayer-leagues/rubric.md)). That makes iRacing's 1 on this metric a feature trade-off rather than a pure failure — but it means solo and offline players and anyone who wants a discipline iRacing doesn't officially support are left with whatever the official release provides, nothing more.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The community has produced thousands of cars and tracks available through dedicated mod platforms, with volume and quality that exceeds the official content library. Developer-facilitated or community-built tooling makes installation trivial (drag-and-drop or equivalent), and foundational mods extend the game's core capabilities — adding weather systems, physics improvements, or engine-level enhancements the base game lacks. Specialist communities (drift, touge, historic racing, specific national series) have chosen this platform as their home precisely because the mod ecosystem enables what official content doesn't. | The game you bought is the foundation. What the community has built on top of it is the reason people are still playing it a decade later. |
| 4 | A healthy mod scene with hundreds of community cars, tracks, and liveries available through official or well-known community channels. Installation is supported (an official in-game repository or a widely-used launcher), and submitted mods go through a quality or review process. Volume is meaningful but not at the scale where it overwhelms the official content library. | There's enough community content to substantially extend the life of the game beyond its official release, with a clear path to finding and installing it. |
| 3 | Community mods exist and are accessible, but installation requires manual file management with no official tooling or widely-adopted launcher. The library covers the most-requested content gaps (a handful of popular real-world tracks or cars not in the official roster) without growing into a broad ecosystem. | There's something out there if you're willing to manage it yourself — just not a scene that meaningfully extends the game. |
| 2 | Mod support is officially absent or restricted, with a small community producing workarounds that require significant technical effort to install and may break between updates. The developer doesn't facilitate modding and may actively work against it. | Getting mods working is a project in itself, and what's available is limited enough that it doesn't change the game's fundamental content ceiling. |
| 1 | The platform is completely closed — no user-submitted content is permitted, all cars, tracks, and content are controlled by official development. This may be a deliberate trade-off (e.g., for competitive integrity) but means the content library is hard-capped at whatever the developer has shipped. | What launched is what you get. No community track, car, or enhancement can extend it. |

# Metric: Track Authenticity & Variety
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Sim Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = a wide, accurately modelled selection of real-world circuits spanning multiple track types, 1 = a small handful of generic or inaccurately modelled tracks

## Why This Matters

research.md lists "accurate tracks" and "good track variety" directly among the Reddit-sourced factors that define a good sim racing game, and "nurburgring" appears as its own standalone Google Trends query — notable because it's a single circuit earning dedicated search volume, which only happens for tracks the community treats as a benchmark of authenticity rather than just another stage to race on.

The reason track accuracy carries this much weight in sim racing specifically (versus arcade racing, where invented tracks are the norm) is that the track surface is itself part of the physics simulation, not just the backdrop for it. Laser-scanning technology captures real circuits down to millimeter-level surface detail — cracks, camber, undulation — and a road course typically needs more than 250 million scan points to reproduce. That detail isn't cosmetic: a properly scanned track lets the same surface irregularities that affect a real car's grip and balance affect the simulated one, including how weather and tire heat interact with specific patches of track over a race. A track that's only correct in layout but not surface fidelity removes a layer of the physics the rest of the simulator is trying to model.

Variety matters alongside accuracy because the sub-genre's appeal spans genuinely different disciplines — high-speed ovals, technical road courses, and street circuits each demand different car setups and racecraft. A roster that's accurate but narrow (all road courses, for instance) under-serves a meaningful share of the audience the same way a roster that's broad but low-fidelity does.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Track roster spans high-speed ovals, technical road courses, and street circuits, with marquee real-world venues (e.g., Nürburgring Nordschleife, Spa-Francorchamps, Daytona) built via laser-scanning or equivalent fidelity. Surface undulation, camber, and bumps match the real circuit closely enough that real-world drivers recognise specific corners by feel. | You can build genuine track knowledge here that would hold up if you walked the real circuit. |
| 4 | A strong roster of real-world tracks across multiple types, with marquee venues built to a high standard. A portion of the roster — DLC packs, less prominent venues — is modelled to a noticeably lower fidelity than the flagship tracks. | The headline tracks are excellent. Expect a step down in detail once you get past the venues the game is built around. |
| 3 | A reasonable number of real-world tracks, but variety skews toward one type (mostly road courses, with ovals or street circuits thin or absent), or layout accuracy is solid while the fine-grained bump and camber detail that actually affects car behaviour is missing. | The tracks are recognisable and raceable, but won't surprise you with the texture the genre's best titles capture. |
| 2 | Track count is thin or leans heavily on fictional or generic layouts. Included real-world tracks are recognisable in shape but don't replicate surface character, making them feel interchangeable with each other. | Tracks blur together — you're racing a layout, not a place. |
| 1 | Few tracks, little to no variety in type, and minimal real-world fidelity even where real venues are nominally included. | Laps feel the same regardless of which track is loaded. |

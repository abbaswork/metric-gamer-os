# Metric: Physics & Handling Realism
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Sim Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = the car's behaviour communicates real-world vehicle dynamics accurately enough that skills transfer, 1 = handling is arcade-tuned despite the sim label

## Why This Matters

Physics is the trait that defines the sim-racing sub-genre against arcade racing in the first place — it's the entire reason the audience exists. research.md names "realism," "good physics engine," and "Good driving feel" directly among the Reddit-sourced factors players use to judge a good sim racing game, and the Google Trends list is dominated by titles whose entire identity rests on physics fidelity (Assetto Corsa, iRacing, Automobilista 2, Le Mans Ultimate). The keyword findings reinforce this from the search side: a large share of queries are about setup and tuning for these specific simulators, which only makes sense if the underlying physics model rewards that depth of tinkering.

The community debate isn't whether realism matters, but how it should be balanced against feel — a distinction that matters for how this rubric is scored. Forum discussion of "is realism the most important thing for a race sim" (OverTake.gg) converges on the idea that a convincing physics model has to cooperate with controls and feedback to produce a believable illusion; modelling alone isn't the bar, modelling that holds up under real driving inputs is. This is consistent with how the genre's flagship titles differentiate themselves: comparisons between iRacing and Assetto Corsa Competizione's tire models repeatedly come back to how each car's grip degrades and how weight transfer is communicated under load, not just whether lap times are fast or slow.

The practical test the community applies is whether skill learned in the sim transfers — to other sims, and in some cases to real cars. iRacing's marketing and community both lean on real-world partnerships (NASCAR, IMSA, INDYCAR) and pro drivers using the platform for practice as proof that its physics model is a valid proxy for seat time. That's the high bar this rubric scores against: not just "does it feel different from an arcade racer," but "does driving it teach real racecraft."

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Tire model simulates thermal buildup, wear, and surface-specific grip (rubber laid down, marbles off-line, wet-to-dry transitions) in ways that visibly change race strategy lap to lap. Weight transfer and load are communicated through the car's behaviour before a mistake happens, not just after. The model holds up well enough that real-world drivers and series use it as a practice tool, and skills learned here transfer to other top-tier sims. | The car teaches you. Mistakes that would catch a real driver off guard catch you the same way, and the lessons stick. |
| 4 | Strong physics across most of the roster — weight transfer and tire wear are convincingly modelled for the simulator's flagship car classes. A handful of cars or older content feel noticeably less refined than the best-modelled vehicles. Surface differences (kerbs, off-line grip) register clearly even if not at the finest grain. | The best cars in the game are genuinely excellent teachers. A few corners of the content library don't hold up to the same standard. |
| 3 | Cars generally obey real-world principles — braking distances and cornering loads feel plausible — but the deeper systems are simplified. Tire wear and thermal effects exist without much granularity, or weight transfer is modelled similarly across very different vehicle classes. Good enough to teach racecraft fundamentals without rewarding the deepest car-control skills. | You'll learn real lines and braking points, but the car won't punish or reward the finer points of car control the way the genre's best titles do. |
| 2 | Physics break down in specific, predictable ways — cars feel underdamped or floaty, kerb and contact behaviour doesn't match real-world expectations, or most vehicle classes handle with a similar baseline regardless of how different they should feel. Skills built here don't transfer convincingly elsewhere. | You can post fast lap times without learning anything that holds up outside this specific game. |
| 1 | Handling is arcade-tuned despite the sim positioning — cornering grip feels roughly constant regardless of tire state or surface, weight transfer is scripted or absent, and contact resolves unrealistically. Nothing about driving the car builds transferable racecraft. | It plays like an arcade racer wearing a simulator's branding. |

# Metric: Physics, Handling & Force Feedback
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Sim Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = the car's behaviour communicates real-world vehicle dynamics accurately enough that skills transfer, backed by force feedback that reports grip and load to the wheel in real time; 1 = handling is arcade-tuned despite the sim label and wheels receive flat or broken feedback

## Why This Matters

Physics is the trait that defines the sim-racing sub-genre against arcade racing in the first place — it's the entire reason the audience exists. research.md names "realism," "good physics engine," "Good driving feel," and "good wheel support" directly among the Reddit-sourced factors players use to judge a good sim racing game, and the Google Trends list is dominated by titles whose entire identity rests on physics fidelity (Assetto Corsa, iRacing, Automobilista 2, Le Mans Ultimate). The keyword findings reinforce this from two directions at once: a large share of queries are about setup and tuning for specific simulators (which only makes sense if the underlying physics model rewards that depth of tinkering), and separately, four Google Trends entries are wheel hardware brands or products by name — Logitech, Moza Racing, Fanatec, and "Sim Racing wheels" itself as a standalone query. Players are simultaneously asking how to tune the car and which wheel to buy to feel it properly.

Those two search patterns point to the same underlying relationship: **force feedback is the channel that delivers physics information to the player.** The sim-racing community describes FFB as "what turns playing a game into something that feels like driving an actual car," because it's the only system that reports tire grip and load back to the player in real time rather than after the fact — sensing the wheel go light as the tires near their limit, or feeling resistance build through a loaded corner. A sim with an excellent physics model is only as useful as what the player's wheel base can translate back to their hands. This is why the two axes belong in the same metric: the physics floor sets what there is to feel, and force feedback quality determines how much of it actually reaches the driver.

The community debate on physics isn't whether realism matters, but how modelling cooperates with controls and feedback to produce a believable illusion — modelling alone isn't the bar, modelling that holds up under real driving inputs is. The practical test is whether skill learned in the sim transfers — to other sims, and in some cases to real cars. iRacing's marketing and community both lean on real-world partnerships (NASCAR, IMSA, INDYCAR) and pro drivers using the platform for practice as proof that its physics model is a valid proxy for seat time. That's the high bar: not just "does it feel different from an arcade racer," but "does driving it teach real racecraft." Force feedback sits at the same high bar: at 5, experienced players can drive by feel alone, with the wheel communicating grip loss and surface transitions before the visual cues confirm them.

## Rubric

Scores are assessed across two axes:
- **Physics & Handling** — tire model depth, weight transfer communication, surface-specific grip, skill transferability
- **Force Feedback** — breadth of wheel hardware support, FFB resolution and per-effect tuning, real-time grip communication

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | **Physics:** Tire model simulates thermal buildup, wear, and surface-specific grip (rubber laid down on-line, marbles off-line, wet-to-dry transitions) in ways that visibly change race strategy lap to lap. Weight transfer is communicated through the car's behaviour before a mistake happens, not just after. The model is credible enough that real-world drivers and series use it for practice, and skills transfer to other top-tier sims. **Force Feedback:** Native plug-and-play support spans budget through high-end wheel bases (Logitech, Moza, Fanatec, Simucube) with deep in-sim FFB tuning — separate effect channels for road texture, kerbs, and slip. FFB distinctly telegraphs grip loss, weight transfer, and surface change; experienced players can drive by feel alone without relying on visual cues. | The car teaches you. Mistakes that would catch a real driver off guard catch you the same way — and you feel them through the wheel before you see them on screen. |
| 4 | Strong physics across most of the roster with convincingly modelled weight transfer and tire wear for flagship car classes; a handful of cars or older content are noticeably less refined. Surface differences (kerbs, off-line grip) register clearly. FFB supports most major wheel brands with solid default profiles, and the big moments — lock-up, oversteer onset — come through clearly, even if fine surface texture is muted; niche or very new hardware may need community configs to feel right. | The best cars and the best setups are genuinely excellent teachers. A few corners of the roster or the hardware list don't hold up to the same standard. |
| 3 | Cars obey real-world principles — braking distances and cornering loads feel plausible — but deeper systems are simplified: tire wear and thermal effects exist without granularity, and weight transfer is modelled similarly across very different vehicle classes. FFB supports major brands but tuning is shallow (gain and strength sliders only, no per-effect control); feedback confirms that something is happening at the tires without giving much resolution on what exactly. | You'll learn real lines and braking points, but neither the car nor the wheel will reward the finer points of car control the way the genre's best titles do. |
| 2 | Physics break down in specific, predictable ways — cars feel underdamped or floaty, kerb and contact behaviour doesn't match real-world expectations, or most vehicle classes share a similar handling baseline regardless of how different they should feel. FFB is limited to a handful of wheel bases or requires workaround drivers; feedback is generic regardless of car or tire state, or clips and oscillates unpredictably under load. | You can post fast lap times without learning anything that transfers outside this game, and the wheel isn't telling you enough to change that. |
| 1 | Handling is arcade-tuned despite the sim positioning — cornering grip feels roughly constant regardless of tire state or surface, weight transfer is scripted or absent, and contact resolves unrealistically. Wheel support is an afterthought: major hardware brands have broken or absent FFB, or force feedback is flat regardless of what the car is doing. | It plays like an arcade racer wearing a simulator's branding, and the wheel is just a steering input device. |

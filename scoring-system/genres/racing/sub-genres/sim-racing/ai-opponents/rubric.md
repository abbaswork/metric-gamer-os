# Metric: AI Opponent Quality
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Sim Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = AI races at a consistent, believable pace set by genuine skill, 1 = AI pace is artificially tied to the player's position or behaves unrealistically

## Why This Matters

research.md lists "good AI oponents" directly among the Reddit-sourced factors that define a good sim racing game. AI carries more weight in sim racing than the wording suggests, because it's the only opponent available outside of organised online sessions — for solo practice, time-limited play, or anyone not racing in a structured league (see [Multiplayer & League Ecosystem](../multiplayer-leagues/rubric.md)), AI quality is the entire offline experience.

The community's specific, repeated complaint is rubber-banding: AI pace that ties itself to the player's track position rather than racing at a fixed skill level. Players describe the failure mode precisely — it "punishes good driving" and "rewards bad driving," and once understood, it becomes exploitable rather than challenging: a driver can deliberately sit mid-pack "within striking distance of the win" and stay there until the final lap, at which point the artificial tension evaporates. The frustration isn't abstract difficulty-tuning disagreement; it's that the AI's pace stops meaning anything once a player learns the field will always stay close regardless of actual gap. A second, related complaint is AI that resorts to unrealistic catch-up behaviour — speed bursts that close a gap and then convert into contact rather than a clean pass, sometimes described as "kamikaze" driving.

The alternative the community holds up as correct is AI whose pace comes from a real performance approximation of the track and car, with difficulty exposed to the player as an explicit strength setting rather than baked-in position-tracking — race results then reflect the gap between the player's actual pace and a fixed target, which is what makes winning or losing legible and earns the result.

## Sources

- **research.md — Reddit (What makes a good Sim racing game):** "good AI oponents"
- **Steam Community (Wreckfest discussions) — "Rubberbanding AI is dumb and counterproductive!":** "It punishes good driving" and "It rewards bad driving"; players "learn the sweet spot in the pack, where you are within striking distance of the win" and exploit it; "once you know HOW to deal with rubberbanding AI, that is NOT the case [exciting]" — "There is no challenge" (web search)
- **Autosport Forums — "AI in race games":** community discussion of kamikaze AI behaviour — speed bursts used to catch up that resolve into contact rather than a clean overtake, and proposals for AI pace driven by a fixed per-track performance approximation with a player-facing difficulty slider instead of position-based scaling (web search)

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | AI lap times and race pace are set by a fixed skill/aggression level — often exposed as an adjustable strength setting — rather than the player's track position. AI overtakes and defends using realistic racecraft (real braking points, defensive lines) and respects normal racing etiquette, including avoiding contact that a competent real driver would avoid. | A gap you build is a gap that stays earned. Wins and losses both mean something. |
| 4 | AI is mostly consistent and skill-based, with rare moments of obviously scripted catch-up pace, or one or two repeatable, exploitable behaviours (e.g., a predictable braking point at one corner) that don't undermine the overall race. | The challenge holds up across most of a race weekend, with only the occasional moment that breaks the illusion. |
| 3 | AI provides a reasonable challenge, but attentive players can detect pace that noticeably tightens or loosens based on their track position. Contact avoidance is inconsistent — AI sometimes drives through a clearly contested corner without adjusting. | The race is fun until you notice the pattern — after that, you can predict when the field will close back up. |
| 2 | AI pace is clearly rubber-banded: falling behind doesn't stick, and a comfortable lead evaporates after a single player mistake. AI displays repeatable unrealistic behaviour, such as dive-bomb passing attempts or speed bursts that ignore the player's position on track. | You can build a lead through good driving and watch it disappear for reasons that have nothing to do with your pace. |
| 1 | AI race pace is tied almost entirely to the player's position rather than genuine performance. Races carry no real stakes because the field stays artificially close regardless of skill gap, and AI routinely causes unavoidable contact or fails to react to the player's car at all. | Offline racing isn't really racing — it's a scripted approximation that ignores how well you actually drove. |

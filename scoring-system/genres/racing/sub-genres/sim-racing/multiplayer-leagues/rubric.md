# Metric: Multiplayer & League Ecosystem
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Sim Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = a structured competitive ecosystem (rating systems, leagues, official esports) that gives races real consequence, 1 = unstructured public lobbies with no rating or league infrastructure

## Why This Matters

research.md names "leagues," "good multiplayer support," and "professional eSports" directly among the Reddit-sourced factors players use to judge a good sim racing game — and unlike arcade racing, where multiplayer is mostly about having a working online mode at all, sim racing's audience expects multiplayer to be a structured competitive ladder, not just a matchmaking queue.

The reason structure matters this much is that sim racing's physics fidelity is wasted in a lobby where contact is unaccountable. iRacing's safety rating and incident-point system is the genre's reference implementation: incidents accumulate against a driver regardless of fault, a hard cap disqualifies repeat offenders mid-race, and license progression rewards drivers who finish clean over drivers who are merely fast — which is precisely the mechanism that keeps higher-rated lobbies racing the way the simulator's physics deserve to be raced. Without something equivalent, online lobbies default to the "unranked public lobby" failure mode the community associates with contact and griefing.

Leagues and esports are the layer above matchmaking. The iRacing league directory alone lists hundreds of active leagues spanning disciplines, and the platform runs prize-backed official series — the FIA F4 Esports Global Championship and the INDYCAR Thrustmaster Pro Series among them — that connect directly to real-world racing bodies and, in some cases, real driver development pathways. That structure is what turns "I raced online" into "I have a season, a series, and stakes," which is the multiplayer experience this sub-genre's audience is specifically asking for.

- **iRacing League Directory (iracingleagues.com):** hundreds of active leagues listed across disciplines (web search)

- **simracingcockpit.gg — "iRacing Safety Rating, Explained":** incidents accumulate as net points regardless of fault; a hard cap (commonly 17x) results in disqualification; license progression rewards drivers who "finish consistently with single-digit incident counts" over the fastest drivers (web search)

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | A built-in rating or license system gates lobbies by skill and incident history, keeping races clean by default. An active in-game or closely tied league directory spans dozens to hundreds of leagues across disciplines. An official, prize-backed esports pathway exists and connects to real-world racing series or driver development. | A season, a series, and stakes are available the moment you want them — and the lobby you land in matches your skill and clean-driving record. |
| 4 | A skill or safety rating system meaningfully separates lobby quality. A healthy third-party or community league scene exists even without deep in-game tooling. Some organised competitive structure (cups, championships) exists without a fully developed esports pipeline. | You can find a clean, competitive season without much friction — it just may live in a community Discord rather than the game's own menus. |
| 3 | Online races run reliably, but matchmaking doesn't account for skill or incident history beyond basic filters. Leagues exist mainly through external community organisation rather than in-game support. No real esports presence. | Multiplayer works, but building a structured season is on you to find and organise. |
| 2 | Online play is largely unranked public lobbies prone to contact and griefing, with no incident accountability. League play, if it exists, is fragmented and hard to discover. | Expect inconsistent lobbies and real effort required to find a clean, organised race. |
| 1 | Online multiplayer is an afterthought — a small or unstable player base, no rating or incident system, and no organised league or competitive scene to speak of. | There's no real path from "raced online once" to an actual season. |

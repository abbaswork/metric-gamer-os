# Metric: Multiplayer & Split-Screen
**Genre:** Racing
**Sub-genre:** Kart Racing
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = local split-screen and online racing are both fully supported and can be combined, with split-screen players able to join the same online lobby, 1 = multiplayer barely functions, locally or online

## Why This Matters

research.md's Reddit list names "good online multiplayer" and "split screen" as two separate factors — and unlike arcade racing's [Multiplayer & Online Racing](../../arcade-racing/multiplayer/rubric.md), where the Google Trends evidence pointed mainly toward online play, kart racing's own research puts split-screen on equal footing with it. That tracks with the sub-genre's identity: kart racers are the couch co-op reference point for the entire racing genre, going back to four-player Mario Kart 64 nights long before "online multiplayer" was a category at all.

Mario Kart 8 Deluxe — research.md's top-ranked entry — and its successor Mario Kart World set the bar for combining the two: local split-screen players can join the same online lobby together, so a living room of four people can race against players from anywhere rather than choosing between local and online play. That capability comes with a real, well-documented tradeoff — Mario Kart 8 Deluxe runs 2-player split-screen at a full 1080p/60fps but drops to 30fps once a third or fourth player joins, a performance compromise players have specifically asked Nintendo to let them trade for lower resolution instead, since each quadrant of a 4-player screen is small enough that the frame-rate drop reads as the bigger loss.

Sonic Racing: CrossWorlds — also directly named in research.md's Top Games list — is the contrast case for what that combination actually requires: it supports local split-screen for up to 4 players in offline modes, and separately supports online racing with crossplay matchmaking, but online play is strictly one player per device — split-screen racers cannot join an online lobby together at all. That gap is exactly what separates the top of this scale from the middle: having both modes individually isn't the same as letting players use them together.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Both local split-screen (up to at least 4 players) and online play are fully supported, and the two can be combined — a split-screen group can join the same online lobby together rather than choosing one mode or the other. Online connections are stable enough that races complete without frequent drops. | However you want to play with friends — same couch, across the country, or both at once — the game gets out of your way and lets you. |
| 4 | Both local split-screen and online play work well independently, but they can't be combined, or one of the two carries a real cost (e.g., a meaningful frame-rate or resolution drop in 4-player split-screen, or occasional online connection drops). | Local and online multiplayer both deliver, you just can't mix them, or you'll notice a real tradeoff in one mode. |
| 3 | Local split-screen and/or online play function but in a limited form — split-screen capped at 2 players, or online matchmaking is slow or thin — with no option to combine local and online groups. | You can play with others, just not in every way you might want to. |
| 2 | Multiplayer technically works but is frequently undermined by connection drops, severe performance degradation in split-screen, or matchmaking that rarely fills lobbies. | Getting into a race with friends is more often a hassle than a given. |
| 1 | Multiplayer barely functions — split-screen or online modes are absent, broken, or so degraded that completing a race together isn't realistic. | Racing with other people isn't really on the table. |

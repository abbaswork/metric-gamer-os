# Metric: On-Pitch Match Feel
**Genre:** Sports
**Sub-genre:** Football
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = shooting, passing, and dribbling carry real physical weight and reward skill, 1 = ball physics feel arbitrary or broken

## Why This Matters

research.md's Reddit findings put "the dopamine spike when scoring a goal" and "high skill ceiling and competitive environment" at the center of what players want from a football game — and this is the one theme in the pool with zero equivalent in a management sim. You can't feel a goal you didn't physically score yourself. Trends data backs this up with a video-game-specific (not tournament) signal: "football video game" sits at 87 interest (+3%) and "world cup video games" spikes +700% — both phrased to disambiguate from the real-world tournament, confirming genuine demand for how these games actually play, not just license/roster news.

This metric is also the foundational fork the genre has been built on for over two decades. The long-running EA Sports FC/FIFA vs. Pro Evolution Soccer rivalry was fought almost entirely on this axis: PES built its reputation on weightier, more deliberate ball physics and a higher skill ceiling on dribbling and first touch, while FIFA/FC prioritised faster, more arcade-accessible responsiveness aimed at a broader audience. Community discussion makes the stakes of getting this wrong explicit: FIFA/FC in recent iterations has been criticised for ball physics that feel "like a balloon, not natural like PES," with player movement described as "floating or ice skating." Players on the other side specifically praise games that produce "many bounces, rebounds, shot trajectories and goal-mouth scrambles over more rigid, one-dimensional shooting mechanics" — the feel of a live, physical encounter rather than a scripted one. Neither heavier nor lighter physics is objectively correct, but a game that commits to neither fails both audiences at once.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Shooting, passing, and first touch carry real physical weight — you feel the difference between a rushed control and a cushioned one, a side-foot tap-in and a curled finish. Skill moves and close-control dribbling reward practice with a visible ceiling above the AI's default behaviour. | The better player at the controls wins the ball battle, not the better stats sheet. |
| 4 | Strong, responsive ball physics with clear feedback on contact quality, but a specific situation undercuts it — aerial duels, first-time finishes, or 50/50 tackles feel more scripted or "canned" than the rest of the game. | Most of the match feels great. A few moments remind you it's still a simulation. |
| 3 | The ball does what you tell it to, but momentum and weight feel generic across situations — most goals come from positioning and AI gaps rather than a won physical or technical duel. | It plays fine. You're rarely fighting the controls, but you're rarely impressed by them either. |
| 2 | Controls feel sluggish or input-delayed relative to on-screen action; shots, passes, and tackles produce outcomes that don't track with what you actually did. | You can finish a match, but you spend it fighting the controls more than the opponent. |
| 1 | Ball physics are absent or broken — scoring, passing, and dribbling outcomes feel arbitrary regardless of input. No technique to learn, no skill expressed. | There's no football here to get better at. |

# Metric: Monetisation
**Genre:** Sports
**Sub-genre:** Football (niche: Free)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = free-to-play progression is achievable and fair without paying, 1 = competitive viability is effectively locked behind spending

## Why This Matters

This niche metric swaps in for Content & Game Modes (action sims) or Transfer Market & Squad Building (free management sims like Top Eleven) when scoring a free-to-play page. eFootball and FC Ultimate Team-centric content both monetise primarily through randomised player/pack acquisition — a model that has attracted some of the most sustained regulatory and community scrutiny of any monetisation system in gaming.

The regulatory record is specific. Belgium's Gaming Commission ruled in April 2018 that FIFA Ultimate Team packs constituted "games of chance" in breach of the country's gambling laws. Belgium's Minister of Justice Koen Geens stated: "It is often children who come into contact with such systems and we cannot allow that." EA initially contested the ruling, then capitulated — disabling FIFA Points purchases for Belgian players entirely, so pack acquisition could only happen through in-game earning. Under Belgian law, non-compliance carries fines up to €800,000 and prison sentences up to five years. The Netherlands took a similar position; Austria's Supreme Court reached the opposite conclusion in January 2026. The UK Gambling Commission's advisory body reviewed the issue internationally. The regulatory divergence is still live, meaning how aggressively a game's free-to-play economy is designed has real consequences beyond community opinion.

The complaint isn't that monetisation exists — it's whether competitive viability is realistically achievable through free play, or whether the gap between paying and non-paying squads becomes wide enough that spending, not skill, decides who wins.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | A competitive squad is realistically achievable through free play within a reasonable timeframe; paid options accelerate progress but don't unlock anything free play genuinely can't reach. | You can compete with players who spent money — it just takes you longer to get there. |
| 4 | Free progression is viable but slower than intended, or a specific tier of content (a particular event or top-end player pool) is meaningfully harder to reach without spending. | You can get most of the way there for free. The very top is a real grind or a real cost. |
| 3 | Free play gets you a functional squad, but the gap to paying players is large enough to be a regular disadvantage in competitive modes. | You can play. You'll notice who paid and who didn't. |
| 2 | Meaningful competitive progress requires spending in practice, even if it's technically possible for free — the free path is disproportionately slow or randomised. | The game is "free," but competing seriously isn't, really. |
| 1 | Competitive viability is effectively locked behind spending — free-to-play progression cannot realistically reach a competitive squad. | This is a paid game wearing a free price tag. |

# Checklist Writing Guide

This guide must be read before writing any metric score checklist.

---

## What a checklist is

One sentence per score band (5 through 1), written the way a knowledgeable human game reviewer would actually write — not a rubric paraphrase, not AI imitating a reviewer. It lives on the game page and gives readers an immediate, plain-language read of what each score means.

Each sentence summarises the **Description** column of the rubric band — the substantive criteria that define the score. Do not summarise the "What This Means for You" column. That column is a one-liner consequence or takeaway; the checklist needs to tell the reader what the score actually requires, not just what it feels like to receive it.

---

## Before you write

1. Read the rubric in full
2. Identify the single core dimension the metric measures — this is your thread
3. Find the defining characteristic of each band in the rubric description
4. Write one sentence per band that captures that characteristic in plain reviewer voice

---

## Dos

- Write a proper, complete sentence — one clear thought with a subject and verb
- Use simple, plain language that anyone would understand
- Capture the defining characteristic of each band from the rubric faithfully — don't miss what makes that band distinct from the one above it
- Thread the same core dimension through all five bands at decreasing intensity — every band answers the same question at a lower level
- Be specific and concrete about what the experience actually is

---

## Don'ts

- No jargon: words like "throughline," "competent," "overarching," "consistently engaging," "archetypes," "routing choices," "accessible routes" are all examples of this
- No comma splices — two independent clauses joined by a comma is not a sentence
- No listing features instead of describing the experience
- No em-dashes
- No clever-sounding phrases that read as AI rather than human
- Don't paraphrase the rubric mechanically — synthesise the felt experience
- Don't summarise the "What This Means for You" column — summarise the Description; the checklist must capture the substance of the rubric band, not just the consequence

---

## Good examples

"Every mission feels fresh, introducing new ideas and memorable moments without falling into repetitive filler."

"Every combat arena feels distinct, with varied layouts and meaningful positioning that keep firefights engaging."

"A strong cast of distinct, well-written characters each has a clear arc that develops over time, with dialogue that feels personal, memorable, and genuinely human."

"A varied cast of memorable characters offers meaningful relationships that unlock new dialogue, events, and story content over time."

---

## Bad examples

"Each combat space has its own shape and offers real positional choices, with cover, elevation, and geometry that reward knowing where to be."
— Lists features (cover, elevation, geometry) instead of describing the experience of playing in those spaces.

"Most characters are well-written with genuine arcs, though a few exist mainly as quest-givers, and the central throughline is consistently engaging."
— "Throughline" and "consistently engaging" are editorial filler no real reviewer would write.

"layouts vary, cover creates angles, geometry rewards positioning"
— Comma splice. Not a sentence.

"Every NPC feels like a distinct person, and the deeper you get with them the more the game gives you in return."
— Trying to sound clever. Reads as AI, not human.

---

## The test

Read the sentence aloud. Ask: would a real person who knows this genre actually write this, or does it sound like a machine trying to sound human?

# Metric: Squad Play & Class Design
**Genre:** Shooters
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = classes meaningfully differentiate squad roles and reward coordinated play, 1 = classes are cosmetic-only and squads offer no mechanical benefit over playing solo

## Why This Matters

Large-scale multiplayer shooters live or die on whether squads function as more than a group of players standing near each other. Battlefield 5's class-and-squad system was singled out specifically for mechanics like fortification — squads that build and defend positions together get a tangible advantage that solo play can't replicate. The community's interest tracks closely with whether a game's classes actually create interdependence: a Medic who can revive, an Engineer who can repair vehicles, a Support player who can resupply ammo. When these roles are real, squads have a reason to stick together and communicate. When classes are reskinned versions of the same loadout with no functional split, "squad play" becomes a lobby UI label rather than a system.

This is distinct from general teamplay in arena shooters. The defining trait here is mechanical role specialization tied to squad-level cooperation — not just "5 v 5 with comms," but classes built so that a squad with all four roles filled plays meaningfully differently from four players running the same loadout.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Classes have non-overlapping abilities that create real interdependence — reviving, repairing, resupplying, or fortifying are tied to specific roles. A full squad with all roles filled has a clear mechanical edge over four players in the same loadout. | Filling out a squad's roles changes what your team can do. Coordination has a payoff you can feel. |
| 4 | Classes have distinct abilities with strong role identity, though one or two roles see more use than others because their value is higher in most situations. Squad cooperation is rewarded but not strictly required to function. | You'll gravitate to a role and feel its impact, even if the squad doesn't always need every class filled. |
| 3 | Classes exist and have nominally different loadouts, but the practical difference between playing one class versus another is small in most matches. Squad mechanics (revives, resupply) work without meaningfully changing strategy. | You can tell the classes apart on paper. In a match, picking one over another rarely changes much. |
| 2 | Classes are mostly cosmetic distinctions over a shared core kit. Squad-specific mechanics are minor conveniences rather than systems worth building a strategy around. | Squading up feels social, not tactical. The class menu doesn't change how you play. |
| 1 | No meaningful class distinction, or classes exist without any squad-level mechanic tying them together. Playing solo and playing in a coordinated squad produce the same experience. | There's no real reason to organize as a squad. You could be four strangers and nothing would change. |

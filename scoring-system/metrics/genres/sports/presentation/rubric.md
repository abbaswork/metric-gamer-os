# Metric: Presentation
**Genre:** Sports
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = broadcast-quality atmosphere, 1 = generic or silent

## Why This Matters

Presentation is what separates a sports game that feels like the event from one that feels like a facsimile of it. Commentary, stadium atmosphere, crowd behaviour, broadcast overlays, and pre-match build-up all determine whether the game creates the sensation of watching and playing real sport, or simply simulates the mechanical outcomes without the context around them.

Commentary is where the community draws the sharpest distinctions — and where the failure mode is the most specific. The complaint is not that commentary exists and is imperfect. It is that commentary loops quickly enough to become actively distracting during a normal session. Undisputed, the boxing game, received direct community complaints that the commentary was "repetitive and stupid" and "very repetitive and inaccurate, especially on knock downs." In a boxing game, knock downs are among the most critical moments — commentary that describes them inaccurately or repeats the same lines across consecutive events collapses the immersion at precisely the moments that should land hardest. The same pattern appears in broader sports game communities: repeated commentary lines across a session become community in-jokes, which is a reliable signal that the repetition is noticeable enough to undermine the presentation layer entirely.

The secondary layer is stadium and crowd behaviour. A full stadium that reacts to key moments — that gets quieter when the home side goes behind, louder in the final minutes of a close match — does more for immersion than graphical fidelity. The absence of this reactivity is felt most clearly in games that simulate sports with strong live atmospheres, where the gap between what the real crowd sounds like and what the game delivers is most obvious.

This is the default 5th metric for Sports. When the sub-genre is Free, this metric is replaced by Monetisation Model — F2P sports games rarely invest in broadcast-quality production, and the defining question for those games is whether the business model is fair.

## Sources

- **Undisputed (boxing) Steam Community discussions** (live source, confirmed via web search) — commentary described as "repetitive and stupid" and "very repetitive and inaccurate, especially on knock downs"; the specificity of the complaint to knock down moments confirms the failure is most damaging in the game's highest-stakes situations
- **Harry Potter: Quidditch Champions Steam Community discussions** (live source, confirmed via web search — September 2024) — voice lines described as repetitive within normal sessions; confirms the commentary repetition complaint is not isolated to mainstream sports titles
- **r/FIFA / r/EAFC and r/NBA2k** (training data) — repeated commentary lines becoming community in-jokes is a consistent pattern; specific lines that loop quickly are cited by name in threads, confirming players track and document them; NBA 2K's broadcast presentation is consistently cited as a series strength in comparison discussions
- **r/patientgamers** (training data) — presentation quality is cited as a factor in whether a sports game "holds up" on revisit; games with strong atmosphere are described as retaining their appeal longer than mechanically similar games with weak presentation

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Broadcast-quality presentation throughout. Commentary is contextual — it references what has happened in the current match, player form, and the significance of the moment. Lines do not loop within a normal session. Stadiums react to match state with crowd behaviour that changes as the game situation changes. Pre-match and half-time presentation reflects the real broadcast format of the sport. | The game sounds and feels like the real event. Commentary will describe what is happening rather than what usually happens, and the crowd will behave differently in the 85th minute of a 1-0 game than it does in the first half of a comfortable win. |
| 4 | Strong presentation with some repetition. Commentary is mostly contextual and well-produced but lines begin looping before a long session ends. Stadiums feel alive in most situations. Broadcast structure and overlays match the sport. The repetition is noticeable but does not dominate the experience. | The atmosphere will hold up across most of your play time. You will start recognising repeated commentary lines eventually, but it will not undermine the experience. |
| 3 | Functional presentation that covers the basics. Commentary is present and relevant but generic — it could describe almost any match in the sport without feeling specific to what is happening in this one. Crowd noise is appropriate but flat. Overlays work. After a few hours you will tune the commentary out entirely. | The game sounds like a sports game. It does not sound like this match. The presentation layer does not actively hurt the experience, but it does not add to it. |
| 2 | Underdeveloped presentation. Commentary is sparse, quickly repetitive, or contextually disconnected from match events in the way Undisputed's commentary was disconnected from the knock down moments it was supposed to describe. Crowd behaviour is static. Broadcast structure is absent or perfunctory. | The game will feel quieter than the sport it is representing. The audio layer will remind you that you are looking at a screen rather than pulling you into the match. |
| 1 | No meaningful commentary or presentation elements. Matches play out in near-silence or with placeholder audio. No broadcast structure, no crowd reaction, no contextual audio. Every match sounds the same regardless of what is happening. | The game communicates nothing about the sport beyond the mechanics. |

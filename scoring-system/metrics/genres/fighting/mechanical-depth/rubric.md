# Metric: Mechanical Depth
**Genre:** Fighting
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = deep systems with a meaningful skill ceiling, 1 = no real reward for mechanical investment

## Why This Matters

The FGC places significant value on depth. Not complexity for its own sake, but the kind of depth where genuine knowledge produces genuinely better results. The community criticism directed at accessibility-first design — auto-combos, simplified inputs, reduced execution requirements — isn't about gatekeeping. It's about whether the systems in a game actually reward the time players put in. When a button-masher and a practiced player produce similar outcomes in most situations, the incentive to learn disappears.

The games that earn strong mechanical reputations have clear layers: basics that get you started, intermediate knowledge that separates competent players, and advanced techniques that look different from beginner play. Street Fighter's parry system (Street Fighter III), Guilty Gear's Roman Cancels, and the Tekken wall carry and movement system are examples the community returns to when describing what mechanical depth looks like in practice. The bar isn't execution difficulty — it's whether deeper knowledge produces better outcomes.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The system has clear layers. Basics are reachable quickly, but mastery requires genuine mechanical investment. Advanced techniques — consistent combos, option selects, defensive mechanics — produce results that beginners cannot replicate. Matches between skilled players look different from beginner matches in ways that are visible even to casual observers. | You'll find something new to work on well past your first hundred hours. The system keeps giving you reasons to go deeper. |
| 4 | Solid mechanical depth that rewards sustained investment. Most of what the system offers takes real time to uncover. A few mechanics exist that don't justify the learning cost, but the core is deep enough that skill differences are clear and consistent. | You'll keep improving and feeling it. The occasional mechanic won't seem worth it, but the system as a whole holds up. |
| 3 | Functional mechanics with a limited ceiling. Basic knowledge and advanced play produce similar results often enough that the incentive to go deeper is unclear. The game works but doesn't reward you proportionally for what you put in. | You'll plateau within a reasonable time. Getting better is possible but the game stops making it obvious when it's happening. |
| 2 | The gap between button-mashing and informed play is small. Advanced mechanics exist but the system doesn't reliably produce better outcomes for the player using them. | Opponents who know less than you will win often enough to make the investment feel arbitrary. |
| 1 | The system doesn't reward mechanical knowledge. Outcomes are driven by factors outside the game's mechanics — character choice, reaction time — rather than game-specific knowledge. | There is no reason to study this game. Time spent in training mode doesn't translate to better results in meaningful ways. |

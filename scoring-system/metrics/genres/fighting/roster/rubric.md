# Metric: Roster Size
**Genre:** Fighting
**Type:** Quantitative
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = largest and most varied roster, 1 = smallest

## Why This Matters

Roster complaints are among the most consistent launch-day criticisms in the fighting game community. Street Fighter 5 launched with 16 characters and was immediately called out for having the worst launch roster in the franchise's recent history. Mortal Kombat 1 launched with 22 characters and was described by the community as a "paltry selection." Street Fighter 6 and Tekken 8 faced similar criticism for trimmed base rosters padded with paid DLC. The size expectation is anchored to what previous entries delivered — players arrive knowing what the franchise has offered before, and a smaller roster reads as a step back regardless of how polished the individual characters are.

Roster size matters because it determines archetype coverage. A roster with 40 characters has room for grapplers, zoners, rushdown characters, stance-switchers, and charge characters. A roster of 15 may cover the basics but leaves large gaps in what's actually playable.

## Rubric

| Score | Threshold | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | 40+ characters | The roster has enough variety that you'll find your archetype without compromising. Grapplers, zoners, rushdown characters, and technical charge characters all have real options. |
| 4 | 30–39 characters | A solid selection that covers the main playstyles. You'll find something that fits how you want to play, though a few niches feel underserved. |
| 3 | 20–29 characters | The roster works but you'll feel the gaps. A few archetypes are missing entirely and the selection within any one style is thin. |
| 2 | 12–19 characters | You'll cycle through the full roster quickly. Meaningful choice within your preferred style is limited from the start. |
| 1 | Under 12 characters | The roster is too small to cover the basics. The game defines what you play rather than the other way around. |

# Metric: Character Identity
**Genre:** Fighting
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = every character feels genuinely distinct, 1 = characters feel interchangeable

## Why This Matters

Roster size and character identity are different problems. A large roster where every character shares the same toolkit is a different game than a smaller roster where each character demands a different approach. The fighting game community consistently draws this distinction — characters should feel like they belong to different disciplines, not different skins. The specific complaint that surfaces in FGC discussions is homogenization: all-rounder characters designed to appeal broadly, where no one is exceptional at anything and no character requires a fundamentally different strategy to play or fight against.

Guilty Gear Strive is the community reference point for strong character identity — every character has at least one property or mechanic that doesn't appear on anyone else in the roster. Conversely, games that reduce unique tech and push auto-combos receive criticism for making characters feel interchangeable at entry level and limiting the ceiling of differentiation at high level.

## Sources

- **Dan Fornace's 10 Tips for Making a Fighting Game (Medium)** — "rather than pursuing perfect balance, give characters their own identities and playstyles instead of making them homogenized with similar tools and properties" (live source, confirmed via web search)

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Each character has at least one mechanic, property, or system interaction that doesn't appear on anyone else in the roster. Archetypes are clearly differentiated — a grappler, a zoner, and a rushdown character require genuinely different habits to play and to fight against. | Picking a new character means learning a new game within the game. What you know from your main doesn't fully transfer, and neither does your opponent's read on you. |
| 4 | Most characters have distinct identities with a few exceptions. The main archetypes are represented and the gaps between them are real enough to matter in matches. | You'll feel real differences between most of the roster. A few characters borrow too heavily from each other, but the overall variety holds up. |
| 3 | Characters vary in moveset but share enough mechanical ground that habits transfer easily from one to the next. The roster covers the basic archetypes but the distinctions between them are narrow. | You'll find your playstyle translates to most of the cast. The variety exists on paper more than it does in how matches actually play out. |
| 2 | Most characters play similarly regardless of type. Mechanical differences exist but rarely change how a match unfolds at any level of play. | Switching characters doesn't ask much of you. Your opponent won't need to change their read on you based on who you've picked. |
| 1 | The roster feels like reskins of the same character. Characters share so much mechanical DNA that your approach doesn't change regardless of who either player selects. | There is no reason to learn the matchup. Every character plays close enough to the same that game knowledge doesn't extend to character knowledge. |

# Metric: AI Quality
**Genre:** Fighting (Single Player)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = AI problems absent, 1 = AI is the main obstacle

## Why This Matters

Fighting game AI earns its criticism differently from other genres. The core complaint isn't that the CPU is too hard — it's that the CPU cheats. "Laser spam, hyper armor on so many moves, 1 hit kills, unseeable/non-reactable moves, perfect reads" was how the NeoGAF community described Tekken 7's final boss. The specific failure pattern: AI that doesn't follow the game's own rules. A normal player can't react to a move in zero frames, can't spam projectiles indefinitely, and doesn't get hyper armor on every action. When the CPU does these things, the only winning strategy is to find a cheap counter-tactic rather than apply actual game knowledge. That's the community's line between difficulty and unfairness — difficulty tests your knowledge of the game, unfairness tests your ability to exploit the AI's scripting.

Good AI doesn't need to be unbeatable. It needs to follow the same rules you do and make intelligent decisions within them.

## Sources

- **NeoGAF: "Tekken 7 is the worst single player experience I've ever had in a fighting game"** — direct player quotes: "laser spam, hyper armor on so many moves, 1 hit kills, unseeable/non-reactable moves, perfect reads"; "a game forcing you to play in an unnatural way — the only way to win is to find some cheap tactic" (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **NeoGAF thread comments** — players note the removal of Ghost Battle, Survival, and Team Battle from Tekken 7 compared to prior entries; "Arcade mode is like only 4 fights" (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **r/Fighters and r/MortalKombat** (training data) — "cheap AI" and "CPU reads" are recurring complaints in single player discussion threads; player complaints about AI that doesn't play by the rules are consistent across franchises

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | The CPU follows the same rules as a player. It uses mix-ups, respects spacing, makes occasional mistakes at lower difficulties, and scales logically. Difficulty settings produce genuinely different opponents without the CPU resorting to properties or behaviors outside normal play. | Fighting the CPU teaches you the game. The habits you build against it carry into real matches. |
| 4 | Solid AI that plays within the game's rules at most difficulty levels. Some top-end difficulty settings introduce slightly elevated reaction times or tighter reads, but the AI stops short of unresponsive or impossible mechanics. | The difficulty curve is real. You'll lose to the CPU for reasons that make sense. |
| 3 | Functional AI that becomes inconsistent at higher difficulty levels. Lower settings play fair; upper settings introduce CPU-exclusive behaviors that require workarounds rather than skill. | The mid-range difficulties are fine. Push into harder settings and the AI starts doing things no human opponent would. |
| 2 | CPU-exclusive behaviors are present at most difficulty settings. The AI reads inputs faster than is humanly possible, spams mechanics outside normal frequency, or uses hyper armor in ways that aren't available to players. Winning requires finding the CPU's scripting gaps rather than playing the game well. | You'll find the cheap tactic that works and lean on it. Applying actual game knowledge stops being reliable. |
| 1 | The CPU doesn't play the game. Boss fights and high-difficulty settings ignore the game's fundamental rules entirely. Input reading is instant, impossible moves are used freely, and losing feels arbitrary rather than earned. | You're not fighting a version of the game you're supposed to be learning. |

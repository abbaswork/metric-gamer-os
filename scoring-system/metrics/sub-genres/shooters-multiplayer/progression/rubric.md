# Metric: Progression System
**Genre:** Shooters (Multiplayer)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Delight — 5 = progression reflects skill and feels earned, 1 = progression is designed around spending or obscures player improvement

## Why This Matters

Progression systems in multiplayer shooters are one of the most debated community topics because they sit at the intersection of business model and gameplay. The core community expectation is clear: gameplay-relevant unlocks should be earned through play. Cosmetics as a paid tier is accepted. Competitive advantage behind a paywall is not.

The second dimension is whether the system reflects actual improvement. SBMM (Skill-Based Matchmaking) is the flashpoint here. A former Call of Duty producer described the problem directly: SBMM forces kill/death and win/loss ratios toward 1.0, making it impossible for players to see improvement in their own stats. The community complaint is not that skill-based matching exists. It's that when combined with SBMM, no progression metric reflects whether you're actually getting better.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Weapon unlocks and gameplay-relevant attachments are accessible through normal play within a reasonable timeframe. Paid content is cosmetic only. Ranked structures reflect skill progression and give players a way to measure improvement. | You can see yourself getting better. Unlocks arrive at a pace that reflects how much you've played. |
| 4 | Most meaningful unlocks are accessible through play. A few items are time-gated longer than feels proportional. Nothing behind a paywall changes competitive outcomes. | The system works with you most of the time. The occasional grind wall is short. |
| 3 | Progression is functional without reinforcing a clear sense of improvement. Unlocks arrive at a steady pace that doesn't feel proportional to skill development. The paid tier offers accelerators that create a visible gap between spending and non-spending players. | You'll unlock things at a steady rate. Whether it reflects how well you're playing is harder to say. |
| 2 | The line between cosmetic and gameplay advantage is blurred. Paying accelerates access to attachments or loadout options that affect competitive outcomes. SBMM suppresses visible improvement enough that the progression system stops working as feedback. | Whether you're improving is hard to tell. Spending changes what's available in ways that matter in matches. |
| 1 | Progression is structured around spending. Gameplay-relevant unlocks sit behind real money or require a grind so steep that paying is the practical path. The ranked or stat system is disconnected from actual skill. | Progress doesn't feel like yours. |

# Metric: Netcode / Server Quality
**Genre:** Shooters (Multiplayer)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = server quality problems absent, 1 = servers define outcomes more than player skill

## Why This Matters

Hit registration is the shooter community's most direct measure of server quality. "It's all about the hitboxes and netcode" is how the NeoGAF community put it, and the ongoing CS2 debates around hit registration anomalies confirm this is an active conversation. The specific complaint pattern is familiar: a shot that visually connects doesn't register, or an enemy takes damage behind cover after the player has already moved. When this is a regular experience, trust in the game collapses. Players stop attributing losses to their own skill and start attributing them to the connection.

Server tick rate is the technical dimension the community tracks most closely. 64-tick was the long-standing standard; 128-tick is preferred for competitive play because high-speed interactions — peeking, close-range duels — are resolved more accurately. Anti-cheat is the other axis. CS2's Overwatch-replacement system and Valorant's Vanguard are the community reference points for how seriously developers take cheating at the infrastructure level.

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Hit registration confirms what happens on screen. Close-range and high-movement encounters resolve accurately. Anti-cheat is active and functional. Server regions cover the active player base with workable ping. | Losses feel like your mistake. The connection is not a variable you have to manage. |
| 4 | Reliable servers with occasional hit registration anomalies that don't define the experience. Specific scenarios produce inconsistencies sometimes. Anti-cheat handles the majority of cases. | You'll notice the odd shot that didn't land. It doesn't happen often enough to change how you play. |
| 3 | Generally functional servers with documented issues in specific scenarios. Hit registration holds up in most situations. Cheating is present at higher ranks without being overwhelming. Server regions have gaps that leave some of the player base with a ping disadvantage. | You'll adapt to the game's quirks. The connection affects you often enough to be worth knowing about. |
| 2 | Hit registration problems are a regular community discussion. Specific situations produce outcomes that don't match what happened on screen with enough frequency that the community tracks them. Cheating is visible enough to change how you approach competitive play. | The servers are a factor in every session. You'll start second-guessing clean shots. |
| 1 | The servers are the main obstacle to competitive play. Hit registration is unreliable enough that skill becomes secondary to connection quality. Cheating is widespread enough to define the experience at high level. | You're not playing the game at this point. You're playing the lag. |

# Metric: Monetisation Model
**Sub-genre:** Free (applies across all genres)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = monetisation problems absent, 1 = the business model defines the experience

## Why This Matters

Free-to-play games are evaluated differently from paid games because the business model shapes the entire design. The community distinction is specific: cosmetics behind a paywall are accepted. Gameplay content behind a paywall is not. When cars, characters, weapons, or tracks that provide competitive advantage are locked to paying players, the free label becomes a funnel rather than a genuine offering.

The failure patterns the community documents are consistent across genres. Energy systems that cap how long you can play for free. Car or character rosters where the strongest options require real money. Battle passes where skipping the paid tier means missing content that affects how you compete. Warframe is the most commonly cited example of a fair free-to-play model — all gameplay content is earnable through play, even if it takes time. Real Racing 3 and the Asphalt series are the most cited examples of the opposite: games that are technically free but that design every friction point around extracting a transaction.

## Sources

- **r/patientgamers and r/gaming** (training data) — "predatory monetisation" is a well-documented community term; free-to-play fatigue discussions cite pay-to-win car access, energy systems, and paywalled roster slots as the specific failure modes
- **r/Warframe** (training data) — Warframe is regularly cited as the community benchmark for fair free-to-play: all gameplay content earnable through play, with microtransactions limited to cosmetics and time savings
- **r/MobileGaming** (training data) — Real Racing 3, CSR Racing, and the Asphalt series are documented as canonical examples of racing games designed around extraction; energy systems and locked car tiers are the specific complaints
- **Steam reviews across free-to-play titles** (training data) — "pay-to-win" and "pay-to-progress" are among the most common negative tags in free-to-play game reviews across all genres; "completely free, no pressure" is among the most common positive signals

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | All gameplay content is accessible through play. Cars, characters, tracks, and maps that affect competitive outcomes are earnable without spending. Paid content is cosmetic only. No energy or timer systems limit how long you can play. | The free experience is the full game. Spending changes how you look, not how you compete. |
| 4 | Almost all gameplay content is accessible for free. Some items require more time than paying players would spend, but no content behind a paywall creates a meaningful competitive gap. The paid tier is cosmetic with minor convenience additions. | You'll get the full experience with more time investment. Paying speeds things up without changing what you're competing with. |
| 3 | Some gameplay content is locked or heavily time-gated for free players. Cars, characters, or maps with competitive relevance take significantly longer to access without spending. The core game is playable for free. The gap between free and paying is noticeable. | You can enjoy the game for free. You'll feel the ceiling before long. |
| 2 | Gameplay content with competitive relevance is behind a paywall or a grind so steep that it's designed to convert. Energy systems or daily caps limit how long you can play in a session. The free experience is an extended demo. | Spending is the intended path. Playing for free means working around the design. |
| 1 | The free label is a funnel. Every meaningful decision point has a transaction attached. Energy systems, paywalled competitive content, and gacha mechanics combine into a model designed around extraction. | The game is not free in any meaningful sense. |

# Metric: Competitive Balance
**Sub-genre:** Fighting (Multiplayer / Competitive)
**Type:** Qualitative (community consensus)
**Direction:** Complaint — 5 = meta supports broad character variety, 1 = one or two characters define everything

## Why This Matters

The FGC has always had tier lists and has never expected perfect balance. Tier lists are part of the culture. The community complaint isn't that top-tier characters win more — it's when a top tier is so dominant that it narrows the competitive field to the point where off-meta picks become a handicap no amount of skill can fully overcome. The opposite problem is equally documented: characters so weak that playing them at high level requires winning despite the game rather than because of it.

The healthy balance the community describes isn't equal win rates. It's a meta where different archetypes appear at high level, where lower-tier mains still achieve tournament results, and where character knowledge matters more than character choice in the majority of matchups. Guilty Gear Strive is frequently cited as a game that maintained this standard; Street Fighter 5 later in its life was criticized for a top tier that was too consistently dominant.

## Sources

- **Dan Fornace's 10 Tips for Making a Fighting Game (Medium)** — "when characters are sufficiently different, the game appears balanced while accentuating player preferences and creating more varied tournament results" — notes that distinct identity is the path to perceived balance (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **eventhubs.com: "Are 'essential' characters becoming a problem for fighting game rosters?"** — documents the roster composition debate including competitive viability expectations (live source, confirmed via web search)
- **r/Kappa and r/Fighters** (training data) — tier list discussions are consistent and document community tolerance for imbalance; the line between accepted variance and broken meta is drawn around tournament representation diversity
- **Smash Bros competitive community** (training data) — the Brawl Meta Knight era is the canonical example of a broken meta where one character was too dominant; used as a reference point in balance discussions across fighting games

## Rubric

| Score | Description | What This Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | A wide range of characters appear in high-level play and tournament results. Top-tier characters win more, but off-meta picks still achieve results through execution and knowledge. No single character or small group dominates pool composition at competitive level. | Your character choice reflects what you enjoy playing, not what you have to play to compete. You'll find opponents playing all kinds of styles at every level. |
| 4 | A clear top tier exists but doesn't dominate consistently enough to narrow the competitive field. Off-meta picks win matches regularly at high level. The tier spread gives lower-ranked characters a real path through knowledge and execution. | You can go off-meta without feeling like you're fighting uphill from the start. Results are still possible. |
| 3 | A top tier that noticeably shapes tournament pools but doesn't make lower-tier picks unwinnable. Most of the roster appears in competitive play at some level, but the gap between the top and the rest is wider than the community wants. | You'll feel the matchup spread if you play off-meta. Winning is possible but you're working harder than your opponent for the same result. |
| 2 | One or two characters appear in a large majority of competitive play. Playing off-meta is a real disadvantage in important matches that game knowledge alone can't bridge. The meta is narrow enough that matchup unfamiliarity becomes a consistent advantage for top-tier players. | If you're not playing the top tier, you're giving something up every time you sit down to compete seriously. |
| 1 | The meta is broken. One character or a small group dominates competitive play to the point where the game's identity is defined by them. Tournament results are predictable based on character selection alone. | Character choice matters more than game knowledge. The meta isn't a metagame — it's a problem. |

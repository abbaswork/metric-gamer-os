## Introduction

Apex Legends is the battle royale that most players in the FPS community still bring up when movement skill and squad play come up in the same conversation. Released in February 2019 with no prior marketing across PC, PS4, and Xbox One, it surpassed 25 million players in its first week and 50 million within its first month. It's free to play, built around teams of three, a roster of Legends with distinct class abilities, and a movement system derived from Titanfall 2. Seven years and nearly thirty seasons on, it's one of the most-played shooters on the planet with over 100 million registered players.

Whether it still earns that for a player coming to it now is a different question. The game has changed considerably across those seasons, and the reputation doesn't always match what's waiting in the lobby. The metric breakdown below covers what Apex does well and where the experience has real gaps — across gunplay, movement, squad design, maps, game modes, community health, and monetisation — so you can judge whether the version that exists in 2026 is worth your time.

## Metrics

### Gunplay — 4 / 5

The gunplay in Apex is built from Titanfall 2's DNA and the individual weapon feel shows it. For example, the R-301 and the Flatline are both ARs that cover similar ranges, but the R-301 has a predictable, low-recoil pattern that rewards sustained fire while the Flatline hits harder and asks more of you in managing the kick. Building a loadout around one means making a real decision about engagement range and playstyle, not just picking whichever has higher base damage. Hit feedback is immediate and the audio confirms contact clearly so you're not checking the kill feed to know if your shots are landing. Unfortunately, rotational aim assist on controller is strong enough in certain weapon matchups that it gives controller players a measurable edge in close-range exchanges, and it's been an unresolved issue across multiple seasons. If you're on PC with mouse and keyboard in crossplay lobbies, it's worth knowing.

---

### Movement — 4 / 5

Movement is what Apex does better than any other battle royale. Slide jumps, superglides, and tap strafes chain together with momentum carrying through so getting somewhere quickly becomes a skill in itself, not just time spent between fights. Legends like Octane and Pathfinder extend that further with ability-based repositioning on top of the base movement kit, so your traversal options shift depending on who you're running. Unfortunately, wall-running isn't in the base kit the way it is in Titanfall 2, and that's the specific ceiling this metric is held to. If you're coming from Titanfall you'll feel the difference. If this is your first Respawn game you won't, and the movement will still be the best you've played in a battle royale.

---

### Squad Play & Class Design — 5 / 5

Legends are the reason Apex doesn't play like a standard battle royale, and a well-composed squad is proof of that within the first fight. There's no hard class lock, but a squad with Bloodhound scanning before an entry and Caustic locking the building they're pushing into is playing a different game from two players running aggressive loadouts without a plan. The ability synergies are mechanical: Ash's tether holds enemies in position while Bangalore's smoke cuts sightlines, and Caustic's gas punishes anyone trying to move through it so the combination creates a situation that solo play simply can't replicate. In ranked, the gap between a squad that built around its Legends and one that didn't shows up before the first fight is over.

---

### Maps — 4 / 5

The map rotation is uneven, and that matters in Apex because you can't choose which map is active in a given session. King's Canyon and World's Edge give you varied terrain and enough landing zone diversity that map knowledge takes real time to build, with positioning decisions that carry weight on both. Unfortunately, Storm Point is the problem in the rotation. It's effectively a walking simulator, with matches frequently involving long cross-map rotations between spread-out POIs and stretches where you're covering ground without a fight in sight. If Storm Point is in the rotation when you're playing, landing close and playing the ring is a better approach than trying to use the whole map.

---

### Game Modes — 2 / 5

Apex is a Battle Royale, and the rest of what sits around that hasn't found permanent footing. Arenas, the 3v3 mode that gave players a genuine alternative to the 60-player lobby, was removed in Season 16 after its player base thinned out and replaced by Mixtape, a rotating playlist of Team Deathmatch, Control, and Gun Run. Those modes work as a break from Battle Royale but none have the same identity as the core game, and rotating between them means you don't always get the one you're in the mood for. Unfortunately, there's no offline option at all. Even the firing range requires a live connection so if you want to practice mechanics outside a live lobby, you're limited to what the firing range supports inside an active session.

---

### Community — 2 / 5

The cheating problem in Apex is real and it hasn't been resolved. A player who logged 530 ranked games in one competitive split estimates 20 to 30 percent of opponents were using something, from Cronus Zen recoil removal to active wallhacks. EA Forum threads from 2026 describe it as the game's downfall, and Respawn's legal action against cheat providers hasn't translated into anything players can feel in their lobbies. Population isn't the issue. Season 28 pulled 300,000 concurrent Steam players at launch so getting into a match is quick. Whether those matches feel clean is the separate question, and in ranked it matters more than in casual play.

---

### Monetisation Model — 3 / 5

Apex is free to play and the competitive experience is free in the part that matters most. No weapon is locked behind the battle pass, and new guns join the loot pool for every player when they're added to the game. Legends are earnable through play via Legend Tokens. Unfortunately, the recent monetisation history is messier than the underlying model suggests. In 2024 Respawn attempted to remove the option to buy the battle pass with Apex Coins, a route that let players use earned in-game currency, and reversed the decision after community backlash. Season 24's Legend Spotlight Pass charged around $10 for a 20-tier track ending in a randomised Legendary skin, which landed poorly. New Legends with distinct ability kits take real time to unlock for free players so if you're joining after several seasons of new characters, the catching-up gap is worth knowing about before you commit.

## Pros

- The ping system is the best non-verbal communication tool in any shooter; full squad coordination is possible without voice chat
- Crossplay across all platforms means squad size isn't limited by who owns what hardware
- Seasonal content updates shift the meta and map rotation without requiring a purchase
- The lore and Legend writing are a genuine strength with a community that engages with them seriously

## Cons

- New players are entering against several years of accumulated movement tech expertise and the matchmaking doesn't always protect against that gap early on
- The Heirloom system sits at a roughly 3% pack drop rate, putting a guaranteed cosmetic at over $170 in packs; it's optional but its presence shapes how the rest of the store is perceived
- The ranked system has persistent issues with split resets and the skill gap between casual and competitive lobbies
- Nintendo Switch support ends August 4, 2026

## Who Is This Game For?

**Play this if** you want the most movement-expressive battle royale available and are prepared to invest the time learning the mechanics, or if you play with a regular squad and want a game where composition and coordination produce a measurable result.

**Skip this if** you want meaningful mode variety beyond Battle Royale, if cheating in competitive lobbies is a dealbreaker for you, or if you're on the original Nintendo Switch with no plans to upgrade.

---

## Verdict

Apex Legends is one of the best movement shooters you can play, and the closest any battle royale has come to making squad coordination feel like a genuine mechanical skill. The gunplay, the Legends system, and the best maps in the rotation all hold up. Game Modes is thin, cheating in ranked is a real and unresolved problem, and Respawn's monetisation experiments have tested the goodwill the core experience earns — but if you're going in with a regular squad and realistic expectations of what free-to-play looks like in 2026, it's worth your time.

## FAQs

**Is Apex Legends cross platform?**
Yes, Apex Legends supports crossplay across PC, PlayStation, Xbox, and Nintendo Switch 2, so you can squad up with friends regardless of platform. Crossplay is enabled by default but you can restrict your lobby to a single platform if you prefer.

**What platforms is Apex Legends available on?**
Apex Legends is available on PC via Steam, the EA App, and the Epic Games Store, plus PS4, PS5, Xbox One, Xbox Series X|S, and Nintendo Switch 2. The original Nintendo Switch version is being discontinued on August 4, 2026, and the mobile version was shut down in May 2023.

**How long is Apex Legends?**
There's no campaign so there's no set completion time. Individual Battle Royale matches run between 15 and 25 minutes on average depending on how quickly your squad engages or gets eliminated. How long you play overall depends entirely on what you're working toward.

**Is Apex Legends hard?**
Yes, the skill ceiling is high. The movement mechanics alone take hours to learn at a basic level and considerably longer to chain together effectively, and the Legend abilities add another layer on top of the shooting. The starting experience is accessible, but the gap between a new player and a veteran is significant and the matchmaking doesn't always shield you from it early on.

**Can you play Apex Legends with friends online?**
Yes, you can squad up with up to two friends in standard Battle Royale or queue in duos with one. Crossplay means it doesn't matter which platform you and your friends are on.

**Can you play Apex Legends solo?**
You can queue into standard Battle Royale on your own and be matched with random teammates, but there's no permanent solo mode. Solos has appeared as a limited-time mode in the past and its availability depends on the current season's playlist.

**Does Apex Legends have DLC?**
No traditional paid DLC. The battle pass releases each season with cosmetic rewards, and cosmetic items are available through the in-game store. No content that affects competitive play is locked behind a purchase.

**Is Apex Legends on Game Pass?**
Apex Legends is available through EA Play, which is included with Xbox Game Pass Ultimate and PC Game Pass. Subscription catalogues change, so it's worth checking current availability before counting on it as your route in.

**Is Apex Legends free?**
Yes, Apex Legends is free to download and play on all platforms. All weapons and core game content are available without spending. Cosmetic items — Legend skins, weapon wraps, and Heirlooms — are where real-money spending applies.

**Is Apex Legends pay to win?**
No. All weapons are in the standard loot pool and available to every player. Legends are earnable through play via Legend Tokens, though unlocking newer characters without spending takes time. Nothing in the battle pass or store provides a direct competitive advantage over another player.

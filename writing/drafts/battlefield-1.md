## Introduction

Battlefield 1 is the WW1 entry most players in the series still cite as the moment the franchise got it right again after a string of divisive releases. Released in October 2016 across PC, PS4, and Xbox One simultaneously, it's the fifteenth installment in the franchise and the one that stepped away from the contemporary military settings the series had occupied for years. The campaign runs as War Stories, six self-contained vignettes set across different theaters of the First World War, each following a different protagonist and a different style of warfare, from a British tank crew at the Battle of Cambrai to an ANZAC runner at Gallipoli. The multiplayer supports up to 64 players across modes built around territory control and pushing battle lines.

Whether it still delivers depends on what you're after. The campaign is a standalone question since each War Story plays the same regardless of who else is online. The multiplayer is harder to read in 2026, with the player base having contracted since launch and only certain modes filling reliably at this point. The metric breakdown below covers gunplay, movement, campaign structure, maps, game modes, squad design, and community health so you have a clear picture of both sides before you install.

## Metrics

### Gunplay — 4 / 5

The gunplay in Battlefield 1 is built around weapons that feel different from each other because the WW1 setting makes it unavoidable. A bolt-action like the SMLE MKIII puts everything on a single well-placed shot and asks a different skill from you under pressure than the Selbstlader 1906, which fires fast enough as a Medic's weapon that you're actively tracking a moving target between shots. A Support player lying prone with a bipod-mounted MG 15 n.A. is suppressing a lane in a way the Assault's MP 18 can't replicate, so the choice between weapon classes is a genuine tactical decision. Hit audio and reload animation carry the slower weapons, making each chamber cycle feel deliberate. Unfortunately, the Medic's semi-automatic rifles ended up covering enough ranges well enough that they became the default for players who wanted flexibility, and the deeper weapon variety the arsenal offers got underexplored in competitive lobbies as a result. The campaign pushes you toward whatever the theater demands, which is where the full spread of what each weapon type does becomes clearest.

---

### Movement — 4 / 5

Movement in Battlefield 1 is built around two different skill sets, and the vehicle layer is the one that rewards time investment. On foot, the core mechanics work cleanly but don't develop into anything beyond getting from cover to cover quickly, and it's the vehicles where the skill ceiling actually sits. A cavalry horse can jump debris and lean into a melee attack mid-gallop, opening a flanking option that no other mode of movement provides. A two-seater plane requires pilot and gunner to coordinate their approach angle together, so flying one well is a shared skill split across both crew members. Tanks cover different ground by class: a light tank threads through infantry territory quickly, while a heavy Landship needs clear lanes and the commitment to hold them. Getting in or out of any vehicle during a fight is clean enough that it doesn't interrupt the play around it. Unfortunately, the heavier vehicles are slow enough to reposition that committing to a lane early is a decision you'll feel the cost of if you read the map wrong.

---

### Campaign Structure — 4 / 5

The War Stories format is the most ambitious thing Battlefield 1's campaign does, and it mostly earns it. Each story follows a different soldier through a different theater, and the tonal range is real. Through Mud and Blood is a tank crew story at the Battle of Cambrai, where the tank itself is both the set piece and the obstacle, with progress coming through managing a multi-position vehicle under fire. Nothing is Written opens the Arabian Desert as an unusually open landscape, where the approach to objectives shifts from mission to mission. Unfortunately, the anthology format means there's no narrative thread across the campaign as a whole, and within some individual stories the missions repeat the same beat before reaching their payoff. The individual stories hold up on their own terms, and at around six hours the campaign is short enough that the uneven pacing between vignettes doesn't drag.

---

### Maps — 4 / 5

Battlefield 1's map pool is built around the idea that the terrain of the First World War is worth playing on in its own right, and most of the time it delivers. Sinai Desert spreads objectives across sand dunes and desert ridges, with vehicle lanes wide enough for cavalry charges and flanking routes that infantry can use to cut off armor without fighting it directly. Argonne Forest removes vehicles almost entirely and channels both teams through dense woodland, so the positioning skills that pay off on Sinai don't carry over and you're reading a completely different map. The rotation covers enough different theaters that map knowledge builds separately for each one, and experienced players feel the difference clearly. Unfortunately, Suez is the weak point in the rotation and has been since launch: too small for Conquest, with only three capture points clustered close enough that both teams spend most of the match funneling toward each other with almost no cover, and the side that spawns on the left wins often enough that it feels structural. It's one map in an otherwise strong pool, but its design problems are obvious enough that you'll notice them the first time you land in it.

---

### Game Modes — 3 / 5

Battlefield 1's mode list has two genuinely distinct experiences and both are worth playing on their own terms. Conquest is the series standard: 64 players across a full map, capturing and holding flags, with the match determined by who controls territory longest. Operations chains multiple maps under a shared ticket pool, so a team that loses ground on one map continues fighting on the next. The Behemoth reinforcements given to a struggling attacker mean the match rarely ends in a rout. Rush adds a third identity, channeling both teams down a linear attack/defend structure where the front line shifts as M-COM stations fall. Unfortunately, the rest of the mode list doesn't add much beyond those three. Team Deathmatch and the smaller modes are variations on the same core without the scale or structure that makes the main modes work, and there's no offline option for any of it; the campaign is the only part of the game that doesn't need a live server.

---

### Squad Play & Class Design — 4 / 5

Battlefield 1's class system gives each of the four roles a job the others can't do, and the difference is felt most in Operations where individual mistakes carry more weight. A Medic who stays in position and revives downed teammates keeps a squad in a fight that a group without one would have lost several tickets earlier. A Support player dropping an ammo pouch near the squad after an engagement removes the decision of pushing or falling back to resupply. Scout's spotting flares put enemy positions on the minimap for the whole squad, changing how the next push gets planned. The Assault's anti-vehicle gadgets are the only meaningful infantry answer to an enemy tank in a lane, so a squad without one has to work around the tank instead of removing it. Unfortunately, the class synergies are resource-based and produce measurable advantages without fundamentally changing how the match plays. A well-composed squad plays better, but it's playing a more efficient version of the same game, not a different one. In public matchmaking, players frequently ignore their class role, and the system only pays out fully when your squad is coordinating.

---

### Community — 3 / 5

Battlefield 1's community is in reasonable health for a nine-year-old shooter, and the active player base doesn't have notable toxicity or cheating issues at this population level. Steam averages around 6,400 concurrent players in mid-2026, peaking closer to 14,000 around update windows, with the console populations on PS4 and Xbox One adding to that. Conquest fills reliably at peak hours, and Operations has enough players to run full campaign sequences without hitting empty lobbies. Unfortunately, the mode list thins out significantly beyond those two. Most smaller modes are functionally empty outside specific community event windows, and DLC expansion modes are split from the base game pool, so Premium Pass content is only reliably playable when enough Premium Pass holders are in the lobby to fill it. If Conquest and Operations at peak hours are what you're after, the population holds up, but most of the mode list won't be populated if you go looking for it.

## Pros

- The Revolution Edition bundles the base game with all four DLC expansions, which is the better value if you're buying now and avoids the matchmaking fragmentation that separates base game and Premium Pass players
- Available through EA Play, included with Xbox Game Pass Ultimate and PC Game Pass
- The War Stories campaign is fully offline and replayable at any time with no server connection needed

## Cons

- No crossplay between PC, PS4, and Xbox One; each platform has its own matchmaking pool, which spreads an already reduced player base three ways
- Launching from Steam on PC still requires the EA App, adding a separate login step and install on top of the Steam client
- DLC expansion maps and modes require the Premium Pass or Revolution Edition; base game buyers and DLC owners end up in separate matchmaking pools for Operations content

## Who Is This Game For?

**Play this if** you want 64-player WW1 warfare with a vehicle sandbox at its center, from tank crews to aerial dogfights, and you're going in at peak hours where Conquest and Operations fill; or if the War Stories campaign draws you in and you want to explore each theater of the war at your own pace.

**Skip this if** you're planning to spend most of your time in anything other than Conquest or Operations, since most of the mode list won't have populated servers in 2026; or if offline bot play is how you usually learn a game before going online, as that option doesn't exist here.

---

## Verdict

Battlefield 1 is one of the strongest entries in the franchise, and the WW1 setting gives it an atmosphere the series hasn't matched before or since. Conquest and Operations still deliver what large-scale multiplayer should feel like when the lobbies fill, and the War Stories campaign holds up at any point. The catch is that the mode list only works for those two modes in 2026, with niche modes sitting empty and DLC operations depending on whether your team has the Premium Pass.

## FAQs

**Is Battlefield 1 cross platform?**
No. PC, PS4, and Xbox One players are in separate matchmaking pools with no crossplay between them. You can only play with friends on the same platform.

**What platforms is Battlefield 1 available on?**
Battlefield 1 is available on PC via the EA App and Steam, PS4, and Xbox One. It runs via backward compatibility on PS5 and Xbox Series X|S, but there's no native version for those platforms; you're running the last-gen build with no frame rate or resolution upgrade.

**How long is Battlefield 1?**
The War Stories campaign runs around six hours for a direct playthrough and closer to thirteen for full completion, including collectibles and optional objectives. The multiplayer has no fixed endpoint.

**Is Battlefield 1 hard?**
The campaign offers adjustable difficulty settings, so it's accessible at lower settings and more demanding at higher ones. The multiplayer has a real skill curve, with vehicle operation in particular taking time to learn well, and coordinated squads consistently outperforming players who don't use their class roles.

**Does Battlefield 1 have multiplayer?**
Yes. Online multiplayer supports up to 64 players across Conquest, Operations, Rush, Team Deathmatch, and several other modes. Conquest and Operations are the most reliably populated in 2026.

**Do I need to play previous Battlefield games first?**
No. Battlefield 1 is a standalone experience with its own setting and characters. No prior knowledge of the series is needed.

**Does Battlefield 1 have DLC?**
Yes. Four paid expansions were released: They Shall Not Pass, In the Name of the Tsar, Turning Tides, and Apocalypse. If you're buying now, the Revolution Edition includes all four alongside the base game, which is the better value and avoids the matchmaking split between base and Premium Pass players.

**Is Battlefield 1 on Game Pass or EA Play?**
Yes. Battlefield 1 is available through EA Play, which is included with Xbox Game Pass Ultimate and PC Game Pass. Subscription catalogues change, so verify current availability before counting on it.

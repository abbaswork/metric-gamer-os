## Introduction

Burnout Revenge is the fourth mainline entry in the Burnout series, and the one longtime fans still argue about relative to Burnout 3: Takedown. It launched in 2005 for PS2 and Xbox, followed by an Xbox 360 version in 2006, and shipped with 79 vehicles racing across 8 locations and 13 track layouts. A full run through its World Tour career and the Perfect Revenge Rating chase pushes past 30 hours for anyone going for full completion.

Whether that scale still adds up to a good arcade racer today is a different question, especially given how much has changed since 2005. This page breaks down the car roster, the driving and crash feel, the customization options, the career structure, and what multiplayer actually looks like today on original hardware or through backward compatibility, so you know exactly what you're picking up before you commit.

---

## Metrics

### Car Roster — 3/5

Burnout Revenge's roster looks bigger on the box than it plays. The game ships with 79 vehicles, climbing to 89 once you count the free Xbox 360 downloadable cars, built from 16 standard models spread across 8 location-themed series, each pairing a muscle car with a racer. Those same 16 designs reappear as faster DX and GT reskins as you climb the ranks, and promotional tie-ins like the Best Buy and Dolby versions of the Etnies Racer are identical under the hood with only the paint job changed. Unfortunately, that means the real design variety tops out at around 16 to 18 distinct cars, well short of what the headline count of 79 suggests. If you're the type who wants a different car for every mood, you'll cycle through what's here quickly.

---

### Driving Feel & Mastery — 4/5

Burnout Revenge nails the moment-to-moment feel that makes an arcade racer worth playing. Traffic checking lets you ram same-way vehicles for a burst of speed and, if you connect with a rival, a Traffic Takedown, while Vertical Takedowns, landing on a rival's roof after launching off a jump, add another way to end a race early that goes beyond a straightforward ram. Crash Mode got a real rework too. You time a boost at the start, then chain Aftertouch and Crashbreakers through the wreckage yourself to keep the destruction going. For example, steering a wrecked car mid-crash into a second pile of traffic to extend your Crash Dollar total turns a single explosion into a small chain of decisions. Unfortunately, the AI itself doesn't put up much resistance, and there's no difficulty slider to compensate, so the real challenge comes from chasing Perfect Revenge Ratings since the field itself won't push back much. If you're expecting the traffic-weaving tension arcade racers usually build their skill ceiling around, checking your way through traffic turns that rhythm into something more aggressive and less precise.

---

### Car Customization — 1/5

Car customization doesn't exist in Burnout Revenge in any real sense. There's no performance tuning and no paint, livery, or body kit editor of any kind anywhere in the menus. What passes for variation is the DX and GT reskin tier each car unlocks into as you climb the ranks, and unlocking that tier is purely a function of progression, with no player input into how a car looks or performs beyond it. If you're the type of player who wants to spend an evening making a car look and feel like yours, there's nothing here to do that with, so the roster and career are where Revenge asks you to spend your time.

---

### Career & Progression — 4/5

Burnout Revenge's World Tour career leans entirely on event variety to keep things moving. You cycle through Race, Road Rage, Burning Lap, Eliminator, Crash, and Traffic Attack events as you climb through ranks that top out at Insane and Fearless, and Traffic Attack turns the new checking mechanic into its own event type, tasking you with hitting a Crash Dollar target purely by ramming traffic. Unfortunately, there's no rival character or story thread tying any of it together, so winning an event matters mechanically without ever meaning much beyond the next rank. Players chasing full completion get an extra layer on top in the Perfect Revenge Rating chase, which demands an Awesome rating and a gold medal in every single event, so there's a real long-term goal here even without a narrative pulling you toward it.

---

### Multiplayer & Online Racing — 3/5

Multiplayer in Burnout Revenge is really a story of two different eras. At launch it supported online races for up to 6 players across five event types, including several Crash Mode variants built specifically for online groups, alongside 2-player split-screen you could play locally without needing any of that online infrastructure. Unfortunately, none of the online side is reachable anymore. EA shut the PS2 and Xbox servers down in November 2007, and the Xbox 360 servers followed for good in October 2017, so the 6-player races and online Crash variants that once defined the mode simply aren't an option today. What's left is the split-screen, and it holds up fine for what it is, running cleanly on original hardware or through Xbox 360 backward compatibility without needing any of the infrastructure that's now gone. If multiplayer is the reason you're picking this up, plan around a couch and a second controller, since there's no matchmaking queue left to join.

## Pros

- No online account, launcher, or internet connection required. Every single-player mode, including the full World Tour career, runs entirely offline on original hardware or through backward compatibility.
- The Xbox 360 version runs natively on Xbox One and Xbox Series X|S through backward compatibility, so you don't need to track down a PS2 or original Xbox to play it on modern hardware.
- Won Racing Game of the Year at the 2006 Interactive Achievement Awards, alongside several other industry awards from 2005.

## Cons

- Load times between races and menus are noticeably slow, and that hasn't improved on backward compatibility.
- There's no PC or Steam release and no modern digital re-release, so a physical disc or an existing library entitlement is the only way in.
- Chasing full completion, every vehicle plus every Perfect Revenge Rating, is a serious time investment tracked at 50 to 100+ hours by completion trackers.

---

## Who Is This Game For?

**Play this if** you want short, replayable arcade races built around over-the-top crash spectacle and don't need deep vehicle customization to enjoy building out a garage. **Play this if** you've got a second person on the couch, since the split-screen still holds up even though online doesn't. **Play this if** you like a career with plenty of format variety to chase, between circuit races, Road Rage, Traffic Attack, and Crash Mode, rather than one built around a story.

**Skip this if** you're specifically looking for the tuner-style customization Need for Speed built its identity on, since Revenge has none. **Skip this if** you want a real single-player challenge, since the AI is softer than Burnout 3's and there's no difficulty option to compensate. **Skip this if** you're hoping to race online, since the servers have been dead for years across every platform.

---

## Verdict

Burnout Revenge is one of the best crash-focused arcade racers you can still play today, and its Crash Mode and traffic checking mechanics hold up well twenty years on. The complete absence of car customization and a career with no real narrative stakes keep it from matching its own high points elsewhere, and online racing being permanently offline now limits it to a single-player and local co-op experience. If you're after couch multiplayer chaos or a well-built solo career, Revenge still delivers; if customization or ranked online racing matter to you, look elsewhere.

## FAQs

**Is Burnout Revenge cross platform?**
No, Burnout Revenge doesn't support cross-platform play. It released years before crossplay existed as a feature, and its PS2, Xbox, and Xbox 360 versions never connected to each other online even when the servers were active.

**What platforms is Burnout Revenge available on?**
Burnout Revenge is available on PlayStation 2, Xbox, and Xbox 360, all released between September 2005 and March 2006. The Xbox 360 version also runs on Xbox One and Xbox Series X|S through backward compatibility.

**How long is Burnout Revenge?**
The main World Tour career takes around 17 hours to finish. Add in extra events and you're looking at closer to 20 hours, while chasing full completion, including every vehicle and Perfect Revenge Rating, runs past 30 hours.

**Is Burnout Revenge hard?**
No, Burnout Revenge sits on the easier side for the genre, and there's no difficulty setting to change that. The AI is noticeably softer than Burnout 3: Takedown's, so if you want a stiffer challenge you'll need to chase Perfect Revenge Ratings instead of relying on the AI to push back.

**Does Burnout Revenge have multiplayer?**
Yes, Burnout Revenge has both local split-screen and online multiplayer built in. Online isn't playable anymore since the servers were permanently shut down, but local split-screen still works.

**Does Burnout Revenge have couch co-op or split screen?**
Yes, Burnout Revenge supports 2-player split-screen racing locally. It works on original hardware and through Xbox 360 backward compatibility today.

**Can you play Burnout Revenge with friends online?**
No, online play isn't possible anymore. EA shut down the PS2 and Xbox servers in November 2007, and the Xbox 360 servers were shut down for good in October 2017.

**Do I need to play the previous Burnout games first?**
No, Burnout Revenge doesn't require any prior entry to follow. There's no ongoing story to catch up on, so you can jump straight into World Tour without missing anything.

**Which Burnout game should I start with?**
Start with Burnout 3: Takedown if you want the series at its toughest and most demanding. Revenge is a reasonable starting point too if you'd rather ease in with softer AI and the added traffic checking mechanic.

**Does Burnout Revenge have DLC?**
Yes, the Xbox 360 version had free bonus vehicles available through Xbox Live Marketplace and retailer kiosks, including branded cars from Alienware, Dolby, and Spike TV. Given how old the game is, it's worth checking whether that Marketplace content is still listed before assuming it's available.

**Does Burnout Revenge have an easy mode?**
No, Burnout Revenge doesn't offer adjustable difficulty settings of any kind. The AI itself already sits on the forgiving side, which is the closest thing the game has to an easy mode.

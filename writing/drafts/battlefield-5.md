## Introduction

Battlefield V is the WWII-set entry in the Battlefield franchise that most players bring up when squad-based fortification comes up in conversation, the mechanic that let four-player squads dig in around an objective instead of just standing near it. Released in November 2018 across PC, PS4, and Xbox One, it runs matches up to 64 players across modes ranging from Conquest and Breakthrough to the multi-stage Grand Operations, with every map and mode added after launch arriving free through the Tides of War update cycle instead of behind a paywall.

Whether that fortification system and the rest of the package still hold up for a multiplayer shooter pushing toward a decade old is the real question, especially with a population that's had years to thin out and newer entries in the series competing for the same lobbies. If you're deciding whether Battlefield V is worth picking up now, on sale or otherwise, there's a fair amount to weigh between the campaign, the core multiplayer modes, and the battle royale spin-off that arrived later. The metric breakdown below covers what still holds up, where the population and platform limitations show, and which part of the package is actually worth your time.

## Metrics

### Gunplay — 4 / 5

Battlefield V's gunplay earns its reputation among the community, with a WWII arsenal that feels distinct from the modern-set shooters most players are used to. The Kar98k's bolt-action snap and the StG 44's controlled automatic fire handle nothing alike, and the suppression mechanic, which blurs the screen and increases sway for whoever's caught in sustained fire, forces real positioning decisions instead of just trading shots in the open. Unfortunately, DICE pushed through a time-to-kill change about a year into the game's life that bulked up player health and slowed kills down enough to spark a genuine community revolt, reverting it within weeks once the backlash hit. If you're judging the gunplay off reviews or footage from that specific window, it's worth knowing the version you'll actually play today is the one DICE walked back to.

---

### Weapon Variety & Customization — 4 / 5

The arsenal in Battlefield V splits cleanly by class and era, and the Specializations system lets you build into a weapon as you rank it up instead of just unlocking a flat damage increase. For instance, you can trade slower reload speed for reduced sway on a bolt-action rifle, or build an SMG toward faster aim-down-sights at the cost of recoil control, and both choices change how the weapon actually plays instead of just padding a stat sheet. Unfortunately, a sizeable chunk of those specializations sat behind Tides of War's grind-based unlock system at launch, so the variety that makes this metric work wasn't fully available to anyone who picked the game up in its first few months. Coming to the game years after launch with the unlock tree already earned gets you a more complete picture of the system than early adopters got.

---

### Squad Play & Class Design — 5 / 5

Squad Play & Class Design is where Battlefield V does its best work, and the fortification system is the reason. Any class can drop sandbags or razor wire with the build tool, but the Support class builds faster and can throw up offensive fortifications like machine gun nests, which turns a coordinated squad into something a solo player simply can't replicate. A Medic chaining revives and a Support resupplying ammo and repairing cover already give a squad an edge on their own, and a Recon spotting incoming armour on top of that is what turns four players into an actual unit instead of four guns pointed in the same direction. This is the system the genre still gets compared against when squad cohesion comes up, and it holds up because the roles carry real mechanical weight beyond what's printed on the class select screen.

---

### Maps — 4 / 5

Battlefield V's maps mostly do the job large-scale multiplayer needs them to, balancing vehicle lanes against infantry routes without either one taking over completely. Hamada, the largest map in the launch lineup, gives tanks room to manoeuvre across open desert while still leaving infantry enough cover to flank in close, and Narvik pairs a snowy mountain assault with enough verticality that positioning matters as much as what you're driving. Unfortunately, a handful of maps lean too hard on one dominant vehicle sightline, Twisted Steel in particular, where infantry crossing open ground without armour support of their own can feel like the map is fought entirely on the vehicles' terms. If you're an infantry-only player, it's worth knowing which maps in the rotation are going to ask more of you than others.

---

### Campaign Structure — 4 / 5

The War Stories campaign trades length for genuine variety between its four launch missions, and that trade mostly pays off. Tirailleur puts you in a Senegalese infantry unit fighting through occupied France, while Under No Flag drops you into a heist-style infiltration mission in North Africa instead. Nordlys swaps both of those for a snowbound survival story through Norwegian resistance territory, and none of the four launch missions repeat what came before it. Unfortunately, each War Story wraps up just as it's finding its footing, and DICE adding a fifth story, The Last Tiger, as free post-launch content months later is a pretty clear admission that the launch lineup needed more. If you're buying Battlefield V specifically for the campaign, treat it as a handful of strong short stories and size your expectations around that.

## Pros

- The fortification system gives squads a mechanical reason to stick together instead of just grouping up in the same spot
- Frostbite 3's destruction and weather effects make the WWII environments feel like they're actually reacting to the fight
- Every map and mode added after launch came free through Tides of War instead of behind a season pass
- Combined Arms gives solo players or small squads a PvE option against AI when a full 64-player lobby isn't what you're after
- Firestorm still holds up as a slower, squad-focused take on battle royale if you're burnt out on faster-paced alternatives

## Cons

- No crossplay between PC, PS4, and Xbox One splits an already aging population across three separate pools
- Firestorm's active player count has thinned out enough years on that queue times outside peak hours can run long
- The launch-era time-to-kill change and its reversal left a lasting dent in how some of the community talks about the game
- War Stories' four launch missions are short enough that the campaign alone won't fill a weekend

---

## Verdict

Battlefield V is one of the best large-scale multiplayer shooters you can play if squad-based fortification and real class identity are what you're looking for. The War Stories campaign adds some value once you're already in, and Firestorm is worth a look if you haven't tried it, but neither one is why you'd buy Battlefield V today, the core Conquest and Breakthrough experience with a full, coordinated squad is.

## FAQs

**Is Battlefield V cross platform?**
No, Battlefield V doesn't support crossplay between PC, PS4, and Xbox One. You can only matchmake with other players on the same platform you're playing on. Cross-generation play does work within the same platform family, so PS4 and PS5 players can match together, and the same goes for Xbox One and Xbox Series X|S.

**What platforms is Battlefield V available on?**
Battlefield V launched on PC, PS4, and Xbox One on November 20, 2018, with Deluxe Edition owners getting early access from November 15. There's no dedicated PS5 or Xbox Series X|S version, so you're playing it through backward compatibility on current-gen consoles.

**How long is Battlefield V?**
The War Stories campaign across all five missions takes most players somewhere between 5 and 8 hours to finish. That number climbs if you're chasing collectibles or playing on a higher difficulty. Multiplayer has no fixed length since it's built around ongoing matches instead of a story to complete.

**Is Battlefield V hard?**
Not in any punishing sense. The War Stories campaign has adjustable difficulty settings if you want more resistance, and multiplayer's difficulty comes down to the skill of whoever you're matched against, not the game itself. The fortification and class systems do take a few hours to actually understand if you want real value out of squad play.

**Does Battlefield V have multiplayer?**
Yes, multiplayer is the main draw, with modes like Conquest and Breakthrough, plus the multi-stage Grand Operations, supporting up to 64 players per match. Squads are capped at four players, and the class system is built around squads filling different roles instead of everyone running the same loadout.

**Can you play Battlefield V with friends online?**
Yes, you can squad up with up to three friends in standard multiplayer, or play Combined Arms, a separate co-op mode where a squad of up to four takes on AI-controlled objectives. Combined Arms can also be played solo if you don't have a full squad available.

**Do I need to play previous Battlefield games before this one?**
No, Battlefield V doesn't require any previous entries. The War Stories campaign is a set of standalone missions with new characters and settings each time, and multiplayer doesn't carry any story requirement at all.

**Does Battlefield V have DLC?**
No paid DLC. Every map, mode, and War Story added after launch came through the free Tides of War update cycle, which ran across six chapters plus a final Summer Update before content support ended in mid-2020.

**Is Battlefield V on Game Pass?**
Battlefield V is available through EA Play, which is included with Xbox Game Pass Ultimate and PC Game Pass. Subscription catalogues change over time, so it's worth double-checking current availability before you commit to it for that reason alone.

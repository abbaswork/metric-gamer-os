## Introduction

Doom Eternal is a first-person shooter developed by id Software and published by Bethesda Softworks, released in March 2020. It's the direct sequel to Doom (2016), following the Doom Slayer through a demonic invasion of Earth while expanding the combat system considerably from where its predecessor left off.

At its core, Doom Eternal is a resource management game disguised as a shooter. Your resources are designed to run low during combat, and the only way to keep them topped up is to stay aggressive. The result is a pace that rarely lets you settle, with every encounter functioning as a problem to be solved with the tools available to you at that moment.

## Metrics

### Gunplay — 5 / 5

Doom Eternal's gunplay is about as good as it gets in a first-person shooter. For example, every weapon has a distinct sound design that tells you exactly what it's doing to an enemy. The feedback from landing a shot with the Super Shotgun or the Ballista is immediately satisfying in a way that holds up well into the back half of the game. The gore system adds a physical weight to every kill that's hard to find elsewhere in the genre.

---

### Weapon Variety — 5 / 5

Doom Eternal gives every weapon in your arsenal a specific role, and the game is designed to teach you when to use each of them. The Ballista is built for fast-moving aerial targets. The Super Shotgun's Meat Hook mod doubles as a movement tool, pulling you across an arena whenever you need to close distance quickly. By the end of the campaign you'll have found a purpose for everything available to you, often switching between tools in the same fight.

---

### Level Design — 4 / 5

Doom Eternal's combat arenas give you room to work with, and the best levels use verticality to make positioning matter. Exultia and ARC Complex use multi-level spaces where knowing the layout is as valuable as knowing your loadout. Unfortunately, some of the later levels favour narrower corridors and more enclosed spaces, which makes them feel less considered than the game's stronger first half.

---

### Campaign Structure — 4 / 5

Doom Eternal has a strong sense of forward momentum across its opening two thirds. For example, each mission introduces either a new enemy type or a new mechanic that shifts what the combat demands from you. The game spaces these introductions well enough that you're never overwhelmed all at once. Unfortunately the final stretch steepens considerably, and players who haven't fully internalised the game's systems by that point are likely to find the last few missions a significant step up from everything that came before.

---

### Enemy Variety — 5 / 5

Doom Eternal's enemy roster is one of its strongest qualities, and the design philosophy is clear from the first time you encounter a Marauder. It requires specific timing and spacing that the game teaches you through earlier encounters. The Archvile changes the dynamic of any fight it appears in, forcing constant movement and removing cover as a viable option. By the final act you're regularly managing a full cast of enemies that each demand a different response at the same time, and the game is designed well enough that it feels like mastery rather than chaos.

## Pros

- The combat system rewards mastery in a way that builds into a genuine flow state
- Resource management under pressure keeps every encounter engaging from the first mission to the last
- Movement toolkit is deep enough that replaying difficult encounters feels different each time
- Difficulty settings range from genuinely accessible to punishing, covering a wide range of players
- Visual identity is strong enough that each environment feels distinct despite the pace

## Cons

- Platforming sections between arenas interrupt the pace without contributing much
- Story lore is delivered through collectible text entries rather than the action itself
- The difficulty spike in the final stretch catches some players off guard
- Switch version has noticeable performance compromises compared to other platforms
- Steeper learning curve than Doom (2016), and some players disengage before the system fully clicks

---

## Verdict

Doom Eternal is one of the most demanding first-person shooters you can play, and it pays back every hour you invest in learning its systems. The platforming sections and the way the lore is delivered are worth knowing about going in, but if you're prepared to meet the game on its terms, the combat makes everything else worth it.

## FAQs

**What platforms is Doom Eternal available on?**
Doom Eternal is available on PC via Steam and Battle.net, PS4, PS5, Xbox One, Xbox Series X/S, and Nintendo Switch. The PS5 and Xbox Series X/S versions arrived in June 2021 and run at a higher frame rate than the original releases.

**How long is Doom Eternal?**
The main campaign takes most players between 12 and 15 hours. Including challenge completions and optional exploration pushes that closer to 20 hours.

**Is Doom Eternal hard?**
Yes, it's noticeably more demanding than Doom (2016). Multiple difficulty settings are available, ranging from a more accessible entry point through to a permadeath mode for players who want the maximum challenge. The difficulty curve also steepens in the final third of the campaign, which is worth knowing before you get there.

**Do I need to play Doom (2016) before Doom Eternal?**
Not essential. The campaign works as a standalone experience. Playing Doom (2016) first adds context for the story, but it's not required to enjoy or understand Doom Eternal.

**Does Doom Eternal have DLC?**
Yes. The Ancient Gods, Part One and The Ancient Gods, Part Two both extend the campaign and are available as paid DLC. Neither is required to complete the main game, but both are worth playing if you enjoy the base experience.

**Is Doom Eternal on Game Pass?**
Doom Eternal has been available through Xbox Game Pass. Subscription availability can change, so check the current status on the Xbox or PC Game Pass catalogue before purchasing.

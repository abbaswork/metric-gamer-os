## Introduction

Gran Turismo 4 is the PS2 entry that most people in the sim racing community still bring up when the topic of single-player depth comes up. Released in late 2004 and early 2005, it arrived with 721 cars, more than 50 tracks, and a career structure that takes most players well past the 100-hour mark. It's the fourth main entry in the Gran Turismo series and the one that defined what a Polyphony Digital game could be at its ceiling.

Whether that depth still holds up in practice is a different question. GT4 has a reputation that precedes it, and reputations sometimes have a way of hiding where the cracks are. If you're coming to the game fresh, or coming back after a long gap, there's a lot to work through before you know whether GT4 is still worth your time. The metric breakdown below covers what still holds up today, what shows its age, and what kind of player is going to get the most out of the time investment.

## Metric Descriptions

### Car Roster — 5/5

GT4's car roster is the reason players still use this game as the benchmark for content value in a racing title. With over 700 cars from more than 80 manufacturers, the depth within individual manufacturers is what sets it apart from games that just inflate numbers to win comparisons. For example, the Honda lineup spans multiple generations of the NSX and Civic Type R variants across different years, which means if you're building a garage around a marque you care about in real life, you'll still be discovering options well into your time with the game. The roster reflects real automotive culture rather than just filling a grid, and for a car enthusiast rather than a casual racing game player, that's the version of the game you actually want to own.

### Track Variety — 5/5

The track list in GT4 is one of the most varied lineups you'll find in any racing game from this era. Real-world circuits like the Nürburgring Nordschleife and Circuit de la Sarthe sit alongside city-based street tracks including Côte d'Azur and Tokyo R246, while original fictional layouts like Autumn Ring and Trial Mountain fill out the rotation with circuits that hold up well over repeated laps. The selection spans tarmac, dirt, and snow, which keeps the session variety high as you're working through the career. If you're focused on circuit racing, you won't exhaust what's here in a normal playthrough.

### Handling Model — 4/5

GT4's handling model is more realistic than its predecessor in the ways that actually matter for someone driving with intention. Cars feel meaningfully different across classes, and the gap between a stock road car and a tuned race variant of the same model shows in how they respond to your inputs, not just in the numbers on the selection screen. For example, you can feel the difference in grip behaviour between tyre compounds on corner entry, and suspension tuning translates into real lap time differences rather than cosmetic adjustments. Unfortunately, the low-speed physics carry well-documented quirks that appear across most of the car roster, and the dirt and snow handling feels noticeably disconnected from what you'd expect on loose surfaces. That's worth keeping in mind if you're planning your career around the rally events. For tarmac racing on a wheel, the force feedback communication at speed is where the model does its best work.

### Split Screen Performance — 3/5

GT4's 2-player split screen works and delivers a genuine couch racing experience, but the performance trade-off is something you'll notice as soon as you boot it up. The game runs at a locked 60fps in single player, and that drops to 30fps when you add a second viewport. For a game where the tarmac racing feels as polished as it does in the main career, the step down is more noticeable than it would be in a title with less refined base performance. Unfortunately, this means the mode you'd use with a friend runs at exactly half the frame rate you're used to in solo play. Visual quality per screen also reduces alongside the frame rate drop. It's still worth using if split screen matters to you, but you'll know you're in a reduced mode from the first corner.

### AI Quality — 3/5

The AI in GT4 is functional in the sense that it doesn't actively derail your races, but it's not going to make you feel like you've earned a win in a way that sticks with you. Opponents drive predictable lines at difficulty-appropriate pace, and the scaling across league tiers does mean the faster events feel properly calibrated. For example, in the Professional and Endurance events, you're genuinely working to keep up if your car isn't built correctly. Unfortunately, the AI doesn't respond to where you are on track. It runs its own race regardless of your position, which means overtaking is a routing exercise rather than a wheel-to-wheel contest. Once you've identified where each opponent class brakes, that knowledge carries across the whole career. Set your expectations before you get in.

## Pros and Cons

**Pros**
- Photography Mode is a serious creative tool that places cars at real-world locations via Photo Travel, not a novelty feature bolted on as an afterthought
- B-Spec mode lets you assign an AI driver to manage long endurance grinds, which makes the multi-hour races far more practical without removing the strategy layer
- The license tests function as a genuine driving school, covering real techniques like threshold braking and trail braking through scenarios that demand clean execution rather than just speed
- The Used Car Dealership rotates its stock over time, so rare and vintage vehicles become available as you progress and give you a reason to keep checking in between sessions
- GT4 runs at 60fps across all conditions on PS2 hardware, which was exceptional for the era and makes it feel noticeably smoother than most of its contemporaries

**Cons**
- Online multiplayer was announced and cancelled before launch, leaving the game with no online component despite it being marketed during development
- The credits required for top-tier machinery in the late career become a grind, and the most efficient earning method often means replaying early races rather than progressing
- Engine audio across the roster is broadly generic and doesn't reflect the individual character of the cars the way the handling model does
- There's no way to sell cars, so prize vehicles from event wins stack up as duplicates in your garage with nothing to do with them
- There's no penalty for corner cutting or contact with AI opponents, which means sloppy or aggressive driving is never punished and removes some of the immersion from competitive events

## Who Is This Game For?

**Play this if** you want the deepest single-player racing career available on PS2. The A-Spec structure gives you well over 100 hours of content and the 721-car roster means you'll still be finding new options deep into your time with the game.

**Play this if** you're a car enthusiast who cares about manufacturer depth. Brands like Honda and Nissan each span multiple generations within the roster, so you can build a garage around a marque you actually care about rather than just filling slots.

**Skip this if** online multiplayer is a requirement. GT4's online mode was cancelled before launch and the game shipped with no online component at all.

**Skip this if** your focus is rally or dirt racing. The off-road physics are noticeably weaker than the tarmac model and the rally events don't reflect what the rest of the game does well.

---

## Verdict

Gran Turismo 4 is still one of the best PS2 racing games you can go back to, and the A-Spec career remains a legitimate reference point for what single-player racing structure can look like. The AI and the late-game credit grind show their age, but neither undermines what the handling model and track variety deliver when the game is running at its best. If you care about racing games as a genre and haven't spent time with this one, it's worth the investment.

## FAQs

**Is Gran Turismo 4 cross platform?**
No, Gran Turismo 4 isn't available across multiple platforms. It's a PlayStation 2 exclusive, and there's no crossplay since the game has no online multiplayer component at all.

**What platforms is Gran Turismo 4 available on?**
Gran Turismo 4 is available on PlayStation 2 only. It launched in Japan in December 2004, with North America and Europe following in early 2005. It hasn't been re-released on modern platforms.

**How long is Gran Turismo 4?**
The main story campaign runs around 77 hours. Adding the side events and special content pushes that to roughly 169 hours, and completionists have logged over 330 hours. It's one of the longest single-player campaigns in the genre.

**Is Gran Turismo 4 hard?**
The difficulty scales through the license system, so the early Beginner and Amateur events are accessible. The game becomes significantly more demanding as you work through the International B, International A, and S license tiers, and the Endurance races at the top of the career are the most demanding content in the game.

**Does Gran Turismo 4 have split screen?**
Yes, Gran Turismo 4 supports 2-player split screen in Arcade mode. It's not available in the main career, but it's a functional option if you want to race a friend on the same console.

**Do I need to play the previous Gran Turismo games first?**
No, Gran Turismo 4 is a self-contained experience. The career and car roster stand alone, and you don't need any knowledge of the earlier entries to get into it. If you've played other Gran Turismo titles, the physics conventions will feel familiar, but they're not required reading.

**Does Gran Turismo 4 have DLC?**
No, Gran Turismo 4 has no DLC. Everything in the game is included on the original disc.

**Can you play Gran Turismo 4 on PC?**
Gran Turismo 4 was never officially released on PC. It's widely played through the PCSX2 PlayStation 2 emulator, which runs it well on modern hardware and supports resolutions and frame rates beyond what the original PS2 hardware could output.
